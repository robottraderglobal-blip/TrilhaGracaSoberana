# Sinclair Ferguson: Pursuing Integrity (Pre-Conference)

FERGUSON: It is a tremendous joy to be here, 
and I feel very privileged, and since I haven't  

been the pastor of a church for twelve years 
now, I am thrilled to be able to speak to you.  

I also have a certain reluctance, because I 
have an honorific role as an old man, but I'm no  

longer in the trenches the same way you are in the 
trenches, and so, I do stand before you with some  

sense of inadequacy, but we have the Word of 
God, and not only do we have the Word of God,  

but Ligonier, or top people in Ligonier, whom I've 
never been able to discover who they really are,  

almost always give you a text in 
order to give you a beginning.

And the text they've given to me as 
a beginning, as you may already know,  

is from the 78th Psalm. And I want to read those 
verses, first of all, at the end of Psalm 78,  

and then they've asked me to speak on this really 
important subject of pursuing integrity in the  

ministry. And I think probably of recent 
generations, our own generation feels the  

pressure of that and the sadness of failure in 
it more than many other preceding generations.

And so, this is the Word at the end of Psalm 78:  

"The Lord chose David his servant 
and took him from the sheepfolds;  

from following the nursing ewes he brought 
him to shepherd Jacob his people, Israel his  

inheritance. With upright heart he shepherded 
them and guided them with his skillful hand."

And whoever, whatever mysterious person 
chooses these texts, this is really a  

beautiful text to have chosen. It might not 
be the first text that came to your mind,  

but it's particularly interesting because this 
is one of Asaph's psalms, and there are about  

ten psalms just at this point in the Psalter, as 
though they'd collected all of Wesley's hymns or  

Isaac Watts' hymns, or the Gettys' hymns, and 
put them together in a little Asaph section.

And since I think all of us know especially the 
73rd Psalm in which Asaph reveals to us something  

of his melancholic spirit, he reminds me, because 
he was the music director in David's congregation,  

that when you're looking for a music director, 
you need to find somebody who knows what the  

minor key is in the Christian life, and has a 
touch of melancholy about him, who knows what it  

is to lament as well as to rejoice, and who also 
has a sense of the whole history of the church.

Because this is what this psalm is about: he 
traces the history of the church from earlier  

generations right through to his own generation 
in the time of David, and he describes them in  

a very interesting way, both at the beginning 
of the psalm and then at the end of the psalm.  

You'll notice in verse 5, and then later on, 
towards the end of the psalm, in verse 71,  

he describes the people of God in a twofold way. 
They are described on the one hand as Jacob,  

and on the other hand as Israel. And you 
don't need to be a rocket scientist or a  

great theologian to understand what these two 
names for the same individual are communicating.  

They are communicating that although he became 
Israel, he never escaped from being Jacob. And  

as we know that it's true of his personal 
history, it's clear that Asaph is conscious  

it's also been true of the whole history of the 
redeemed people of God, from the Exodus onwards.

And it's a real reminder to us that just as 
the Lord called David to be the shepherd of  

Jacob-become-Israel without fully and 
finally being delivered from Jacob,  

the same is true for the pastors of God's people 
in the new covenant era. We are not dealing with  

those who are exclusively, from the tip of their 
heads to the tip of their toes, perfected saints,  

but those who have been born again, who have 
been given a new name in Jesus Christ, justified  

and adopted, and yet, as Luther so wonderfully 
taught the church at the time of the Reformation,  

although we are justified in Christ, we remain 
sinners in ourselves until that justification  

comes to its crowning glory in our final 
sanctification and also in our glorification.

And in a sense, this is the 101 lesson of pastoral 
ministry. As shepherds of the flock, we will begin  

to smell of the sheep, and at times our princely 
sheep who have been brought into God's kingdom,  

made kings with Christ, heirs of glory, still 
remain sinners, and our task is to pastor  

them from this world of sin into that world of 
glory. And if we are going to be able to do that  

with integrity and with honesty, we must pursue 
spiritual and moral integrity in our own lives.

And Asaph encapsulates that in verse 
72, doesn't he? "With upright heart,  

he shepherded them and guided them with his 
skillful hand." And he puts his finger there,  

on, as it were, the two essential 
requirements in our pastoral  

ministry. The first is the personal 
integrity in which we are faithful,  

and the second is the giftedness which makes 
us skillful in pastoring the people of God.

I suppose the New Testament version of 
this is the way in which the Lord Jesus,  

in Matthew 24:45, describes the good servant, 
or the good steward, in the house of God. What  

are his chief characteristics? First 
of all, that he is faithful—that is,  

that he has integrity before God. And then, 
secondly, that he is wise—that is to say,  

he is gifted in serving the people of God the 
food that they need. And our opening two addresses  

in our time together today focus on these two 
areas, on personal integrity and pastoral skill,  

on being faithful and also being wise. And 
our task in this hour—or this 31 minutes and  

45 seconds—is to focus on the first of these: 
what does it mean for us as under-shepherds in  

the flock of Jesus Christ to be faithful in our 
ministry and to exhibit real pastoral integrity?

And I want to try and answer that question 
by asking four questions, and we'll spend,  

I think, most of the time on the first, but let 
me at least tell you what the questions are. The  

first question is, why is pastoral integrity so 
important? The second, how is pastoral integrity  

threatened? The third—which is, I think, an 
important question—what is the key to pastoral  

integrity? And the fourth, where are we going 
to find the resources that we need to sustain  

pastoral integrity? So, this is a four-point 
exposition, which is really disguising a  

15-point message. And you will understand that 
at points I may just list what I want to say.

So, why is this integrity so important? If we 
grasp the importance of it, the weight of it,  

the graciousness of the burden of it, then I 
think it will help us to sustain integrity,  

because we realize it isn't just important for 
ourselves, but, as it were, we keep our lives,  

ourselves, our bodies under lest we ourselves 
be disqualified; but it's so important for the  

ministry that we share, the gifts that God 
has given to us, to help the people of God.

And I think there's a very simple answer to this 
question, why is integrity so important. I could  

put it like this: it's important because 
the servant is never above the master,  

and the servant needs to become like the 
master in order to represent the master.  

Our Lord Jesus Himself—basic principle in 
calling the apostles—found in the words,  

"Follow Me." And of course, those words, from one 
point of view, were to be interpreted literally:  

leave your boats, get up and 
follow Me. But within the context,  

certainly of the world in which Jesus lived, 
they mean much more than that, don't they?

"Follow Me" is not just a formal matter. 
"Follow Me," in Jesus' case, means,  

first of all, "Be with Me." He took them to be 
with Him in order that He might send them out.  

And this clearly is a fundamental element in 
our pastoral integrity. We cannot model the  

Lord Jesus to our people unless we are 
with the Lord Jesus in His presence. It  

means "Follow Me" in the sense of, "Be with 
Me." It means "Follow Me" in the sense of,  

"Watch Me." Watch me. That is to say, while you 
are called to be an under-overseer of the flock,  

all the while you are watching over the flock, you 
have an eye to the Lord Jesus Christ. You know,  

those of you—those of us who preach 
know what our congregations don't know,  

which is many things are simultaneously going 
on in our minds while we are preaching to them.  

We are preaching to them, and we notice Mrs. Jones 
has fallen asleep, or it might be, in your case,  

Mr. Kim, and two things are going on in our 
minds. So you don't need to be schizophrenic  

for your vision to have two persons in view: 
the persons of your congregation that you are  

overseeing, looking over and looking after, but 
simultaneously, as it were, as you look at them,  

you see beyond them to the Lord Jesus Christ, 
and your eyes are towards Him as the eyes of  

servants are looking to the hand of their master 
and maidservants to the hand of their mistress,  

we always have one eye on the Lord Jesus Christ. 
We live everything out in the world as those who  

live in the presence of Jesus, and only those who 
live in the presence of Jesus will, as it were,  

convey in their ministry to people the atmosphere 
of the presence of Jesus. And we are always  

looking to Him. And because we are always looking 
to Him, we are always seeking to imitate Him.

And that's built into "Follow Me." This was 
the culture of a first-century rabbi. His  

students not only literally followed 
Him, watched Him, listened to Him,  

but they imitated Him. And supremely 
the Lord Jesus teaches us to imitate  

Him—where? In John chapter 13: "Just as 
you have seen me lay aside my garments,  

take the servant's towel, wash your feet, so 
you also ought to wash one another's feet."

I think if anyone had asked the Apostle 
Paul, what is the heartbeat of your ministry,  

I think he might have said, "Well, have you 
not read 2 Corinthians 4, verse 5? "We do not  

preach ourselves, but Jesus Christ as 
Lord." And were I writing that verse,  

that's where the sentence would end. 
But it's not where Paul's sentence ends,  

is it? His sentence ends with, "and we are your 
bondslaves for Jesus' sake." And in many ways,  

that is the key to pastoral integrity. 
It's the key to not abusing your people.  

It's the key to not making the sheep food for your 
own ambition, that when you stand before them,  

when you live among them, you're constantly on 
your knees before them, because you believe that  

whatever giftedness you may have, it has 
been given to you not for your sake—not  

for your aggrandizement—but for the sake of their 
blessing and the glory of the Lord Jesus Christ.

And of course, from one point of view, that 
imitation—that imitation of the pastor—a  

very natural thing. It's like Paul says in 1 
Thessalonians 1, "You became imitators of us."  

That was certainly before he said to them, 
"Imitate me." It was something that happened  

naturally. How do I learn what it means to 
be a Christian if I'm in Thessalonica? Well,  

I look at Paul, I watch Paul. But then his letters 
are punctuated with an exhortation to do that:  

"As we follow Christ, you follow us; as we imitate 
the Lord Jesus, you should learn to imitate  

us." And while we are not apostles, that's a 
transferable principle in all ministry, isn't it?  

We should never have been ordained to the 
ministry of the Word of God if those who thus  

ordained us did not see that there was something 
sufficiently like Jesus in us to encourage others  

to be like Jesus too. And this is a very simple 
principle, but it's the reason, of course, the  

great emphasis in the New Testament instruction 
about elders and pastors—it's not largely on  

their gift. In a sense, it's assumed there will 
be a gift. It's largely on their character.

It's not accidental, incidentally, that one of the 
qualifications for an elder in the New Testament  

church is that people who are outsiders, who 
aren't Christians—what they think about him.  

I don't know that there are many sessions 
or groups of elders in churches who,  

when they are thinking about, "How do we 
add to our elder board?" ask the question,  

"Does anybody know what 
outsiders think about him?"

Now, they may hate him, but that actually 
may be an evidence of his Christlikeness;  

but the really important thing is character. 
And certainly in the world in which I've lived,  

I have sometimes felt, at least quietly, 
without ever saying publicly, that there are  

men who believe they are called to the ministry 
because they can argue you into the ground,  

who don't realize that that's a character 
flaw—that's a disability—that you are  

argumentative and you're able to dominate 
with speech, but not imitate Jesus in life.

And so, this is why integrity is so 
important, because it is inevitable,  

brothers—we may not like it this way—but it is 
inevitable that people think about the voice of  

God the way they hear us preach the Word of God, 
and because their access, as a group of people,  

to the character of Jesus and what feeds their own 
reading of Scripture in a well-structured church  

is always what happens from the podium, they 
will begin to think that Jesus is a bit like us.

And the evidence for that, at least in my 
experience, is where there is a strong ministry  

of the Word, it's really interesting to see how 
the attitudes, the aptitudes, the interests,  

the dispositions, even the eccentricities of the 
people, are actually mimicking the person who has  

ministered God's Word. And there are all kinds of 
ways—I haven't time to go into—where that appears,  

sometimes in the most superficial ways; but the 
place where, in a sense, either most gloriously  

or most tragically, it appears is in the 
reduplication of the character of the pastor in  

the people, because they associate the ministry of 
the Word with the character of the Lord, and they  

know they should seek to imitate Him. And that's a 
very obvious reason why integrity is so important.

There's a second reason—I'll deal with it much 
more briefly—and that is because the ministry of  

the Word requires it. The really significant thing 
about the quality of our ministry of the Word,  

brothers, is not the size of our congregation or 
the importance of the place where we minister.  

What will last for eternity is not 
going to be geographical location,  

nor size of congregation, but what we put into the  

people of God in our ministry of the 
Word—both in public and in private.

And if I read the New Testament aright—not least 
the pastoral epistles and also what the Apostle  

Paul has to say about his fellow workers in 
various places—what determines that, insofar as  

it is determined in the Lord's providence by the 
human instrument, is the character of the pastor.  

Sometimes, I've noticed young seminary graduates 
don't get that. They go to a church where Pastor  

So-and-So was there for forty years. He read very 
few really great books. He had never read Greg  

Beale on the book of Revelation. He hadn't even 
read William Hendriksen on the book of Revelation.  

He comes from another generation, and this young 
man has had a really great theological education.  

He can even spell the word "premillenarian" 
properly. And he assumes, because of that,  

that he is God's gift to this congregation. 
And he doesn't know that the Scripture teaches  

us that the ultimate aim of our charge—1 
Timothy 1, verse 5—is that we love them.  

And he assumes that his gifts equate to 
thirty years of the former pastor's love,  

and he doesn't understand why it is that the 
people are not immediately overjoyed with his  

ministry, and think—as used to be said in my 
earlier days in Scotland—he is the greatest  

thing since sliced bread was invented. And 
that's why pastoral integrity is so important.

Over the years, I have come to think that, at 
least in one respect, the Greek philosopher  

Aristotle was right in his work on communication 
and rhetoric, when he argues that there are  

three characteristics of rhetoric that has 
integrity. The first is logos—that is to say,  

the clear exposition of the truth when we 
think about it in terms of ministry. The  

third is pathos (or pathos)—that is to say, that 
the truth is communicated in an affectional and  

emotional atmosphere that's married to the 
truth, because truth communicates to people,  

not simply by propositions. So, there is an 
atmosphere to the different genre of Scripture,  

and there is an atmosphere to our communication 
of the Word of God that should be appropriate to  

the truth that is being communicated. And in the 
middle, you remember Aristotle says there needs to  

be ethos—ethos—that is to say, true communication 
needs to leave the impress on the hearers  

of the integrity of the character of the speaker 
with the message the speaker is communicating.

And as you grow in discernment, it's usually not 
too difficult to notice where that is lacking—that  

there is a kind of pretense in the rhetoric, 
because it doesn't go through and through with the  

life of the individual. When I was a small boy, 
if you went away on holiday, your friends expected  

you would bring back what we called "rock," 
a stick of rock candy—hard candy that would  

break your teeth. And each holiday place would 
have its own candy with the name of the place,  

it seemed, embedded at the end of the rock. 
And if you bought a cheap version of this and  

broke it in two, the name had disappeared. 
If you had bought the expensive version,  

no matter where you broke up the rock, the name 
was still there; it was embedded right through.  

And I've sometimes thought that's 
a kind of parable of preachers,  

isn't it? There is preaching that 
sounds in its rhetoric magnificent,  

but the more discerning you are, the more likely 
you are to notice there seems to be a difference  

between the rhetoric and the pathos, and an even 
greater difference between the rhetoric—the logos,  

which may be true—and the character of the 
individual. He's got hold of the truth,  

but over the months and years, it becomes clear 
that the truth has not really got hold of him.

And if we are to exercise pastoral 
integrity and are called to be those  

who model the life of the Christian 
believer before the congregation,  

I've sometimes said, you know, when we are 
choosing elders, what we want is men about whom,  

if an outsider who isn't a Christian comes among 
us and says, "Well, what does it mean to live the  

Christian life?" we ought to be able to say, 
"Keep your eye on him for a couple of days,  

and then you come and tell me what 
it means to live the Christian life."

And since we are the under-shepherds and 
pastors of God's people, that kind of  

integrity is absolutely of the quintessence 
of our ministry. It is that important,  

and not least when men are coming forward as 
candidates for the ministry, we ought surely  

to see a spark of this in their lives and be 
able to say to them, as Paul said to Timothy,  

"Now, as you go on, make sure," you remember 
how he says it—these words can haunt you,  

even paralyze you—"make sure everyone sees your 
progress." That'd be a testing question in our  

congregations, wouldn't it? Somebody did a survey, 
and the question was, "Have you noticed—have you  

noticed—Pastor Ferguson's progress since he came 
here?" Because that's what we are summoning our  

people to, and it is unavoidable that in our 
ministry to them, we become models of it. 
 
 

Well, that's why it's so important. How then is 
it threatened? And clearly, there's an answer to  

this: it's threatened the same way integrity 
is threatened in every believer. You know,  

we are not super-humans. We may sometimes have 
difficulties in the congregation that make  

us feel as though we've been treated 
as sub-humans, but we are believers,  

and we are afflicted and prosecuted by Satan 
the same way every other believer is. And yet,  

at the same time, I think it's clear in the New 
Testament there is a consciousness that because  

of our role—our office, I don't like the word, 
but that's what we usually call it—our office as  

ministers of the gospel, we become objects 
of special attack because of what we come  

to mean to our people. It is one of the most 
amazing blessings of being a pastor, isn't it,  

however difficult it may be in the trenches: 
the affection of the people of God for us,  

the importance they attach to us, because we 
are the ones who feed them from the Word of God,  

is phenomenal, and it can be glorious. But that's 
exactly why Satan would seek to threaten it.

I've often thought, reading what James says 
about temptation and sin in James chapter 1,  

if he had King David in mind, when "sin, when 
it's fully grown, eventually leads to death."  

And you remember the death of the child. And, 
you know, Asaph has such wonderful things to say  

about David, but Asaph must also have known that 
there was an asterisk at David's name and that  

he had failed, and his failure was not simply 
a private matter. Because he was the shepherd  

of God's people, and the whole story of God's 
people from that point onwards is the story how  

that asterisk of his failure works out. And he's 
saying to us here, in a kind of code language,  

"Yes, David was the shepherd, but precisely 
because David was the shepherd, in subtle ways  

he apparently did not recognize his integrity 
was specifically focused by the evil one."

And how? Well, the same way—judging by observation 
and by temptation—the same way the integrity of  

pastors is attacked by Satan with the three most 
basic tools: one is sexual, another is financial,  

and another is powerful. And they operate in 
different ways. And although it's true that the  

seed of every known sin is present in each of our 
hearts, there is no doubt that we are differently  

wired and therefore more easily prey to the 
devil's attack to bring us down, to seek to  

destroy the fellowship of God's people, to divide 
God's people. I wonder if you've noticed this:  

that when a pastor falls, it's almost the easiest 
way to divide the congregation. There are going  

to be all kinds of opinions on what happened, all 
kinds of blaming. And so often it's true in the  

Christian church, nobody stands up and says, "Is 
it possible that an enemy has done this?" And the  

Lord Jesus teaches us that's a really important 
question for us to ask, "Has an enemy done this?"

And so, we're counseled again, especially by 
the Apostle Paul, to be watchful in these areas,  

because there's always the danger of pride and 
self-sufficiency: "It happened to him, but it  

could never happen to me." That's why Paul says, 
"Brothers, not a novice, in case he falls into the  

danger of pride and self-sufficiency." And the 
ministry, in a way that is different from many  

other callings—indeed, most other callings—carries 
with it, because of our role in people's lives,  

because of the platform that we are given, 
carries a great temptation to pride. I remember  

one of the great forefathers of the Reformed 
movement in the United Kingdom, who taught  

in a seminary in London, saying that one of the 
great dangers in the Christian ministry is pride.

And maybe it's just because I'm a Presbyterian. 
Maybe it doesn't happen in Anglicanism, Methodism,  

Independency. But there's pride all over 
the place. It's a huge temptation—people  

grasping for position, people stretching for 
what someone else has. And it exposes us to  

temptation. It weakens us. Or with it, 
then goes the pastoral mask, doesn't it?  

One of the things that Paul loves 
about Timothy is that his faith is  

anupokritos—it's without hypocrisy. It's 
without a mask; that he's the real deal;  

that he may have struggles, but he is the real 
deal. And people know that he is the real deal,  

and that his patterns of interest—which, 
incidentally, to anyone who examines it,  

will be present. If you have a Twitter account 
or an X account, or whatever account, whatever  

social media you have, somebody with discernment 
is going to be able to look through that and  

conclude what you are really interested in. And 
so, we are very much exposed in different ways.

So, what in the third place is the 
key to pastoral integrity? Well,  

let me give you a passage and a few sentences 
on the passage, and you can turn to the passage  

again and again and again and again at your 
leisure. So, what is the passage? Well,  

the passage is Proverbs 4, verses 20 to 
27. And let me just walk you through it. It  

begins with the words "My son." Right—early 
chapters of Proverbs: "My son." Remember how  

Hebrews 12 transposes that into the mouth of 
God? "Have you forgotten the exhortation (from  

Proverbs) in which He (God) addresses you as 
His sons?" So, this is the Father, as it were,  

speaking the Word of God to the son. So, let 
us transpose that into the Lord speaking the  

Word of God to pastors. What does He say to us? 
Well, He says seven things—I think seven things;  

or, if there are more, it's kind of nice to 
think that there are seven basic principles.

Verses 20 to 21: you will be safeguarded so long 
as you keep giving your attention to the Father's  

voice in His Word. That's a guarantee—His Word 
will keep you and give you integrity. Principle  

number two is in verse 22: the Word of the Father 
is life to those who find the truth of His sayings  

and healing to all their flesh. What does that 
mean? Well, if you are to give attention to the  

Father's voice and Word, you are to fix your faith 
on the Father's promise. He has promised, and He  

will do it. Three—verse 23: you are to keep your 
heart with all vigilance. Don't trust it. Watch  

it. Guard it. Keep it. Fourth—verse 24: you're 
to guard your lips, because they are the key to  

the heart. You know, the verb that's used here—at 
least once in the New American Standard Bible—is  

translated "behead." That's interesting, isn't it? 
Not just guard your lips, but behead anything that  

is coming from your lips that is an indication 
of what is actually happening in your heart.

You know, in my world, so many young men have 
thought that they are God's gift to the ministry  

because they find it so easy to speak. And that's 
the problem, isn't it? They don't know anything  

about beheading their lips, and we're being 
called here to that kind of self-discipline.  

And then in verse 25, so important to us, "Let 
your eyes look directly forward and your gaze  

be straight before you." What does that mean? 
That means what you need to discipline as much  

as anything else is your eyes. When you look at 
the great moments of temptation in Scripture,  

the eyes are always there, aren't they? Eve 
at the tree; David on the roof of his house;  

Jesus in the wilderness: "Do You see all these 
kingdoms of the world? You have come here to  

win the kingdoms. See them all, Jesus." 
What do I do with my eyes? I can't let  

them operate simply in terms of what attracts 
me. I need to keep them looking straight ahead.

Sixthly: consider where the pathway that 
you are tempted to follow is going to lead  

you. "Ponder the path of your feet; 
then all your ways will be sure."  

Most of us of a certain age in pastoral ministry 
have probably had at least one man sitting in  

our office with his head in his hands saying, "I 
don't know what came over me." And the answer to  

his question is precisely what is being taught 
us here: "You didn't ponder where the path was  

leading." And then, seventhly, you need to learn 
to be committed to the undiverted life in verse  

27: "Don't swerve to the right or to the left." 
There are always going to be people to the left  

of you and always going to be people to the 
right of you. And this father is saying to  

his son—and our Father is saying to us—be an 
undiverted pastor, and be it with grace. 
 
 

And then, where are we going 
to find the resources? Well,  

I have five points and a second each. The call of 
God in our lives—doesn't that sustain us? Second,  

the Word of God in our hands—more 
than anyone else in the congregation,  

we have this privilege. The people of God 
as our family—that's a good motivation,  

isn't it, for holiness? "I love these people 
so much I would never want to let them down."

I once chose my friends over my family when 
I was a little boy, and ever since—the rest  

of my family are gone long ago, almost fifty 
years ago—I've regretted it. I've lived with  

that regret. It's not paralyzed me, but it 
has wounded me that I chose my friends over  

my family when I was a boy of nine, and I've 
never forgotten the failure. And the same is  

true of the ministry. It doesn't just go away. It 
lives with you forever if you betray the family.  

And then our brothers in the ministry—what 
a help it is to have brothers who are in  

the ministry. And most of all, 
the Son of God who is in heaven,  

who has promised that He will never stop 
interceding for us. So, however daunted we  

may be by the challenge, we have more reasons 
to be encouraged than we have to be daunted.

So let us—let us be willing, even just now, to 
say, "Lord, make me as holy as it is possible
