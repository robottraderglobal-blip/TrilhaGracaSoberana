# John Calvin – Reformed Pastor: The Reformed Pastor with Ian Hamilton

HAMILTON: Welcome to our sixth lecture about the  

Reformed pastor. To call John Calvin a 
pastor is a little like saying Michael  

Jordan played basketball. Calvin’s 
motto dominated his whole life. 

Cor meum tibi offero, Domine, prompte et sincere.
“My heart I give to you, oh Lord, promptly and  

sincerely.” And Calvin lived that out. 
He was a far from perfect pastor. But he  

sought before God to be a faithful pastor. 
And in that respect, he is something of a  

model pastor for Reformed pastors to emulate.
In his commentary on Acts 20, verse 20, where Luke  

tells us Paul taught publicly and from house to 
house, Calvin wrote, “For Christ has not appointed  

pastors on this condition, that they may only 
teach the church in general in the open pulpit,  

but that they may take charge of every particular 
sheep, that they may bring back to the sheep fold  

those who wander and go astray, that they may 
strengthen those who are discouraged and weak,  

that they may cure the sick, that they 
may lift up and set on foot the feeble,  

for common doctrine will oftentimes wax cold 
unless it is supported with private admonitions.” 

If you wonder where Richard Baxter derived his 
conviction that pastors should visit from house  

to house, you need look no further than here. But 
where did Calvin derive that conviction from? He  

derived it from the example of the apostle Paul.
And it’s not hard to hear in those words the echo  

of our Savior’s words in John 10, verse 3, 
“The shepherd calls his own sheep by name,  

and leads them out.” And Calvin was deeply 
concerned to know by name and need all his flock. 

I’d like to focus on four of the main features 
of Calvin’s pastoral ministry. Number one,  

Calvin’s pastoral ministry reflected his 
conviction that love to Christ is the  

animating pulse beat of all truly pastoral 
ministry. Love to Christ is the animating  

pulse beat of all truly pastoral ministry.
In his commentary on Jesus’ words to Peter,  

“Do you love me? Feed my sheep,” 
John 21:15, Calvin wrote these words: 

“By these words, Christ means that no man can 
faithfully serve the church and employ himself  

in feeding the flock if he do not look higher 
than to men. No man, therefore, will steadily  

persevere in the discharge of this office 
unless,” unless what? I wonder how you might  

complete that sentence. “No man will steadily 
persevere in the discharge of this office—the  

office of the pastoral ministry—unless the love of 
Christ shall reign in his heart in such a manner  

that forgetful of himself, and devoting himself 
entirely to Christ, he overcomes every obstacle.” 

This, for Calvin, was the one indispensable 
quality needed to be a pastor: love for Christ.  

Later on in that same commentary in John 21, 
verse 15, Calvin writes, “Those who are called  

to govern the church ought therefore to remember 
that if they are desirous to discharge their  

office properly and faithfully, they must begin 
with the love of Christ.” If this is absent,  

and how right Calvin was, everything is absent.
The motive that animates men as pastors  

is foundation to their usefulness. Three 
times Jesus said, “Simon, do you love me?” 

I’m not sure many search committees have the 
courage, or even the wit, to ask potential  

candidates, “Now tell me, having told me that 
you can faithfully ex animo simpliciter subscribe  

the Westminster Standards, tell me, do you 
love Christ? Does the love of Christ reign  

in any measure in your heart? Is it your great 
desire to love him better, to cherish him more?”  

That for Calvin was the one thing needful, 
that men who pastor love the Savior. 

Secondly, Calvin’s pastoral ministry reflected 
the servant’s spirit of the church’s chief Pastor.  

Commenting on second Corinthians 4, verse 5, 
where Paul writes, “We preach not ourselves,  

but Jesus Christ as Lord, and ourselves as 
your savants for Jesus’ sake,” Calvin wrote,  

“All pastors are nothing more than the 
servants of believers. And they cannot  

serve Christ without serving 
his church at the same time.”  

Hence, it is part of a good pastor not merely to 
keep aloof from all desire of domineering, but to  

regard it as the highest pitch of honor at which 
he aspires, that he may serve the people of God.” 

Calvin didn’t just write about that. He actually 
lived that. When the people of Geneva wrote to  

Calvin two and a half years or so after they had 
dismissed him summarily, they pleaded with him to  

return. Initially, when Calvin heard that this was 
a possibility, he wrote to a friend, Pierre Viret. 

And let me paraphrase what Calvin wrote. He said, 
“Over my dead body will I go back to that place  

where I died countless deaths.” And yet, when 
the appeal came, Calvin saw he had no option.  

He was not his own. He’d been bought with a 
price. He was a servant of the church of God. 

There was only one decision 
that he could take. And he  

responded to the letter. He returned to Geneva.
And the first day after his return, he simply  

picked up on the next verse after where he had 
preached prior to his dismissal in 1538. That’s  

a servant spirit. Not saying, “I told you so. I 
knew you’d come crawling. I knew that God would  

waken you up to your folly.” He simply responded, 
and said, “Your will, not my will, be done.” 

Thirdly, Calvin the pastor never wearied 
of telling his congregation to get out of  

themselves and into Christ. In Book Three of the 
Institutes, Chapter 4, Section 3, he wrote these  

words. “We have taught that the sinner does 
not dwell upon his own compunction or tears,  

but fixes both eyes upon the Lord’s mercy alone.” 
He is always pointing people and pleading with  

people to look away from themselves to Christ.
Samuel Rutherford, 100 years or so on from Calvin,  

wrote in one of his letters, “For every one 
look you take to yourself, take 10 looks at  

Christ.” What is there to find comfort looking 
into yourself? You say, “Well I’m looking to  

see whether my faith is growing.” Well, it’s good 
to have a growing faith. But some days our faith  

advances, and some days it regresses. Where 
does our comfort lie? Our comfort lies wholly  

outside of ourselves in our great Savior, Jesus 
Christ, who loved us and gave himself for us,  

who is the Lord, our righteousness.
In him, our standing before God is  

eternally secure. And one of the great 
designs of Satan is to turn us into ourselves.  

Martin Luther defined, or described sin as 
incurvatus in se, “that which turns you in  

upon yourself.” And the devil’s always looking to 
turn us in upon ourselves, sometimes to flatter  

us. “Aren’t you doing well? Aren’t you growing in 
grace?” “Oh, yes. Thank you. I think I might be!” 

And other times he says, “Well, look at 
you. Do you think God would have anything  

to do with the likes of you?” And you 
think, “Oh well, maybe not. Maybe not.” 

All our hope lies outside of ourselves. And Calvin 
never wearied of telling his congregation to get  

out of themselves and into Christ. And he carried 
that through. Not just in his regular day-by-day,  

week-by-week ministry in Geneva. He carried 
it through in the pastoral council he gave  

to Christian believers on death row.
In the 1540s and 50s, to be a gospel  

missionary in France was literally to sign your 
death warrant. Scores of young men who were  

sent out by the church in Geneva to France 
were burned at the stake. In October 1555,  

Calvin wrote to the five prisoners at Chambéry 
in Southeast France. I’ve been in the town. 

They’re on death row there awaiting death 
for simply being evangelicals, because in  

those days, an evangelical was a Bible-believing, 
Christ-loving, word-of-God-trusting believer. And  

Calvin wrote to them, “If you feel in yourselves 
too much infirmity,” so here are these five men on  

death row. And you could imagine—maybe you can’t 
imagine, maybe no one could imagine—what it must  

have been like for these men looking at themselves 
and their humanity, and trembling perhaps,  

at their fear that they might give way and recant.
Calvin wrote, “If you feel in yourselves too  

much infirmity, have recourse to Him 
who has every virtue in his hand.”  

Hear what he’s saying? “You lack courage? Go to 
Him who is courage itself. You lack steadfastness,  

go to Him who is steadfastness incarnate.”
In an earlier letter to them on September 5,  

1555, Calvin wrote, “The main point is to 
collect all your thoughts in order to repose  

in His paternal goodness.” So here are these men. 
They’re on death row for no other reason than that  

they are preaching that salvation is by grace 
alone, through faith alone, in Christ alone,  

to the glory of God alone, which of course was 
challenging the very foundations of Romanism. 

And Calvin is saying to them, “Here’s what 
your great need is: to repose in the paternal  

goodness of God, that he is your Father.”
I think it was J. I. Packer who said that  

“Father” is the Christian name for God. Well, 
that’s wonderful. And we need to remember that  

the Lord Jesus Christ, again and again and 
again and again, counseled his disciples,  

encouraged his disciples, even commanded 
his disciples, to call upon their Father. 

There are more references to God as “Father” 
in the sixth chapter of Matthew’s gospel than  

there are in the whole of the Old Testament. 
God is called Father in the Old Testament,  

but the revelation of his glorious Fatherhood 
comes in the new covenant. Calvin is saying here,  

“Get our of yourselves and into Christ.”
That’s where all our comforts lie, you know. There  

dwells no good thing in us. Even as Christians, 
what do we have that we did not first receive?  

Are we ever going to come to the Lord 
and say, “Lord, Look what I’ve done.” 

And the Lord would simply say, and who 
gave you the grace, the help, the strength,  

and the wit to do it? “Not to us, oh, Lord, 
not to us, but to your name be the glory.”  

You see, Calvin’s concern here is, yes, 
that these believers might be comforted,  

and encouraged, and strengthened and helped, 
but that God might be honored and glorified. 

And this should be a burden of all pastoral 
ministry, to point our people away from themselves  

to the Lord Jesus Christ. And the Reformed 
pastor has the weightiest obligation to do that,  

because we say we believe that it’s all of grace. 
It’s all of grace. Within us, there dwells no good  

thing. We make no contribution to our salvation 
except the sin that we need saving from. So he  

never wearied of telling his congregation 
to get out of themselves and into Christ. 

And then there is this fourth thing. It is, 
I think, perhaps the most important. And yet,  

in a sense it’s the most intangible. It 
pervades everything Calvin was and did. 

Calvin’s pastoral ministry as his whole 
life, was indelibly shaped and impregnated  

by the overflow of his union with the Lord 
Jesus Christ. Union with Christ was both the  

context and the inner dynamic of Calvin’s 
pastoral ministry. Calvin understood that  

the Lord Jesus Christ is the church’s true 
and chief Pastor. And what marked his life?  

What was the great defining feature of his life? 
It was a life marked by death and resurrection. 

“Unless a corn of wheat fall into the ground 
and die, it abides alone; but if it dies,  

it bears much fruit,” John 12:23 to 26.
Pastoral ministry for Calvin exercised  

in union with Jesus Christ, but union with 
Christ, both in his humiliation and in his  

exaltation—the suffering and the glory. “To 
follow the pattern of Christ so in ministry  

in which the cross came before the crown, one 
must begin,” said Calvin, “with the sufferings  

of the ministry.” And in a sense his thinking is 
almost circular, because actually the reality is  

that we begin not with the sufferings of the 
ministry, but with the grace of the gospel.  

But the grace of the gospel introduces us to 
union with Christ, and the sufferings of Christ. 

So pastoral ministry is not a matter of life and 
death, but a matter of death and life. And this  

is what Paul highlights in 2 Corinthians 
4. We have this treasure, the treasure of  

the gospel in jars of clay, to show that the 
surpassing power belongs to God and not to us.  

We are afflicted in every way, but not crushed; 
perplexed, but not driven to despair; persecuted,  

but not forsaken; struck down, but not destroyed; 
always carrying in the body the death of Jesus so  

that the life of Jesus may also be manifested in 
our bodies. For we who live are always being given  

over to death for Jesus’ sake so that the life of 
Jesus also may be manifested in our mortal flesh.” 

“So,” here’s the conclusion says Paul, “death 
is at work in us, but life in you.” Just as life  

came out of the suffering of Christuniquely of 
course—“he died, the just for the unjust to bring  

us to God.” But that principle, that pattern, 
is true for all Christians, and for Christian  

pastors. And another question that search 
committees could or should ask a prospective  

candidate is, not only, “Do you love Christ,” 
“Are you ready to die for the people of Christ?” 

This is the foundational principle of spiritual 
fruitfulness, And Calvin understood that.  

Union with Christ lay at the heart of Calvin’s 
theology, his ministry, and his life. It was this  

willingness to embrace death that brought him back 
to Geneva in 1541. He wrote to a friend, “There is  

no place under heaven of which I have a greater 
dread; albeit, I would not flinch from doing my  

utmost to help that Church in her wretchedness.”
Calvin actually was quite a poor man. He never  

had much money. Life at times was hard, but 
he embraced the cost, because what else was  

a servant of Christ to do? He didn’t think he was 
being virtuous. He didn’t think he was going the  

extra mile. He was simply taking seriously 
the Lord Jesus’ words in Luke 9, verse 23,  

“Unless you take up your cross daily and 
follow me, you cannot be my disciple.” 

And the Lord Jesus didn’t mean, “You know, life 
is full of little difficulties.” We call that,  

you know, “She’s got her cross to bear.” 
That’s not what Jesus meant. The cross  

was an instrument of execution. And Jesus 
was saying, “Unless you’re ready to die as  

a follower of mine, you cannot be my disciple.”
Maybe someone will ask you in these coming days,  

“What do I need to know if I’m to become a 
Christian?” You’ll need to know that you must  

be ready to die. And for some of our Christian 
brothers and sisters in Iraq and Syria today,  

facing the vile wickedness of ISIS, 
know that reality face-to-face.  

Some of our forebears in your land, and certainly 
in mine, suffered for the sake of the cause of  

Christ and serving the people of Christ.
Union with Christ means that the Reformed  

pastor must die to himself in all its hideous 
forms, self-indulgence, self-aggrandizement,  

self-love, self-will. It’s very easy when you’re 
a pastor to drift into thinking, “My people think  

I’m something,” and actually believe what they’re 
saying. And that’s the death of a ministry. 

And Satan uses that. But we are called to 
exhibit purely and weakly to the people of God,  

the principle of the Christian life: death brings 
forth life. The Reformed pastor must die to pride,  

to financial gain, to recognition, and 
to approval. God may give him financial  

gain. God may give him recognition. God 
may give him approval. But that’s for  

the Lord to do, not for the man to desire.
Such is the Reformed pastor. Jesus said, “I  

am among you as one who serves.” May God give us 
pastors of like heart and of like mind. Thank you.
