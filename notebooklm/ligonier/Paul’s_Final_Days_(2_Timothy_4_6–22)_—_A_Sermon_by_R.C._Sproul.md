# Paul’s Final Days (2 Timothy 4:6–22) — A Sermon by R.C. Sproul

SPROUL: Last week we ended our study of the Book 
of Acts being suspended by Luke’s not giving us  

the final resolution to the life of the apostle 
Paul, and today I promised for you an epilogue  

to sort of fill in the gap between the end of 
the Book of Acts and the end of the life of the  

apostle. So with that in mind I’m going to turn 
this morning to Paul’s last letter that he wrote,  

his Second Epistle to Timothy, to the last chapter 
of that book, in chapter 4 beginning in verse 6. 

For I am already being poured out as 
a drink offering, and the time of my  

departure is at hand. I fought the good fight, 
I finished the race, I have kept the faith;  

and finally there is laid up for me the crown 
of righteousness, which the Lord, the Righteous  

Judge, will give to me on that day, and not to 
me only but to all who have love his appearing. 

Be diligent to come to me quickly; for Demas 
has forsaken me, having loved this present  

world and has departed for Thessalonica; 
Cres'cens for Galatia; Titus for Dalmatia;  

and only Luke is with me. Get Mark and bring him 
with you; for he is useful to me for ministry. And  

Tych'icus I’ve sent to Ephesus. Bring the cloak 
that I left with Carpus at Trōas when you come,  

and the books, especially the parchments. 
Alexander, the coppersmith, did me much harm;  

may the Lord repay him according to 
his works. You also must beware of him;  

for he has greatly resisted our words. In my 
first defense no one stood with me, but all  

forsook me. May it not be charged against them. 
But the Lord stood with me and strengthened me,  

so that the message might be preached fully 
through me, and that all the Gentiles might  

hear. And also I was delivered out of the mouth 
of the lion. And the Lord will deliver me from  

every evil work and preserve me for his heavenly 
kingdom. To him be glory forever and ever. Amen. 

Greet Prisca and Aquil'a, the household 
of Onesiph'orus. Erastus stayed in  

Corinth; but Troph'imus I have left 
in Mīlē'tus sick. Do your utmost  

to come before winter. Eûbū'lus greets you as well 
as Pudens, Linus, Claudia and all the brethren. 

The Lord Jesus Christ, be with your 
spirit. Grace be with you. Amen. 

He who has ears to hear the 
Word of God, let them hear. 

We thank you, O God, for these final words of 
your anointed one and the way that you have  

used them beyond that hour of his imminent 
demise but have used these words across  

the ages to give comfort, encouragement and 
inspiration to all of your people. May that  

be so again this morning. For 
we ask it in Jesus’ name. Amen.

First time I went to Rome I went with a group that 
I was leading in a tour, and we had professional  

tour guides of the city, and they asked me in 
advanced what itinerary we would like to follow  

when we got to that city, and they suggested the 
Coloseum and the ruins of the various temples and  

the Roman Forum and the Vatican and St. Peter’s 
Basilica and all of those famous sites of Rome.  

And I said, “Well, the first place I want to 
see, the most important place I want to go is  

to the Lateran Church. And that shocked the agent 
because he said, “Why would you go to the Lateran  

Church. It’s where the Holy See used to be, but 
the Lateran Church was replaced by St. Peter’s as  

the basilica for the reigning Pontiff.” I said, 
“I want to go to the Lateran Church because that’s  

where the sacred stairs are that Martin Luther 
visited in 1511 and had his crisis experience  

when he joined all the pilgrims there going up the 
sacred stairs on his knees, saying prayers on each  

step, so that he might win indulgences for his 
soul and for the souls of his departed relatives.  

And when he got to the top of those stairs he 
stood up and he said out loud, ‘Who knows if this  

is true.’ And the seeds of disquietude between 
to assail his soul from that experience until  

he was awakened to the gospel of justification by 
faith alone, and the reformation was underway.” 

But there was a bonus in that trip, serendipity, 
something that I didn’t expect when the tour guide  

took us on our way to the Forum to one little stop 
where we went down into what had been originally  

used as a place to gather water, a cistern 
carved out of solid rock, and it was a chamber  

about fifteen feet wide and fifteen feet deep and 
about eight feet high. And this cavern was cold,  

dark, wet, and dank. I said, “What’s this?” He 
said, “Well, this is the Mamertine Prison where  

the apostle Paul was held captive when he was 
waiting execution.” From the steps of that prison  

one can look across the street and see the ruins 
of the Forum where the Romans legislated their law  

and carried out their law, and as a man in prison 
looks out the window of his cell and can see  

the scaffold for his hanging being built, so the 
apostle Paul waited his last moments when his head  

would be placed upon the executioner’s block. And 
I don’t know, none of us knows, how long Paul was  

held prisoner in that cistern. Was it for several 
months? Was it for several weeks? We don’t know.  

But as I stepped down into that place all I could 
imagine was Paul there in that dank, desperate  

place writing his final letter to Timothy. It 
was spooky. It was like being… It was haunted  

almost. I mean, to stand there and think This is 
where it happened. This is where Paul wrote to his  

beloved disciple and gave his last words. And he 
writes to Timothy these poignant words. “Timothy,  

I’m already being poured out as a drink offering.”
He makes this reference to the Old Testament,  

to the Old Testament sacrifice system 
that when the animals were offered  

in that sacrifice they received an oblation, 
a pouring out of blood on the altar that would  

accompany the sacrifice. Now if anybody in 
history understood that the sacrifice of  

Christ was a perfect sacrifice offered once for 
all and that Paul couldn’t possibly add anything  

of merit or value to that perfect sacrifice 
of Jesus given once for all, nevertheless,  

he was acutely aware that it is every Christian’s 
task to bear witness to the perfect sacrifice of  

Christ by offering to Christ the sacrifice of 
our own lives. Remember at the end of Romans,  

after spelling out the doctrines of the gospel, 
Paul says, “Therefore, my beloved brethren, I  

beseech you by the mercies of God to present your 
bodies as a living sacrifice unto him, which is  

your reasonable service.” Which is your reasonable 
act of worship. And so, Timothy, already I’m being  

poured out as an oblation to honor my Lord.
And then he says,  

“For the time of my departure is at hand.” That 
means it’s very, very close. Now I love the  

Reformation Study Bible. It’s one of my works 
that I took great joy in helping with. I was  

responsible, okay, every note, and then here in 
2 Timothy and we look at this where he says about  

his departure, the particular author here writes 
this: Another metaphor for death. Now that’s all  

the note would have said, I would have X’d it out. 
But it goes on and says: Paul held steadfastly to  

the hope and assurance of a destination beyond 
the grace. Because Paul does not say My death  

is at hand. He doesn’t say to Timothy I’m about to 
perish. He doesn’t say, It’s time for me, Timothy,  

to be killed. I prefer this English translation 
where it says “For my departure is at hand.” 

It’s interesting if you go into the sources, 
the Greek here is somewhat strange because the  

word that is translated for departure is the 
word analusis, which we get the English word  

directly from it analysis. So a more literal 
translation maybe this: that Paul says I’m  

already being poured out, and the time of my 
analysis is near. But if he wrote that today  

somebody would think that he had an appointment 
with his psychiatrist or with his tax attorney to  

analyze his financial situation. But rather, the 
term analysis in English when it was first coined  

was closer to the original meaning in Greek, 
to analyze something was to was to parse it,  

to make close, careful distinctions as you 
separated one part from another. And what  

Paul is saying is not that I’m going to die or 
that my personal existence is going to stop,  

but in a very short time my body is going to be 
separated from my soul. I will still be alive,  

and in my soul I will enter into the presence of 
Christ, though my body will be put into a grave. 

The Latin source here is the word from which 
we get the word disillusion, or even better,  

resolution. So we could translate it this way: 
that Paul says For the time of my resolution  

is near at hand. Now, again, he’s not saying I’m 
about to be killed on New Year’s Eve so I can make  

a resolution. That’s not the kind of resolution 
he’s talking about. He’s talking about resolving  

tension, resolving conflict. Those of you who 
love music know what it’s like when the music  

gets away from the tonic foundation and you begin 
to experience the tension of dissonance and your  

ear is crying that the music be resolved. And the 
great musicians know how to resolve that tension  

by bringing the melody back home, if you will. 
And that’s what Paul is saying is My whole life  

has been a life of stress. My whole life has been 
a life of tension. Didn’t we read that week after  

week in the chronicle provided for us by Luke, 
which instead of the Acts of the Apostle could  

have been reading The Tensions and Distress 
of the Life of Paul. But he said that’s all  

done. The time of my resolution is at hand. 
Every problem that I’ve ever had, every pain  

I’ve ever felt will shortly be resolved. So 
Paul is not writing in a vein where his soul  

is discouraged or cast down. He’s saying, 
Timothy, I am ready to go home. I’m already  

beginning the oblation of the final sacrifice.
You see, Paul died a martyr. And again, there’s  

that one little quirk of the English language. The 
word martyr comes from the Greek word martyrea,  

but the word martyrea in Greek does not mean 
martyr. You know what it means? Witness. And  

the reason why we get that word martyr in our 
English is because the early church showed their  

witness so consistently that often their witness 
ended in their death. And so the word martyrdom,  

which originally was synonymous with witnessing, 
has now come to mean those who witness to the  

ultimate degree of paying with their lives.
And now Paul borrows three metaphors to describe  

that witness that is about to be resolved. 
Where he said, “I have fought the good fight,  

I have finished the race, I’ve kept the faith.” 
I’ve told you before the experience I had  

as a seventeen-year-old boy, who watched his 
father die a day at a time. I used to drag him  

to the dining room table and then back to his 
bedroom every night. And then one night as I  

was dragging him, fireman carry, as it were, 
across the living room floor, he said to me,  

“Stop and put me down for a second on the 
sofa.” And so I let him sit down, and I  

sat down next to him and he looked at me and he 
said, “Son,” he said, “I fought the good fight.”  

He said, “I finished the race.” He said, “I’ve 
kept the faith.” Now you have to understand. I  

had never read the Bible. I wasn’t a Christian. 
I had no idea where those words came from,  

but I knew exactly what my father was saying 
to me. My father was looking me in the eye  

and saying I’m going to die now. Those 
were his last words he ever spoke to me.  

My last words to him, to my everlasting 
shame, were the words “Don’t say that,  

Dad,” not realizing what a tremendous testimony 
of confidence of where he was going my father  

was giving when he recited those words 
to me. And so I carried him back to his  

bed. That night he had his final stroke, 
went into a coma, never awoke again here,  

but he was awake there instantly.
Can you imagine how I felt later  

when I became a Christian and was reading the 
New Testament for the first time and I read  

those words for the first time? I said, wow. I 
can’t believe it how rich those words are. The  

threefold metaphor. First of all, Paul says, “I 
have fought the good fight.” Doesn’t that rankle  

you a little bit? In terms of the contemporary 
idea of what Christianity is the Christian should  

never be engaged in combat or conflict or any 
kind. We’re supposed to be peacekeepers always,  

never engaged in arguing or debate and so. 
The New Testament tells us we’re not to be  

quarrelsome. We’re not to be looking for fights. 
We’re not supposed to be belligerent or bellicose  

in our personality. But there’s a fight out there. 
There is a good fight. There is a war out there,  

a spiritual war, to which everyone of us is called 
to be engaged in. There is such a thing as a good  

fight. Every Christian is supposed to believe 
the things of God. Every Christian is supposed to  

defend the truths of God. But let me take it one 
step further. It’s not enough to hold the faith  

or even to defend the faith, but we’re called to 
contend for the faith. Was that not the example of  

the apostle Paul that for a year or so we’ve seen 
Sunday in and Sunday out? Everywhere he went he  

wasn’t just preaching Christianity, he wasn’t 
just defending it, he was contending for it. 

I remember one of the most famous lines 
in the history of Hollywood goes back to  

On the Waterfront when Marlon Brando says to 
his brother “I could have been a contender.”  

What’s it take to be a contender? Anybody 
can become a professional boxer. Bob Hope  

tried it in an undistinguished way. But to be a 
contender you have to win, and you have to win  

enough that you become ranked, that you have to 
be recognized as one of the top ten fighters in  

your weight division to even be thought of for 
a moment as a contender. And then you have to  

fight your way up that ladder until finally you’re 
able to get a championship bout, a shout at the  

title. The challenger is not the champion until 
he defeats the champion, but he is a contender. 

And Paul was a contender. In fact, Paul was 
a champion. Apart from Christ, the supreme  

champion of the kingdom of God in that day.
And he said, I have fought, Timothy,  

and I have fought… It’s interesting the way the 
language goes. “I have fought the fight.” Well,  

if you fought you were in a fight, and if you 
were in a fight, you fought.” And so it’s almost  

redundant, but he says “I have fought the 
fight.” But he wants to say I have fought  

the good fight. There is a good fight, and we 
are all called to be enlisted in that battle. 

Now he changes the metaphor from the boxer to 
the runner where he said “I finished the race.”  

Now the race that Paul’s talking about here is 
not the 100-yard dash. It’s easy, in one sense,  

to be a sprinter, to work like mad for the 
kingdom over the short haul. But Paul wasn’t  

interested in a 100-yard dash; he was in a 
marathon, and he was in a marathon that went  

a lot longer than twenty-six miles. He went in 
a marathon that seemed like it would never end.  

His whole life he was engaged in that race.
Just this morning I was talking to one of our  

members about something, and they said to me, “R. 
C., you got to be patient.” I said, “I know that.  

I pray for patience all the time.” I say, “Lord, 
give me patience right now.” And he says, “My  

grace is sufficient for you.” And he says, “You 
got to take things slowly.” I said, “You don’t  

know me very well. I got two speeds: fast and 
off. There is no such thing as slow.” But that’s  

what it takes to be a marathoner, to endure, 
not to just run for a hundred yards. I mean,  

who would be proud and say to their grandson, 
you know, son, once I was in a 100-yard dash.  

And you should have seen me out of the blocks. I 
was running like my shoes were on fire. But at the  

40-yard mark I dropped out. Who would be proud of 
not finishing a 100-yard dash? I mean, that’s not  

very great. But if I came in last in a marathon 
but finished, I would be proud to tell that  

to my grandchildren to say I finished the race.
This is what Paul is saying to Timothy. Timothy, I  

didn’t just start. I didn’t just sprint. But I ran 
and I ran and I ran and I ran. I was gasping for  

air, and I kept on running. My legs were burning 
with fire, but I kept moving for the hope that  

was in front of me. And now, Timothy, the race is 
over. I broke the tape. I made it the whole way.  

And that whole time, Timothy, I kept the faith.
Here the illustration comes from the military,  

because even in the ancient world as well as 
in the contemporary world the responsibility of  

every soldier was fidelity to the general, to his 
country. Duty, honor, country means fidelity. And  

when the shooting starts the faithful soldier 
doesn’t run. He doesn’t quit. He goes forward.  

And that’s what Paul says about his life. Like a 
first-century marine his motto was Simper Fidelis,  

Always Faithful. And not only in that sense, 
but the other nuance is he was also faithful  

to the doctrine that the Lord gave to him. He 
didn’t change his theology every time somebody  

disapproved of it. He didn’t negotiate and 
compromise the truth every time somebody was  

angered by it. He said I stayed the course. I 
remained faithful to my Lord and to his Word.  

Could anybody have a better epitaph than that?
And so he concludes by saying “Finally,  

there is already laid up for me [in the layaway 
section of heaven; it already has my name on it]  

the crown of righteousness.” In the ancient world 
in the Olympic games when the winner was honored  

in the winners’ circle nobody came and hung a 
gold medal around their neck or a silver medal  

or a bronze medal. Rather, the victor was adorned 
with the laurel wreath symbolizing the title crown  

in his sport. But now he’s talking about not the 
world heavyweight crown or the Olympic crown;  

he’s talking about the crown of righteousness 
that Christ has put aside, has Paul’s name on  

it. He said and now I am ready to receive that 
crown. Which crown of righteousness not only will  

I receive, but all of you who love his coming 
will also receive that crown of righteousness. 

So you see, Timothy, I’m ready to go. And 
even though he mentions those who betray  

him in the rest of the closing lines of 
the letter, he ends with doxology where  

he thanks God for his mercy and his grace. And 
his final words to Timothy “Grace be with you.” 

How I would have loved to have been a fly on 
the wall to watch Timothy when he received that  

parchment of this letter and watch him as he began 
in chapter one and read through, gets to chapter  

three and the exhortation about holding fast to 
Scripture, and then come to chapter four and watch  

Timothy’s tears rolling down his checks as he sees 
these words that Paul is about to die. And he had  

to be weeping when he read these words. And yet, 
at the same time, you could see his chest start  

to swell. That’s my mentor. That’s the apostle 
praying for grace for me and for his people.
