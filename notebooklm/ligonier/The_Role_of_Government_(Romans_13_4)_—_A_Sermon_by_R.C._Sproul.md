# The Role of Government (Romans 13:4) — A Sermon by R.C. Sproul

SPROUL: Well, this evening we’re going 
to continue our study of Paul’s letter  

to the church of Rome. Last week we 
started our study of chapter thirteen,  

and we learned how God has ordained 
civil government to be ministers of his  

sovereign rule over all of the world. And 
I stopped before I dealt with verse four,  

and so I’ll pick it up this evening at verse four 
of chapter thirteen. And I’ll ask the congregation  

to stand for the reading of the Word of God.
For he is God’s minister to you for good.  

But if you do evil, be afraid; for he does not 
bear the sword in vain; for he is God’s minister,  

an avenger to execute wrath on him who 
practices evil. Therefore you must be subject,  

not only because of wrath but also for conscience’ 
sake. For because of this you also pay taxes,  

for they are God’s ministers attending 
continually to this very thing. Render  

therefore to all their due: taxes to whom 
taxes are due, customs to whom customs,  

fear to whom fear, [and] honor to whom honor.
Again, this is not the insights of a first-century  

Jewish scholar, but this is the Word 
of God. Please be seated. Let us pray. 

Father, as we continue to explore the 
implications of these things that are  

revealed to us in your holy Word, we ask that 
you would grant us clarity of understanding  

and hearts that are submissive to the rule of 
your Word. For we ask it in Jesus’ name. Amen. 

Last week as we came to verse four I interrupted 
my message and said that the contents of that  

verse are so many and so weighty that I didn’t 
think I could rush through that verse in the  

few moments we had left last Sunday evening. 
But I’d like to read again for you verse 4:  

“For he” that is the civil magistrate, “is God’s 
minister to you for good. But if you do evil,  

be afraid; for he does not bear the sword in 
vain; for he is God’s minister, an avenger  

to execute wrath on him who practices evil.”
Now this verse, Romans 13:4b, is one of the  

most important verses in all of Scripture in 
terms of the development of historic, classic  

Christian ethics with respect to two monumentally 
important issues of ethics. Number one being the  

issue of capital punishment, and number two 
being the controversial issue of warfare and  

whether Christians can in good conscience ever 
been engaged in war. And what is so important  

about this text is that it is God who has given to 
the civil magistrate the power of the sword. And  

that concept in the language that is phrased “the 
power of the sword” is an idiomatic expression for  

the power of capital punishment, for the power of 
using a weapon that can bring death to enforce the  

law of God. Now the idea of the swords being 
used to enforce the law of God is established  

exceedingly early in the biblical record.
Let me ask you to move back with me for a  

second to chapter three of the book of Genesis. 
The third chapter of Genesis records for us the  

circumstances of the Fall of the human race into 
sin, seduction of Adam and Eve by the serpent  

in the Garden. And then we read at the end of 
that chapter in verse twenty-two these words. 

Then the Lord God said, “Behold, the man has 
become like one of us to know good and evil.”  

And now lest he put out his hand and take also 
of the tree of life and eat and live forever,  

therefore the Lord God sent him out of the 
Garden of Eden to till the ground from which  

he was taken. So he drove out the man and he 
placed cherubim at the east of the Garden of  

Eden and a flaming sword, which turned every 
way, to guard the way to the tree of life. 

So in addition of the loss of the blessing 
that Adam and Eve would have received at the  

hand of their Creator had they passed their 
probation by rendering their obedience rather  

than disobedience, also the consequences of 
their sin included the curse that came upon man,  

the curse that came upon woman, the curse that 
came upon the earth and all things in it. And  

then God drove Adam and Eve out of the Garden of 
Eden where they were forced to live east of Eden  

outside of the presence of that paradise in which 
they were originally placed by their Creator.  

Now in addition to this exile, in addition to this 
banishment where now human beings were forbidden  

to enter again into the Garden of Eden least they 
should attack even the tree of life unjustly,  

God established a sentinel. He put angels at the 
gateway to the Garden armed with a flaming sword  

that turned in each direction in order to prevent 
any intrusion or invasion into that Garden again. 

Here we see the first establishment of physical 
force as a government and governing restraint upon  

sinful people in order to guard the entrance to 
the Garden of Eden. And that image of that sword  

that was placed there as a guard is the image that 
is used then throughout Scripture to indicate the  

authority that is carried by those who were given 
the responsibility of enforcing the law of God.  

We know that in the Old Testament there were 
several offenses that were considered so  

heinous that for the commission of them God in the 
civil code of Israel required the death penalty. 

Now if I can just turn briefly further in Genesis 
to chapter nine, I will read another text that  

is of ultra importance in understanding this 
idea of the power of the sword. Chapter nine  

of Genesis begins with these words.
So God blessed Noah and his sons,  

and said to them: “Be fruitful and multiply, 
and fill the earth. And the fear of you and  

the dread of you shall be on every beast 
of the earth, on every bird of the air,  

on all that move on the earth, and on all the fish 
of the sea. They are given into your hand. Every  

moving thing that lives shall be food for you. 
I have given you all things, even as the green  

herbs. But you shall not eat flesh with its life, 
that is, its blood. Surely for your lifeblood I  

will demand a reckoning; from the hand of every 
beast I will require it, and from the hand of  

man. From the hand of every man’s brother I will 
require the life of man. [Now we have this text  

in chapter nine verse six.]
“Whoever sheds man’s blood, 

By man his blood shall be shed;
For in the image of God 

He made man.”
Now here we see the biblical institution of  

capital punishment for the instance of murder. And 
I want to just pause for a moment to look at this,  

because the way in which this text is expressed 
maybe easily misunderstood. It says in verse 6:  

“Whoever sheds man’s blood, by man his blood shall 
be shed.” We could misinterpret this text to have  

it read simply something like that, He who lives 
by the sword shall die from the sword, kind of as  

a cryptic prophecy of the consequences of living 
a violent life. But the text here in the original  

indicates not a future prediction of what would 
happen to people who shed human blood, but this  

text is written in the form of the imperative. 
God is requiring the death penalty for murder. 

Now when I say that I have to also qualify that. 
If you look through the law code of the Old  

Testament you will see that the offense of murder 
is carefully explained. Distinctions are made in  

the Old Testament law that would correspond 
to our distinctions between first and second  

degree murder, between murder and manslaughter. 
In the case of manslaughter the penalty was not  

death but banishment to cities of refuge and so 
on. But where first degree murder was committed,  

it was not simply that the law of Israel 
allow for capital punishment, but indeed  

the civil magistrates of Israel were commanded 
to execute the one guilty of first degree murder. 

I remember several years ago when the question 
of capital punishment was before the state  

legislature in Pennsylvania. And originally, 
of course, Pennsylvania did have the death  

penalty for murder. And then it repealed it for a 
season, and when it was brought up on the docket  

to be restored to the law of the land the state 
legislature voted to restore capital punishment  

in the case of first degree murder. At that time 
that bill to restore capital punishment was vetoed  

by the governor of the state of Pennsylvania, 
who himself was Jewish by faith. And the reason  

he announced to the press for vetoing capital 
punishment was that because it was unbiblical.  

He argued that the Bible says simply “Thou 
shalt not kill.” And therefore, he said,  

since God prohibits killing human beings we 
must not tolerate the execution of murderers  

through capital punishment. Of course if the 
governor had read just a few pages further in  

the Old Testament he would have seen that 
what God required for the violation of the  

prohibition against murder was the death penalty.
But many Christians have been strongly misinformed  

about this biblical position. Now what I think 
is so important here about the text that I’ve  

just read to you that when capital punishment 
was instituted by God to Noah it was part of a  

renewal of the covenant of creation. And we have 
to understand that throughout biblical history  

we have many, many different covenants that are 
set forth. There is the covenant that God makes  

with Abraham, and then with Isaac and Jacob, 
the covenant that is mediated through Moses,  

the covenant that God makes with David, of course 
the new covenant that is instituted by Jesus in  

the New Testament, but the original covenant 
is called the covenant of creation. And it’s  

important that when God establishes his law 
in creation he does not establish laws just  

for Jews or just for Christians, but the laws 
built into creation are those laws that are  

given to man qua man, to every one who is a 
human being. And so every person who is alive  

on this planet to this day is still under the 
authority of the terms of the creation covenant. 

Now we have a lot of people who don’t believe 
in creation, don’t believe that they’re under  

any covenant responsibility towards God, 
but the denial of that covenant and the  

unbelief in that covenant does not eliminate 
that covenant. Every human being remains in  

a covenant relationship with God inescapably. 
And we are either covenant keepers or covenant  

breakers. The vast majority of the human 
race exists in a state of covenant rebellion. 

Now we notice that after God established 
this covenant in creation and sin came into  

the world and proliferated so rapidly that God 
decided to virtually wipe out the human race,  

saying that he would no longer strive with 
this wicked generation, save for the family of  

Noah. And the whole account of the flood was the 
account, on the one hand, of God’s judgment on the  

mass of fallen humanity, and on the other hand, of 
his grace that was shown to Noah and his family,  

and Noah’s being the instrument of the rescue 
of the wildlife of this world, according to God.  

And after the flood receded, the ark came to rest 
and God then reestablished a covenant with Noah,  

and he set his bow in the sky and said he would 
no longer destroy the world by the flood. But in  

that covenant that we cal. The Noahic Covenant 
we see the restitution and repetition of the  

ordinances and laws of the creation covenant.
Now why am I laboring this point? The point is  

this—that this law of capital punishment for 
murder is not something that is restricted to  

the law code and civil penalties of Israel in 
the Old Testament, nor is it a portion of the  

jurisprudence of the New Testament, but it is 
a law that is rooted and grounded in creation.  

That means as long as creation lasts this 
principle of capital punishment in the  

case of first degree murder is in effect.
Now let me ask the next question. Why? Why  

is it in effect? A few years ago I read an article 
from Larry King in which he was being outspoken in  

his criticism of the Christian community for the 
Christian community’s gross inconsistency. And he  

argued in this manner. He said the Christian 
community keeps protesting against abortion  

on demand on the grounds that it involves the 
taking of human life while at the same time these  

Christians, he said, argue in favor of capital 
punishment. And Larry King said I will not support  

the Christian’s opposition to abortion until the 
Christian church stops its support of capital  

punishment. And you see what Larry King was 
saying. He said we’re inconsistent because on the  

one hand we’re opposed to killing and on the other 
hand we support killing. Now what I wanted to say  

to Mr. King but never had the opportunity was to 
show him that there was no inconsistency in here  

at all. You may argue that the Christian church is 
wrong, that the Bible is mythological and all the  

rest, but there is a strong and coherent point of 
consistency that stands behind both the church’s  

opposition to abortion and the church’s support 
of capital punishment. And what that principle is,  

is this, the sanctity of human life. That 
principle which resounds on virtually every  

page of sacred Scripture reiterated emphatically 
by Jesus in the Sermon on the Mount is this—that  

human life is so sacred that we must never, ever, 
ever, ever, ever, ever take it with malice of  

forethought or out of personal convenience. And 
the reason why human life is so sacred is not  

because there’s some inherent value that is found 
in human beings that is not found among whales and  

eagles and burrowing turtles. What makes the value 
of human life so significant is what is reiterated  

here in Genesis chapter nine. “Whosoever shed 
man’s blood, but man his blood shall be shed…”  

Why? “…for in the image of God he made man.” See, 
that’s what makes us different from the turtles  

and the whales and the chickens and the kangaroos, 
that we have been stamped with the image of God. 

And what God is saying here, dear friends, 
is this. That if somebody with malice of  

forethought rises up like Cain did to Abel 
and kills his brother, the attack on the  

human being that the murderer commits is 
seen by God as an attack on God himself,  

because that human being who was murdered is 
one who is engraved and stamped with the image  

of God himself. And God is saying this. That human 
life is so sacred, so important that if you rise  

up without just cause and kill your neighbor in 
terms of first degree murder, you at that moment  

have lost all rights and privileges with respect 
to your own life. You have forfeit your own life  

and God gives it not to the victim’s relative to 
seek vengeance, but upon the one to whom he has  

given the sword to be God’s avenger and to see 
to it that that punishment is carried out, not  

for the sake of being blood thirsty, but for the 
sake of saying to the world we will not tolerate  

the murder of human beings.
That’s the biblical rationale,  

if you will, the rationale that is seen in 
the Old Testament, the rationale that is seen  

again in Romans 13 when we see God ordaining 
the civil magistrate and giving the power to  

the civil magistrate to be God’s avenger. 
When he gives him the power of the sword,  

dear friends, he doesn’t give him the 
power of the sword merely to rattle  

it. But the power of the sword is given 
for its use to enforce law and justice. 

Now this same verse, the same text is the single 
most important text and serves the locus classicus  

in Christian history for Christian ethics on the 
question of the just war theory. And I’d like to  

spend a few moments with you tonight to talk about 
how the just war theory throughout church history  

has followed the writing here of the apostle 
Paul in Romans 13. We go back, for example,  

to St. Augustine, who made this observation about 
war and about people’s participation in war.  

He said all wars are evil. He excluded from 
that the divinely ordained conquest of Canaan,  

but since we don’t have the direct instructions 
of God from heaven to this day to wage war we  

are left to make those decisions on the basis of 
our human understanding and the application of  

principles drawn from sacred Scripture. And on 
that Augustine said all wars are evil. There’s  

no such thing today as a good war. But, Augustine 
said, not everybody’s involvement in war is evil. 

St. Thomas Aquinas seconded the motion, and in 
his moral theology worked out the details of  

what is involved in a just war. Without going into 
all of the technicalities of it, the fundamental  

principle of the just war theory is this—that 
if one nation or one people aggressively invades  

or attacks another nation that nation, who 
has been attacked and is the victim of the  

external aggression, not only has the right but 
the responsibility to protect themselves from the  

invading aggressor. When Hitler invaded Poland, 
Czechoslovakia, the Lowlands, the other nations,  

the local governments of those nations had the 
moral duty to try to stop the Blitzkrieg from  

coming and enslaving their people. At that 
point the warriors or those nations that  

were being invaded were fulfilling their duty 
as being civil magistrates bearing the sword  

to protect the lives of their own people.
Now notice that sentence to protect the  

lives. Again, the sanctity of life is what lies 
behind the just war theory, that life, again,  

is so sacred that the civil magistrate has been 
given the sword to protect the innocent from the  

evildoer, just as the policeman has been given 
arms and the right to use arms to stop your wife  

from being raped, your house from being invaded, 
your body from being harmed or killed. When the  

civil magistrate uses reasonable force to restrain 
the evildoer he is serving not only the community,  

but he is serving God in that enterprise. 
And again, when we talk about just war we’re  

just elevating those principles to the larger 
domain of national security and national safety. 

In our day and age when we have the opportunity 
to invade without ever bringing soldiers across  

borders but by sending missiles across oceans and 
the kinds of sophistication that you see in modern  

weaponry issues of just war become more and more 
and more complex. And obviously the mentality of  

the New Testament or of the Christian church 
historically is not to encourage a bellicose  

national posture or a militaristic style that 
tries to scare everybody else in the world away,  

but rather, to be and attempt as nations 
to be good neighbors to other nations,  

to be loving to other nations, and to use 
the sword as the last of all possible resorts  

when the defense of the people becomes a 
clear and present measure of necessity. 

I remember back in the sixties, the late sixties, 
a time of the Vietnam War, the way in which our  

country was so divided between hawks and doves on 
that issue. I used to listen to the politicians on  

both sides of that question with their rhetoric. 
And the thing that disturbed me so much was that  

I heard arguments from both sides. Didn’t 
have any soundness to them whatsoever. The  

hawks were saying my country, right or wrong, 
my country. Christians should never say that,  

my country right or wrong, my country. 
My country’s wrong, I must oppose it,  

particularly when human blood is being shed. And 
on the other ones were saying Better red than  

dead, and all this other kind of rhetoric. And 
nobody was really looking at the international  

issues and the issues of international 
law that were involved in that question. 

Now an unenviable position I had in the middle of 
the sixties as a college professor was that I had  

many, many students who were violently opposed, 
well, I should say vehemently opposed to the war  

in Vietnam, who were applying for conscientious 
objector status. And as was the rule at that time,  

they had to file affidavits to the government 
and to the draft boards by people who would, by  

penalty of perjury, give their testimony that in 
their minds the student’s conscientious objection  

was sincere. Now I don’t know how many of those 
papers I had to fill out for students. And again,  

my responsibility there was not to give my view 
on the war, whether I was for it or against it.  

That was beside the point. All the government want 
to know from me was in my judgment did I believe  

that this particularly student was sincere in 
his conscientious objection to the conflict  

in Vietnam. Do you understand the difference?
Now so many students filed objections to the war  

that the Supreme Court of the United States made 
a decision in those days that was, in my judgment,  

one of the worst miscarriages of justice I’ve ever 
seen and one that was made without a peep from the  

Christian community. What happened was the Supreme 
Court of the United States ruled that no one could  

be given conscientious objection status anymore 
unless they could demonstrate that they were  

opposed to all wars. Let me say it again. And 
that’s the present rule of the land. That you  

cannot be a conscientious objector to war unless 
you can demonstrate that you are conscientiously  

opposed to all wars. You must be a card carrying 
pacifist and you cannot choose your war.  

Now that’s really strange because at the Nuremburg 
trials when the war crimes were tried after World  

War II officer after officer after officer 
pled the same excuse for the atrocities that  

they committed in the death camps in World 
War II during the holocaust. They said that  

they were merely carrying out orders, and it was 
their superiors that were responsible, not them,  

because as good German soldiers they simply did 
what their commanding officers commanded them to  

do. And what did the United States argue as this 
position? Our government argued at Nuremburg that  

it is the responsibility of an individual soldier 
to disobey a command given to him by a superior  

officer if that superior officer commands him 
to do something evil. This is the principle we  

talked about last week—or at least, I talked and 
you listened—in terms of chapter thirteen in terms  

of our responsibility to be obedient to the civil 
magistrate unless that magistrate commands us to  

do something God forbids or forbids us from 
doing something God commands. In other words,  

what the position the US took after World 
War II was that those German soldiers should  

have been conscientious objectors, without 
taking position that all wars are evil, that  

everybody’s involvement in war is always evil.
But now it is the Christian who is discriminated  

against. If he holds to the just war theory 
that involvement in war is only legitimate for a  

Christian if the cause is just. But if I see that 
my nation gets involved in a war that is not just,  

even though I hold the position that there are 
such occasions as just war, I do not have the  

legal recourse in my own country to say that I 
refuse to submit to that order. That’s a very,  

very serious matter, because as a Christian 
before you pick up a gun or a knife or a sword  

and kill anybody, you better make sure that the 
cause is just. And it’s silly to assume that any  

government on the face of the earth today can be 
trusted only to be engaged in military activity  

in a just way. I don’t know of any nation in 
history that has not at one point or another  

used its own power and authority in an unjust 
manner. That’s why we have to be vigilant. We  

have to be scrupulous that when the nation is 
in peril and when the nation is involved in the  

just defense of its borders and of its people and 
if the civil magistrate calls me to pick up the  

sword in participation of the civil magistrate’s 
just exercise of the civil magistrate’s duty and  

conscripts me to do that, it is my duty to use 
the sword. But if he conscripts me to engage in  

an unjust aggression or invasion of an innocent 
nation, then I am equally obligated to say no. 

No one ever said that living the Christian life 
is simple or that making ethnical decisions in  

the Christian life is always an easy matter. As 
I said last week, the principle’s easy. We’re  

always to obey the authorities over us unless 
those authorities command us to do something  

God forbids, or forbids us from doing something 
that God commands. We can’t disobey the civil  

magistrate because they inconvenience us, because 
they burden us with too heavy taxation or because  

we disagree with their wisdom. Those are 
not just excuses for civil disobedience.  

But at the same time, we’re not to render 
slavish obedience to any authority,  

because authorities can work against the Word of 
God. And we have to be very careful whenever we  

say yea and whenever we say nay, to make sure 
that our decisions are motivated by an earnest  

desire to obey God in all that he commands.
But again, this is just a mention in passing  

that you can see that when Paul writes that the 
civil magistrate has been given the sword by God,  

the power of the sword, that he has been 
appointed to be an avenger to execute wrath  

of him who practices evil. We have to see therein 
the biblical basis for the establishment of force  

that is given to civil magistrates. If the civil 
magistrate uses that sword to promote evil,  

then the civil magistrate will be judged by God 
in all of God’s wrath. And God himself will take  

vengeance upon that magistrate and upon such 
bellicose nations. Remember, dear friends,  

it is the Lord who raises nations up and it is 
the Lord who brings them down. And we just as  

the Christians of the first century who received 
this letter from the apostle Paul when they were  

in Rome they knew everything about the corruption 
of the Roman system, and yet they listened to  

their apostle defending the authority that God 
had given to the Roman empire to wield the sword. 

I’m afraid that when we object to capital 
punishment in principle, when we object to  

warfare in principle, we are objecting to those 
very things which God himself has established and  

instituted. The reason why the sword is necessary 
is because sin is in the world, and the sword is  

given to work against and to restrain evildoers 
in order to protect the lives of the innocent. 

One last thing. We need to understand in terms 
of the biblical emphasis on the sanctity of human  

life that the primary responsibility, the primary 
mission, the primary task of any civil government,  

whether it’s in China, Russia, the 
United States, Iran or wherever it is,  

the primary responsibility that God gives to 
every human government, secular or sacred,  

is to protect, defend, and maintain human life. 
And when any government turns its back on that  

primary responsibility it is acting in utter 
defiance to the law of God and is exposing  

itself as a nation to the judgment of God. I’ve 
said it before, I’ll say it again. In my opinion,  

the most serious ethical issue that this nation 
has ever had to deal with, way more serious than  

slavery, is abortion on demand, the slaughter of 
the innocent. And now people don’t even bother to  

try to argue that that which is being destroyed 
is not human life. It’s openly acknowledged. But  

what is being destroyed legally with the 
sanction of the federal government and of  

state governments throughout the nation is 
the willful taking of human life. The very  

thing that God appoints government to protect 
against the government now supports. That’s  

a travesty on the sanctity of life. And I hope 
that none of you will ever march in that parade. 

Let’s pray.
Our Father and our God,  

we shutter to think of the ways in which the 
nations of this world have defied your law. They  

will not use the sword when they are called to 
use the sword, and use the sword when they should  

never even touch it. Father, give us a capacity 
to know the difference between good and evil,  

to know the difference between the just views 
of force and godless tyranny. And gives us the  

courage to speak when we see these things turned 
upside down. For we ask it in Jesus’ name. Amen.
