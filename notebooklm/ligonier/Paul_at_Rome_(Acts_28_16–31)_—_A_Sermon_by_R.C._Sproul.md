# Paul at Rome (Acts 28:16–31) — A Sermon by R.C. Sproul

SPROUL: For many months now we have been 
looking at the Book of the Acts of the Apostles,  

and this morning we come to the end of that 
narrative of the history of the early church,  

and so this will be the last sermon that 
I will preach from the text of the Book of  

Acts. Although, God willing, next week I would 
like to have an epilogue to conclude our entire  

study of the Acts of the Apostles. This morning 
I will be reading from Acts 28:17 and reading  

through to the end of the chapter in verse 31.
And it came to pass after three days that Paul  

called the leaders of the Jews together, so 
when they had come together he said to them,  

“Men and brethren, though I have done nothing 
against our people or the customs of our fathers,  

yet I was delivered as a prisoner from 
Jerusalem into the hands of the Romans.  

When they had examined me wanted to let me 
go because there was no cause for putting  

me to death. But when the Jews spoke against 
it, I was compelled to appeal to Caesar—not  

that I had anything of which to accuse 
my nation. For this reason, therefore,  

I have called for you to see you and speak with 
you, because it is for the hope of Israel that  

I am bound with this chain.” And then they 
said to him, “We neither received letters  

from Judea concerning you, nor have any of the 
brethren who came reported or spoken any evil  

of you. But we desire to hear from you what 
you think, for concerning this sect we know  

that it is spoken against everywhere.”
But when they had appointed him a day,  

many came to him at his lodging to whom 
he explained and solemnly testified of the  

kingdom of God persuading them concerning 
Jesus from both the Law of Moses and the  

Prophets from morning till evening. Some were 
persuaded by the things that were spoken,  

and some disbelieved. And so when they did not 
agree among themselves, they departed after Paul  

had said one word. “The Holy Spirit spoke rightly 
through Isaiah the prophet to our fathers, saying,  

‘Go to this people and say, Hearing you will hear 
and shall not understand, and seeing you shall see  

and not perceive. For the hearts of this people 
have grown dull. Their ears are hard of hearing.  

Their eyes they have closed. Les they should 
see with their eyes and hear with their ears,  

lest they should understand with their hearts and 
turn so that I should heal them.” Therefore let  

it be known to you that the salvation of God has 
been sent to the Gentiles, and they will hear it.”  

And when he said these words, the Jews departed 
and had a great dispute among themselves. 

Then Paul dwelt two whole years in his own 
rented house, and received all who came to him,  

preaching the kingdom of God, and teaching the 
things which concern the Lord Jesus Christ,  

with all confidence, no one forbidding him.
May God bless to our understanding the  

conclusion of this record of the church that was 
founded in the blood of our Lord Jesus Christ. 

Once more, O God, as we turn our attention to this 
narrative in its closing paragraphs, we plead for  

the assistance of the Holy Spirit, who inspired 
this record in the first place, that that Spirit  

who testifies of Christ might put Christ before us 
anew even in this day, and that you would give us  

ears to hear and eyes to see (the?) sweetness 
of Jesus. For we ask it in his name. Amen.

I began reading this morning at verse 17, but I 
want to back up for a moment to verse 16, where  

we read these words from Luke. “Now when we came 
to Rome the centurion delivered the prisoners to  

the captain of the guard. But Paul was permitted 
to dwell by himself with the soldier who guarded  

him.” Now we know that when Paul made this arduous 
voyage to Rome with the 276 people aboard the ship  

that ended in wrecking and the rescue of all who 
were onboard that there were many prisoners who  

were being carried to Rome for imprisonment, and 
Paul was only one of those prisoners. And so Luke  

tells us that when they finally came into the 
city of Rome that the centurion turned all of  

the prisoners over to the one who was in charge of 
the fortress prison in the barracks there in Rome,  

except for one person. Paul was not incarcerated 
in the jail there in Rome, but rather was put on  

house arrest. Very much like what Martha Stewart 
experienced when she was released from prison in  

West Virginia, and Martha Stewart had to endure 
with the ankle bracelet that gives the electronic  

signal that lets the authorities know where she is 
at every moment, and she’s bothered by this final  

vestigial remnant of her imprisonment. But Paul 
was made to go to house arrest, and he could enjoy  

the company of his friends, but he was kept under 
guard for twenty-four hours, and he was chained  

by the wrist to a guard. And the guards changed 
their shift every four hours. So that meant in  

a given twenty-four hour period, do the math, 
six different guards were chained at different  

times to the wrist of the apostle Paul. Now I’m 
sure they did not appreciate that guard duty,  

because they were restricted to going wherever 
their prisoner was going and do whatever their  

prisoner wanted to do. And yet, there were no 
more blessed prison guards in the history of the  

world than those six men who had the unspeakable 
privilege of being cuffed to the wrist of the  

world’s greatest preacher, greatest theologian, 
greatest missionary and greatest teacher. Imagine  

what their situation would be at the judgment 
day if they ignored the teaching that they  

heard while they were guarding God’s anointed.
I’ve never been under house arrest nor ever  

seen anybody attached like that by a chain to a 
guard’s wrist. The closest thing to it was when  

I was teaching at Gordon-Conwell Seminary during 
the administration of Gerald Ford. His son Mike  

had spent a month or so as a resident student 
at Ligonier Ministries in Pennsylvania and then  

had enrolled in the seminary there in Boston at 
Gordon-Conwell to prepare for the ministry. And  

though he was the son of the President of the 
United States, most of the time he felt like a  

prisoner because Mike was accompanied everywhere 
he went not by one guard but by two, two members  

of the Secret Service. And so every day when 
he would come to my class, which was in a big  

amphitheater that seated maybe 125 or so students, 
that the Secret Service agents would come to the  

back of the room and escort Mike to his chair and 
then they would go back out and stand guard by  

each of the doors that went to the outside. So 
Mike was free to be a student without being in  

chains. But I will never forget that in probably 
the third day of lectures the secret service  

agents that were sitting out in the hall started 
coming in the room and started listening to the  

lecture, and they sat there for the whole course 
taking notes. They didn’t get any credit for.  

But I thought wasn’t that amazing that these 
men probably had been influence by Mike while  

he was under their protection, so that they 
had taken an interest in the things of God,  

which was not a part of their duties as 
secret service agents. And so I wonder.  

I just can’t help but wonder what happened to 
these guards that were with Paul day and night  

during two years of his house arrest in Rome.
Well, then we are told in verse 17 that after  

three days Paul summoned the leaders of the Jewish 
community there in Rome to visit him in his home  

or apartment, wherever he was residing. He didn’t 
go to the synagogue, as was his custom, because  

he was under arrest. But he sent a message to the 
leaders and invited them to come and visit him,  

and many of them took up the invitation and 
they came to interview Paul. And when they  

came together he gave them his story in brief. He 
said, “Men and Brothers, though I’ve done nothing  

against our people, against our customs, yet” 
here I am. “I’ve been delivered as a prisoner from  

Jerusalem into the hands of the Romans.” And when 
the Romans examined me they wanted to let me go  

because they found no cause for putting me to 
death. But the Jews,” back there “spoke against  

it,” and as a result “I was compelled to appeal 
to Caesar.” That’s why I’m here. But I still  

have nothing against my own nation.
There was no spirit of vengeance, no  

vindictiveness in the heart of Paul. Remember when 
he wrote to the Romans three years earlier than  

this episode he took a solemn vow before God and 
said to the people I would be willing, basically,  

to lose my own salvation if it would mean the 
conversion of my kinsmen according to the flesh,  

Israel. And so even now in chains Paul is pouring 
out his heart to the Jewish leaders and telling  

them he has that same love for his own people.
And he goes on to say “For this reason … I have  

called for you to see you and speak to you, 
because it’s for the hope of Israel that I  

were this chain.” It’s because I’m a Jew. It’s 
because of my love of Judaism that I am here.  

Let’s talk. So Luke tells us that they responded 
to Paul’s announcement by saying, hey, we haven’t  

heard anything from the leaders in Jerusalem. We 
haven’t received any letters. We haven’t received  

any messages, and no one’s come here to Rome to 
bear witness against you. So what’s up with this  

new sect that we hear about that everybody speaks 
against? Now why no one had sent any messages yet  

from Jerusalem is a little bit of a puzzle, 
because Rome required that anybody who had  

a person arrested like they had Paul arrested 
were compelled to come to Rome to bring their  

prosecuting evidence to bear in the court. But 
so far no one from Jerusalem had shown up. And  

I don’t think it’s because those who were opposed 
to Paul in Jerusalem had lost their zeal. I think  

it’s probably because the time of the year. 
We understand that Paul barely made it to Rome  

because they were sailing in tempestuous times 
at the wrong time of the year before shipping  

actually would resume in the spring, and 
so probably the elders in Jerusalem were  

just waiting for better weather for them to 
come down and bring their case against Paul. 

But anyway, this people say we don’t know 
anything about that, but we do know something  

about this sect of Christians that are around 
us here in Rome that everybody has something  

bad to say about. What do you say about that? 
And so Paul now is given the opportunity again  

to bear witness to the Jewish leaders about the 
Christian faith. Now listen to how he does it. 

“So when they appointed him a day, many came 
to him at his lodging to whom he explained  

and solemnly testified of the kingdom of God 
persuading them concerning Jesus from both  

the Law of Moses and the Prophets from morning 
until evening.” What was his point of contact?  

His first point of explanation was the kingdom 
of God. Because, beloved, if there’s any idea,  

if there’s any concept, any motif that connects 
the Old Testament to the New Testament it is  

that central strand in the tapestry of the 
Word of God of the kingdom of God. In fact,  

even when we look at the word gospel in the New 
Testament and we see that in Paul’s letters where  

he’s teaching the church, he talks all the time 
about the gospel of Jesus Christ because the  

content of the gospel has to do with the person of 
Jesus and the work of Christ. But before we hear  

this gospel of Jesus Christ the first reference 
to the gospel is to the gospel of the kingdom. 

You remember when John the Baptist appeared on the 
scene he came forth by the Jordan River calling  

for the nation of Israel to repent. Why? He said, 
“Repent, for the kingdom of God is at hand.” When  

Jesus began his public ministry his message was 
exactly the same. “Repent, for the kingdom of God  

is at hand.” And throughout his earthly ministry, 
particularly in his parables, Jesus would explain  

this idea of the kingdom of God to his hearers But 
particularly with parables he’d say the kingdom of  

God is like unto this…and the kingdom of God is 
like unto that. And so at the center of Jesus’  

preaching was this idea of the kingdom of God.
What’s that about?  

All through the pages of the Old Testament the 
Lord God omnipotent who reigns promises his  

people that he will make his sovereign authority 
manifest, he will make it public when he sends his  

Anointed Messiah into the world. And so the Old 
Testament is all about the promised King of the  

kingdom of God. And so that’s where Paul starts. 
He said our people for centuries have been waiting  

for our king. Let’s go back to Moses. Let’s go 
back to Abraham. Let’s go back to Isaac. Let’s  

go to David. Let’s go to the prophets, because 
that single chord is being sounded again and  

again and again that the King of kings is coming. 
And by going to the Jewish Scriptures, he set  

forth Christ and the gospel of Christ. This was no 
three-minute salvation message, because it would  

take Paul from morning until evening to expound 
all of the content of the Word of God that was  

driving history to that point of the coming King.
Well, what happened when he explained all of  

that to them? We’re told in verse 24 and “some 
were persuaded by the things that were spoken,  

and some disbelieved.” Hasn’t that always been 
the case wherever the gospel is proclaimed?  

Some are persuaded while others remain in 
unbelief. And that happened here under the  

preaching of Paul, and so he said when they 
did not agree among themselves… “They departed  

after Paul had said this one word.” Listen to 
what he said, “The Holy spirit spoke rightly.” 

Now the apostle Paul was not given to redundancies 
or to a waste of breath whenever he lectured or  

preached, but if there ever was a redundancy in 
the apostolic proclamation it’s here when he said  

that the Holy Spirit spoke rightly. Ladies and 
gentlemen, when the Holy Spirit speaks he always  

speaks rightly. The Holy Spirit doesn’t know 
how to speak wrongly. But Paul, in a sense,  

is rubbing their noses in it here. They believe 
that the Scriptures of the Old Testament were  

inspired by the Holy Ghost, and now when they 
leave in unbelief Paul looks at them and he said,  

Well, the Holy Spirit was right, wasn’t he. He 
spoke exactly as to what you people would do.  

And he spoke rightly through Isaiah the prophet.
And now he refers back to the sixth chapter of  

the book of Isaiah. You know the story. That’s 
the occasion where Isaiah sees the Lord high  

and lifted up and the seraphim singing the 
Trisagion Holy, Holy, Holy. And after Isaiah  

is driven to his knees and pronounces a curse 
upon himself, he hears the elevated God saying,  

“Who will go for us? Whom shall I send?” And 
Isaiah on his face lips trembling, blistered  

from the hot coal, said, “Here am I. Send me.”
You see the parallel between the experience that  

Isaiah had in the temple and the apostle Paul 
had on the road to Damascus when he heard the  

voice of the elevated Christ telling him to 
go? Paul reminds them of the call of Isaiah.  

And after Isaiah volunteered for the mission, God 
said to him, Go, Isaiah. You go and you speak. But  

I’m going to make the heart of these people 
fat. I’m going to make their ears deaf. I’m  

going to make their eyes blind, lest they see 
and understand, lest they hear and embrace and  

become converted. Your mission, Isaiah, if you 
choose to accept it, is a mission impossible,  

because you’re going to be preaching to people 
who are spiritually dead, who don’t want to hear  

your message. And your message will not be good 
news; it will be bad news. When Isaiah heard that,  

what did he say? “O Lord, how long? How 
long am I going to have to preach to people  

that don’t want to hear it?” And God said, “Until 
the cities of the nation are desolate. Yet in the  

end,” he said to Isaiah, “I have reserved for 
myself a tenth who will hear by God’s grace.” 

And we’re coming to the end of the whole record of 
the apostolic testimony of the first century. Have  

you heard it? Have you seen it? Or have you become 
calloused to the Word of God? Have you heard it  

so often you don’t want to hear it anymore? So you 
turn a deaf ear to the Word of God. You don’t want  

to see it. You know, it’s impossible to hear the 
Word of God and remain neutral. It’s impossible  

to hear the Word of God and to be unchanged. 
Your either moved in the direction of your  

sanctification when you hear the Word of God, or 
you move in the direction of the hardness of your  

heart. And that’s what Paul said to these people 
who were familiar with the Scriptures, who knew  

the Scriptures, who had listened to the greatest 
expositor of the Scripture that was on the planet  

that Isaiah was telling the truth, because 
the Holy Spirit through Isaiah spoke rightly. 

You say it doesn’t seem fair. Why would God close 
the ears of people, shut the eyes of people and  

then punish them for not hearing and punish them 
for not seeing? Because the closing of the ears  

and the closing of the eyes are God’s judgments 
upon people who in the first place didn’t want to  

hear and didn’t want to see. That’s the way he 
operates, doesn’t he? In his judgment he said,  

“Let him who is evil, let the one who is wicked be 
wicked still.” If you heard the Word of God again  

and again and again and it still (follows? 
falls?) upon a stony, recalcitrant heart,  

God may give you over to that heart forever. 
That’s the warning that Paul is giving these  

people who have heard the truth of God.
And he said, So God is going to close the  

book on you, and he’s going to take this message 
to the Gentiles. And then he said to them “and  

they will hear it.” That’s the only reason we’re 
here this morning is because these people in the  

first century refused to hear the gospel, so God 
grafts us onto this tree as a wild olive tree,  

and where sin abounded grace even more abounded.
Then at the close of the chapter we read these  

words: “Then Paul dwelt two whole years in his own 
rented house, and received all who came to him,  

preaching the kingdom of God, and teaching 
the things that concern the Lord Jesus Christ,  

with all confidence, no one forbidding him.”
I don’t know what happens next in your Bible,  

but mine has a period there. And I think 
What happened to Luke? Did he have a heart  

attack in the middle of the chapter? If 
I paid money for a novel and I read the  

novel all the way through and I get to the end 
and I don’t find out what happens to the hero,  

I’m going to write the publishing company and 
way What’s the matter with you? You left off the  

last chapter. Here we are following the apostle 
through all the world. We get to Rome, not a word  

of his trial. What happened? Tune in next week.
Now let me give you a little bit of an answer  

to that. Luke ends his book by saying that 
Paul was in house arrest there for two years,  

and he ends up his book presumably in the year 
60 AD. Now there’s abundant references from early  

church history that what actually happened after 
this time is that Paul did come before Caesar,  

he did have his trial, and he was exonerated, and 
he was set free, and he resumed his missionary  

travels and went as far as Spain establishing 
churches, established a church on Crete and  

continued freely in his apostolic ministry for 
two or three years. In the year 64 AD in July  

1,941 years ago a disaster, a calamity took place 
in Rome when the city was virtually burned to the  

ground, and of course the legend has it that 
the chair of first violin in Rome at that time  

was Nero. And the story is that Nero himself in 
his madness set fire to Rome and fiddled while  

the city was in flames. But at the end of this 
disaster somebody had to be blamed, and certainly  

Nero was not willing to step forward. And so he 
publicly accused Christians for setting the fire  

that all but destroyed the city of Rome in 64 AD.
You remember at the beginning of his tenure Nero  

was known for his justice and his fairness and his 
good administration, and then somewhere along the  

line he went nuts and became the beast of the 
ancient world. Well, here we see Nero in 64 as  

the beast. The common view of the folks of Rome 
by this time was not hostile toward the Christian  

community, as we’re told by Tacitus the Roman 
historian, but Nero had this personal hatred for  

Christianity. And we’re told that in the year 65 
both apostles Peter and Paul were martyred under  

the cruelty of Nero Caesar. They died in different 
ways. Peter was crucified, and as the tradition is  

he asked not to be crucified right side up 
because he didn’t feel worthy to die in exactly  

the same manner that his Savior had died, and so, 
according to tradition, Peter was crucified upside  

down. Paul, unlike Peter, was a Roman citizen, 
and Roman citizens convicted of capital crimes  

were not executed by crucifixion. They were 
beheaded with the sword. And the tradition is  

that under Nero in the year 65 AD Paul was 
once again a prisoner, once again in jail,  

and he was taken out, publicly executed by a guard 
who took his sword and cut his head from his body. 

Now what went on in the few days and few weeks 
before his last moments on this earth are not told  

to us in the book of Acts, but we can certainly 
learn much from his last letter that he wrote  

to his faithful disciple Timothy. And we’ll 
look at those last words of the apostle Paul,  

God willing, next week as he is in the Mamertine 
Prison awaiting his execution. But for now the  

Book of Acts ends with the apostle free and 
the gospel continuing to spread throughout  

the known world.
Let us pray. 

Father, we pray that our study of this history 
would not fall on deaf ears or on blind eyes,  

but that Spirit who always speaks rightly 
will take the message of Acts into our souls  

that it may bring forth its fruit. 
For we ask it in Jesus’ name. Amen.
