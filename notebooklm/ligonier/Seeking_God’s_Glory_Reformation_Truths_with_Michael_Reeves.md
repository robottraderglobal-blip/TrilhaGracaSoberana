# Seeking God’s Glory: Reformation Truths with Michael Reeves

REEVES: Welcome back.

Now, some hundred and twenty years after the
Reformation got going, some hundred and twenty

scholars assembled in Westminster in what
is now within Greater London in England, to

write the necessary documents for a Reformed
church in England.

And the first question and answer of the Westminster
Shorter Catechism is a beautiful prize flower

of Reformation thought.

Question, "What is the chief end of man?"

Answer, "Man's chief end is to glorify God
and to enjoy Him forever."

The glory of God and enjoyment of Him, these
inseparable twin truths were guiding lights

for the Reformation.

The Reformers held that through all the doctrines
that they had fought for and upheld, God was

glorified and the people were given comfort
and joy.

Through justification by grace alone, through
faith alone in Christ, God was glorified as

utterly merciful and good.

And therefore, people knew through this doctrine
of justification they could have a firm standing

before God, call Him "Abba," confident He
could save and keep them to the uttermost.

Without a priestly hierarchy detaching them
from the world, believers could call each

other "brother" and "sister," live every part
of their life for the glory of God.

Now, a good test of the role of the glory
of God can be seen in how differently Roman

Catholic and Reformation theologies thought
of our assurance of salvation.

Can a believer know that she is saved?

Well, on the side of the Reformation, the
Puritan Richard Sibbes argued this.

He said, "There be many duties and dispositions
that God requires, which we cannot be in without

assurance of salvation on good grounds."

What's that?

"God bids us to be thankful in all things.

Well, how can I be unless I know God is mine
and Christ is mine?

God enjoineth us also to rejoice."

"Rejoice," Paul says in Philippians 4, "Rejoice,
and again I say rejoice."

And Sibbes comments, "Can a man rejoice that
his name is written in heaven without knowing

that it is written there?

Alas, how can I perform cheerful service to
God if I doubt He is my God and Father?

God requires a disposition in us that we be
full of encouragements, strong in the Lord,

that we be courageous for His cause in withstanding
His enemies and our enemies.

But how can there be courage in resisting
our corruptions, Satan's temptations?

How can there be courage in suffering persecutions
and crosses in the world if we do not have

some particular interest?

Know that we have a part in Christ and in
God."

Well, we've already seen that the confidence
Sibbes upheld as a Christian privilege was

damned by medieval Roman Catholic theology
as the sin of presumption.

Remember what the judges said about Joan of
Arc, "This woman sins in that she says she's

certain of being received into paradise."

By the whole line of Roman Catholic theology,
you could only have as much confidence in

heaven as you could have confidence in your
own ability, your own performance.

But while all that theology made sense in
Roman Catholicism, that you cannot have assurance,

that would be presumptuous.

While that made sense, how do you think it
made people feel?

Well, it bred fear not joy.

The need to have personal merit before God
left people terrified of the thought of judgment.

And it was exactly why the young Luther shook
with fear at the thought of death, why he

said he hated God, instead of enjoying Him.

Luther could not be thankful, cheerful, rejoicing,
strong in faith for things Sibbes said we

should be, because Luther before the Reformation
believed only in God as a judge who was against

him.

But with his discovery that sinners are declared
righteous in Christ, that all changed.

No longer was his confidence for that last
day placed in himself.

It all rested on Christ and His sufficient
righteousness.

And so for Luther, the horrifying doomsday
became what he would call "the most happy

last day," the day of Jesus, his friend.

And the consolation such assurance brought
to those who held to reformational theology

was captured perfectly in the striking wording
of the Heidelberg Catechism's question and

answer.

Question (I love how this is phrased) "What
comfort is it to you that Christ will come

to judge the living and the dead?"

Not how terrifying, but what comfort is it
to you?

Answer, "In all my sorrow and persecution,
I lift my head and eagerly await as judge

from heaven the very same person who submitted
Himself to the judgment of God for my sake

and has removed all the curse from me."

You see the comfort in that assurance?

For even the struggling believer that was
the applied theology of the Reformation.

Let's push it into a particular a little bit,
"what happens to us after death" was no sideshow

issue for the Reformation.

Luther's very first skirmish that October
day in 1517, when he nailed his ninety-five

theses to the church door, concerned purgatory,
what happens after death?

Now purgatory was the place in Roman Catholic
theology where Christian souls go after death

to have their sins slowly purged from them
so they can be fit for heaven.

So through time in purgatory, sinners would
be purified and made finally fit for heaven.

Now, the doctrine of purgatory got into full
swing by the late Middle Ages, and fear of

the place began to spawn a large purgatory
industry.

So, prayers and masses would be said for souls
in purgatory to help speed them on their way,

and chantries were founded; you had to be
rich to have one of these.

Priests would be paid for, dedicated to say
prayers and masses for wealthy souls in purgatory.

And so the wealthy can speed their way through
purgatory.

And then, of course, there were indulgences,
awards of merit handed out by the church to

those who'd earned or bought them.

And those indulgences could top up an individual's
personal merit before God, so fast tracking

you through purgatory, or if you got a plenary
or full indulgence, you could leapfrog purgatory

altogether.

It was an indulgence monger, Johann Tetzel,
who had actually stunned Luther into action

with his blood-chilling religious marketing.

He traveled with a traveling musical quartet,
and in one of his lurid sermons he said, "Listen,

can you hear the screams, the cries of your
parents and grandparents?

They're in purgatory.

They cry to you now for mercy.

Can you not hear them?

'You could relieve us with just a little money,'
for when the coin in the coffer rings, the

soul from purgatory springs."

And none of this has really disappeared from
modern Roman Catholicism.

The catechism of the Catholic Church still
affirms belief in purgatory and indulgences.

Indeed, when Pope Benedict XVI wrote about
the last things, in his book he gave more

pages to considering purgatory than to heaven
and hell combined.

And why not?

When justification is thought of as a process
of growth in righteousness, as it is in Roman

Catholicism, purgatory and indulgences make
sense.

Without the righteousness of Christ given
to us, how can anyone be righteous enough

for heaven, unless they have much more time
to get themselves ready than this short life

affords?

But, and here's the point about how it connects
with the glory of God.

To the Reformers, purgatory quickly came to
symbolize all that was wrong with the Roman

Catholic view of salvation.

And I want you to listen to how Calvin here,
as he writes against purgatory, how the glory

of God is on his mind.

He writes, "Purgatory is a deadly fiction
of Satan, which nullifies the cross of Christ,

inflicts unbearable contempt upon God's mercy,
and overturns and destroys our faith."

For what does this purgatory of theirs mean,
but that satisfaction for sins is paid by

the souls of the dead after their death?"

That they're atoning for their own sins in
purgatory, Christ's cross hasn't done it all.

"But," said Calvin, "the blood of Christ is
the sole satisfaction for the sins of believers,

the sole expiation, the sole purgation.

What remains but to say that purgatory is
simply a dreadful blasphemy against Christ!"

Do you see his logic?

His logic is simple, purgatory strips Christ
of His glory as a merciful and fully sufficient

Savior.

It also destroys any confident joy in us.

No joy, no glory.

It went entirely against the grain of Reformation
thought, which cared so passionately about

those two twin prizes.

And yet Protestants have always unanimously
been averse to the idea of purgatory since

the earliest days of the Reformation.

But things are changing.

One of the darlings of modern evangelicalism,
C.S. Lewis, was as winsome as ever when he

turned his pen in support of some form of
purgatory.

Now Lewis believed he had a way to think of
purgatory that didn't fall foul of the problems

that Calvin had seen.

And the way he saw it was that purgatory could
be thought of not as a place to pay off any

remaining debt that the blood of Christ had
failed to cover.

"No," said Lewis, "we can think of it really
as a place where those who are forgiven might

go on to be fully holy and fit for heaven."

In other words, purgatory should be seen not
as a place of punishment but as a school,

where there a taste for holiness is cultivated,
such that graduates might fully enjoy heaven

instead of feeling out of place.

So there in purgatory, Christians, they're
not going to get more forgiven, but they will

get, here's what it's really about, they'll
get acclimatized to the holy atmosphere of

heaven.

It's sort of to prevent heavenly culture shock.

And to illustrate, Lewis turned to Cardinal
John Henry Newman's poem The Dream of Gerontius,

which is the account of a soul's journey from
death to judgment and then purgatory.

And near the end, the soul approaches the
throne of God.

And in order to appreciate the pathos of the
moment, you should listen to Edward Elgar's

musical rendition of The Dream of Gerontius,
and at that moment the full orchestra blares

out the terrifying holiness of God, and in
pitiful strains the soul cries out to be sent

away to purgatory because the soul is unable
to bear the dazzling brightness of God's presence.

And the soul cries these words before the
blazing holiness of God, the soul conscious

of its dirtiness cries, "Take me away and
in the lowest deep there let me be, there

motionless and happy in my pain, lone and
not forlorn, there will I sing my sad perpetual

strain, there will I sing my absent Lord and
love.

Take me away."

Now, Lewis might have sidestepped Calvin's
direct volley, but there remains something

there that is entirely incompatible with Reformation
thought.

Yes, purgatory is not now meant to finish
off the work of the cross in securing our

atonement in this model.

The problem is to do with some of those other
basic questions that we've seen raised by

the Reformation.

Questions like, "What is it that God gives
us?

Does He give of Himself or something called
grace?

What is our new life?

Is it knowing Him or being enabled by Him
for something else?"

And so, here's the problem with what Lewis
has come up with.

Here in The Dream of Gerontius, the soul thinks
holiness and transformation will best happen

away from the presence of God.

There the soul says, "lone, absent from the
Lord," the soul believes it will best mature.

Apparently, absence makes the heart grow fonder,
even in eternity.

But the soul's logic is at complete odds with
everything we've seen.

That we find our joy and we find ourselves
transformed through our communion with God,

by glorying in Him.

Our sanctification is not something God ever
enables from a distance with hands-off.

We find ourselves, 2 Corinthians 3:18, transformed
into his image precisely as we contemplate

the Lord's glory.

It's in communion with Him, there.

So that finally then when He appears, 1 John
3:2, "When he appears we shall be like Him,

for we shall see Him as He is."

Growth in godliness happens in communion with
Him, not away from Him in this idea of purgatory.

Also the overwhelming tone of what that soul
had cried is an aching, sad, perpetual strain,

and that is where any purgatory must leave
it.

Belief in purgatory brings sadness and discomfort.

Reformation thought, on the other hand, always
sees joy found in and contemplating in the

glory of God.

True happiness is found in pressing into,
not away from, the bright glory that purifies

and heals.

And that, friends, is the litmus test of truly
reformational thought.

Does it glorify us and our abilities and our
performance, or does it glorify Him?

And what the Reformers saw, especially through
the message of justification by faith alone,

was the revelation of an exuberant, superfluously
gracious and happy God who glories in sharing

His happiness, not stingy or utilitarian,
a God who glories in being gracious.

And to steal from His glory by claiming any
credit for ourselves would actually be to

steal our own joy in so marvelous a God.

In fact, wrote John Calvin, "that is the secret
of happiness and the secret of life."

He said, "Whatever the philosophers have said
of the chief good is cold and vain, for they

confine man to himself.

But it is necessary for us to go out of ourselves
to find happiness.

The chief good of man is nothing else but
union with God."

That goes against everything we're taught
in our culture today.

That happiness is not found in ourselves,
it is not found in appreciating our own beauty

or convincing ourselves of it.

Deep, lasting, satisfying happiness is found
in the all-glorious God, all of which is really

just another way of saying, "What is the chief
end of man?"

"The chief end of man is to glorify God and
enjoy Him forever."

The fact is, we've been made to enjoy God.

But without the great truths that the Reformers
fought for, which display God as glorious

and enjoyable, we will not do so, we will
not enjoy Him.

Seeing less of this glorious God, we will
be lesser and sadder.

Seeing more of Him, more of His glory, greater
clarity on the glory of His ways and who He

is, then we will be fuller and happier.

Thank you.
