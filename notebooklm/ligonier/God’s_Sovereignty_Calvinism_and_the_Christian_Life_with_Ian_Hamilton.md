# God’s Sovereignty: Calvinism and the Christian Life with Ian Hamilton

HAMILTON: Welcome to the third
of our six lectures

on Calvinism and
the Christian life.

Thus far, we have
seen that Calvin,

at least in his own estimation,
did not devise Calvinism.

He was simply, he believed, if
coherently and systematically,

drawing together the strands
of God's revelation in Holy

Scripture, supremely concerning
the revelation of His grace

and salvation in his
Son, Jesus Christ.

And having highlighted
some of the theological,

spiritual pulse beats that run
through Calvinism like melodies

-- recurring melodies, I would
now like to try and answer

the question, but what does
Calvinism look like in the life

of a Christian and in the
life of a Christian church?

As we noted in
the first lecture,

Calvinism is not a
theological theory.

It's not a religious philosophy
that compels the mind

but leaves the shape of your
life untouched and unmoved.

Biblical truth
reforms and restyles

the believer's life into
the likeness of that

of our Savior Jesus Christ.

And the great ministry
of the Holy Spirit

is to replicate in
the people of God

what he first etched in
the life of the Son of God

in his humanity while on earth.

And so for Calvin,
the great mission

of a pastor, a
scholar, a theologian

was to bring to
the people of God

those great truths that
are to shape and style

and conform our life to the
likeness of our Savior Jesus

Christ.

Where Calvinism, as
it's called, truly

takes root in a
person's life, it

transforms the life
of that person.

So what are the marks -- the
visible marks that will define

the life of any man or woman
who claims to live under

the lordship of Christ and
the sovereignty of God?

First, we need to say this
-- that Calvinism honors

God's unconditional sovereignty.

Now, Calvin is well aware that
there are questions that cannot

be answered concerning
God's sovereignty.

"Who has known the
mind of the Lord

and who has been his counselor?"

There is a holy
reserve in Calvin,

actually, when he comes to
deal with the issue of God's

sovereignty in the Institutes
of the Christian Religion,

his great magnum opus.

Calvin is very well aware that
there are issues beyond which

we cannot go. "The secret
things belong to the Lord.

The things revealed," --
Deuteronomy 29:29 --

"belong to us and our children."

But that did not mean
for Calvin that we

were to hide from the
manifest biblical truth

that God is
unconditionally sovereign,

that from Him and
through Him and to Him

are all things; to Him and
to Him alone be the glory.

He is the God --
Ephesians 1, verse 11 --

who has ordained all things
according to the counsel of his

own will.

And you will know that God's
sovereignty is never seen

in Scripture as an excuse for
believers to become passive.

God's sovereignty does not
suspend human responsibility,

but rather embraces it.

But how will this manifest
itself in our lives?

How is a conviction regarding
the sovereignty of God

to manifest itself in our daily
life as individual Christians

and in our corporate life as
the church of Jesus Christ?

Chiefly, I believe,
in the people of God

giving themselves to consistent,
faithful, heartfelt prayer.

Nothing more honors God's
sovereignty than prayer.

Benjamin Warfield, the
great Princeton theologian,

once described Calvinism as
Christianity on its knees.

And I think that takes us to
the very heart of what Calvinism

is.

Anyone who claims to be a
Calvinist, and Calvin himself

would be very cross if any
Christian called themselves

by his name.

But if we call ourselves
Calvinists and do not

give ourselves committedly,
personally, congregationally

to prayer, we belie
our profession

in the sovereignty of God.

In that light, it's
striking and instructive

to notice in the
early chapters of Acts

that when we
encounter the church,

corporate prayer is at the
very heart of its life.

Acts chapter 1, verse 14 --
"all these with one accord were

devoting themselves to prayer.

Together with the women and
Mary, the mother of Jesus,

and his brothers."

Notice the strength of the verb.

They were all together
devoting themselves to prayer.

And the force of the
verb is very striking.

They gave themselves heart
and soul to corporate prayer.

You see the same thing in
Acts chapter 2 verse 42.

After the great crowd
are brought to salvation

in Jesus Christ
at Pentecost, Luke

tells us that they,
"devoted themselves

to the apostles' teaching
and the fellowship,

to the breaking of
bread and the prayers."

There were set times for prayer.

And these new converts
devoted themselves -- again,

the same word -- they heart
and soul committed themselves

to gathering with their
fellow believers in prayer.

And I've often wondered
whether the demise --

the near demise -- of the
church prayer meeting,

the church gathering corporately
together for prayer in recent

times is perhaps deeply
indicative of the spiritual

atrophy that innervates the
church's witness to the grace

and power of the gospel.

John Bunyan in Pilgrim's
Progress was surely absolutely

right when he wrote, "You can
do more than pray after you have

prayed, but you cannot do
more than pray until you have

prayed."

Matthew Henry said
something very similar.

"When God deigns to
bless His people,

He sets them a praying
for the blessing

which He desires to give them."

I remember as a young
student being deeply struck

by a sentence in a
sermon by my minister

then, George Philip in Glasgow.

I was a young Christian.

I remember nothing
about the sermon,

but I remember one sentence
that struck me deeply and has

remained with me ever since.

He said, "Prayer
is evangelism shorn

of all its carnal attractions."

Prayer is evangelism shorn of
all its carnal attractions.

Now let me be clear.

He was not decrying,
in any sense,

organized evangelistic
endeavors -- far from it.

But he was reminding us,
and certainly reminding me,

that evangelism that is
not rooted in and saturated

with prayer is actually a sham.

The sovereign God alone gives
the increase, and so we pray.

And prayer was one
of the hallmarks

of Calvin's ministry in Geneva.

People prayed, and God
blessed his church.

Confident, trustful
dependence on God

to fulfill all He has promised
is seen above all, is it

not, in a life of prayer.

It's only too easy,
I think -- not

least in the times
in which we live --

to take lower ground, to resort
to a myriad of unbiblical

expediences in the
work of the gospel.

I think we see it
in worship today.

I think we see it in evangelism
where the church, at times,

seems far more influenced by the
principles of a fallen culture

than by the precepts
of God's living word.

When you read these
early chapters in Acts,

it's remarkably striking
the significance that prayer

corporate -- congregational,
we might say --

prayer had in the
life of the church,

and surely Luke is signaling to
us that this is what God looks

to see mark the
life of His people.

We honor God's sovereignty by
praying and by calling upon Him

and acknowledging to
Him, "Lord without you,

we can do nothing."

Calvinism honors
God's sovereignty.

We must understand that
the sovereignty of God

is not simply a doctrine
that we are to confess.

It is a lifestyle
that we are to live.

But secondly, Calvinism
cherishes God's grace.

It honors God's
sovereignty, because He

is the sovereign Lord and
salvation is wholly of Him.

But secondly, Calvinism
cherishes God's grace.

Now note the word "cherishes."

It's only too easy
for grace to become

a theological shibboleth.

But Calvinists cherish, delight
in, love the grace of God.

Thomas Goodwin, the remarkable
17th century English Puritan,

wrote eloquently about grace.

I remember some years
ago, almost stumbling

across this passage in
the writings of Thomas

Goodwin in the second volume of
his collected works, I think.

Listen to this.

He says, "Grace is more
than mercy and love.

It superadds to them.

It does not simply
denote love, but the love

of a sovereign,
transcendent superior,

one that may do what He
will, that may wholly choose

whether He will love us or not.

Now God, who is an infinite
sovereign, who might

have chosen whether
ever He would love us

or no, for Him to love
us, this is grace."

I asked in one of
the earlier lectures

whether we rightly
valued and appreciated

the forgiveness of sins.

Let me ask that
question in relation

to the grace of
God in the gospel.

Do we rightly appreciate, and
cherish, and value the grace

of God in Jesus Christ?

Grace is such a commonplace
among us, is it not?

I preach about it.

I write about it.

I converse about it.

But sadly, rarely
am I overwhelmed

by the wonder of it.

Grace is not simply a
Calvinistic feature.

It is the heartbeat and
glory of the gospel.

And when you read John Calvin
-- and Calvin actually is

remarkably readable.

I think many people are put off.

The very name Calvin
seems to suggest

erudition and profundity.

Well, there is erudition
and profundity,

but he writes such
beautiful, lucid prose

that he draws you into the
lucidity and the profundity.

And when you read
Calvin, you're aware,

here is a man for whom
grace is not simply

a theological denominator.

Here is someone who's actually
overwhelmed by the wonder

of grace.

I mentioned in an earlier
lecture that Calvin says

remarkably little
-- almost nothing --

about his conversion.

And you can spend many
interesting hours asking

the question, "Well, why?"

Why does he say almost two
lines in almost 60 volumes

of writings?

Why does he say so little
-- almost nothing --

about this conversion?

Well, you can give
many answers to that.

But the one that most appeals
to me -- and perhaps, I think,

the one that is most true
of all the answers --

is that it was enough for Calvin
to say this, "God subdued me."

God subdued me.

For Calvin, that was
the wonder of grace.

That said everything.

That was all he needed to say.

Inexplicable sovereign
grace had come into his life

when he was rebelling
against it and resisting it.

He says that he had
been sunk in ignorance.

He had no fondness for the
doctrine of the gospel.

But God, in His undeserved
kindness and love,

broke into his life.

And he would have identified
absolutely with Thomas Goodwin

when he said, "For Him to
love us, this is grace."

And what we need
to understand is

that grace isn't some kind
of heavenly substance.

Grace isn't God
reaching into a treasury

and dolloping blessings
upon His people.

When the Bible speaks of
grace, it speaks of grace

as the grace of God.

Grace is God acting
in love towards us.

Grace is God giving
Himself to us.

Jesus Christ is
the grace of God.

He is Himself the gospel of God.

I love that wonderful
account in Luke chapter

2 of Mary and Joseph
bringing the infant Jesus

into the temple.

And Simeon takes the infant
in his arms and says,

"Lord, let now Your servant
depart in peace for my eyes

have seen Your salvation."

Jesus Christ is the
salvation of God.

And we must be careful
when we think about

grace that we don't separate it
from the person of Christ, who

is Himself the
very grace of God.

Calvinists are jealous
to magnify the grace

of God, and for this reason,
because it opens to us

the heart of the God of grace.

For Him to love
us, this is grace.

And I think Calvinism has
often been given a bad press,

because people talk about
grace, grace, grace, grace

and not about Him, Him, Him.

It's a bit like talking about
justification without actually

preaching Jesus Christ
who justifies us or about

predestination, isolating it
from the God of infinite wisdom

and grace and love, who
ordains all things --

not only according to the
counsel of His own will,

but who ordains all things
wisely, and well, and perfectly.

"We must never separate
Christ from His benefits,"

said Phillip Melanchthon,
Luther's successor

and colleague.

For Him to love
us, this is grace.

And so when people say to me,
"Well, Ian, what exactly is

a Calvinist?" my instinctive
reaction is always to say,

"A lover of Jesus Christ."

They say, "Well, but
isn't that a Christian?"

I would say, "Well, what else
do you think a Calvinist is?"

For Him to love us.

And too often in
the church today, we

have conferences galore about
this, that, and the other.

I actually can't
think when I last

was at a conference that
wholly and exclusively

focused on the person, grace,
and glory of Jesus Christ.

And I say that as someone
who organizes conferences,

to my shame.

I hope to remedy it next
year, actually, both in the UK

and in the USA.

Calvinists are jealous to
magnify the grace of God,

because it opens to us the
heart of the God of grace.

And so when Paul
comes to the end

of his astonishing exposition
of the gospel of God's grace,

what does he do?

You don't find him
arguing or contending.

You find him worshiping.

Before it does anything else
-- and I don't mean we're never

to contend for the
grace of the gospel.

Of course, we are.

We are to "contend
for the faith,

once for all delivered
to the saints."

But for Paul, the gospel
of sovereign grace

brought him to his
knees in adoring wonder.

"Oh, the depth -- both of
the riches of the wisdom

and knowledge of God!

How unsearchable His judgments."

You almost can feel
the passion rippling

through the apostle's words.

They are impregnated with
his own sense of deep wonder

at the grace of
God in the gospel.

And so he launches
into doxology.

Calvinists are
doxological Christians.

We're not first and
foremost defensive.

We are doxological.

We praise and bless God for who
He is and for what He has done.

And so Paul finishes
that doxology, "to Him

be the glory forever."

And going back to the
Westminster Shorter Catechism

again, question 1, "What
is the chief end of man?

To glorify God and to
enjoy Him forever."

Now here's the thing.

And we'll finish with this.

When we glorify God, we discover
that's where our highest

joys are to be found.

When God is glorified, when
the Savior is magnified,

the heart of the believer
is most expanded.

I find that, as a husband, when
people speak well of my Joan,

there is nothing that
more delights me.

And I think, "And she's mine."

Calvinists magnify the
sovereignty of God,

and we glory in
the grace of God,

because from Him, and
through Him, and to Him,

are all things; to Him
be the glory forever.

In our fourth lecture,
we'll continue

to focus on those
marks and notes

that visibly shape the life
of Christians and churches

that call themselves Calvinist.
