# Love Your Enemies: Sermon on the Mount with Sinclair Ferguson

FERGUSON: Now we are coming to the sixth of
our studies in the Sermon on the Mount and

to the concluding words of Matthew’s Gospel
chapter 5.

Let me just remind you of where we are in
Jesus’ teaching.

He has taught the people that the kingdom
of God has come, He has demonstrated the power

of that kingdom in the miracles of restoration
that He has accomplished, and now He is teaching

His people how the gospel transforms the life
that kingdom people lead and live.

He has taught them something about their blessedness.

Interesting today, is it not, that you often
hear people use that term, “I am very blessed,”

and you want to go to them and say, “Are
you blessed because you are poor in spirit,

because God has taken hold of you in this
way?

Are you really just saying in a religious
way ‘I have been very lucky in my life’?”

No, Jesus is speaking about a deep down transformation,
so much so that His disciples live out a life

of righteousness that is much more profound
and much more impactful than that of the Pharisees.

It is actually possible to intimidate people
by your religious practices.

You have probably met some people who are
so intimidatingly religious that they are

off-putting, and Jesus is teaching a righteousness
that goes down so far that instead of producing

people who are intimidating, it produces people
who are meek, people who have this wonderful

sense of poise about their lives that they
are able to be generous, they are delivered

from the superglue of their own greed, and
so He is saying now, “God’s Law was always

meant to point us back to the beautiful life
of freedom and joy that God intended us to

have in the beginning.”

He is coming now to the last of these illustrations
where He has been contrasting the way in which

the Law was misinterpreted and misused with
the way in which we should use it properly.

He comes to another famous statement, “You
have heard that it was said, ‘You shall

love your neighbor and hate your enemy.’”

Now here is another point when it is pretty
clear here, is it not, that He is not speaking

about the Law that God gave but the way in
which that Law was misinterpreted and misapplied.

You love your neighbor, “Ah,” the people
said.

Well the logic is if you love your neighbor
then you what your enemy?

If you love your neighbor then you hate your
enemy.

That is logical is it not?

That is what God wants.

Well of course Jesus is pointing out that
indeed it is far from logical.

I had a philosophy professor, I was a philosophy
student when I was a youngster.

I had a philosophy professor who like all
other philosophy professors had terrible illustrations,

and his great illustration of the law of contradiction
was this, “Professor Wernham takes tea with

sugar is not the opposite of Mrs. Wernham
does not take tea with sugar.”

He was trying to teach us the difference between
something that is a real contradiction and

something that is a false contradiction.

And these teachers of old had come up with
a very false contradiction.

“Love your neighbor,” does not mean, “Hate
your enemy,” and Jesus is wanting to explore

therefore what does it mean for us actually
to love our neighbor?

Now it might be said, “Well, is it not really
biblical to love your neighbor and hate your

enemy?”

What about these prayers that we find in the
Psalms, the imprecatory prayers?

What about the actions of Elijah?

Well you will notice whenever these defenses
of the kingdom against the violent attacks

of the powers of darkness, there is always
the sense about them that the person who is

engaged in them is not acting in a private
capacity.

He is acting in a deep consciousness that
God has given him a position.

David has been given the position of the king,
and what is taking place is actually going

to destroy that kingdom.

It is going to destroy those people, and so
it is appropriate for him to resist evil and

to resist enemies.

But Jesus is saying you should not take these
unusual illustrations from the Old Testament

and universalize them and say, “Well David
said it, so I can say it,” because you are

not David, because you are not the king.

Actually come to think about it, you do not
even live in a monarchy.

This is not your role in society.

So Jesus is saying it was a huge mistake to
take that principle of resisting enemies,

which was a principle to be employed by the
king for the state, and then to apply it to

your private life and say, “If anyone is
my enemy, I will kick them in the teeth.”

So there is not antithesis here.

Love your neighbor, hate your enemy.

The truth of the matter is you need to discover
what Scripture says about the identity of

your neighbor.

There is a wonderful passage, isn’t there,
later on in the Gospels when a lawyer asked

Jesus the question, “What is the greatest
commandment?

Love God with all your heart and soul.

Love your neighbor as yourself.

Next question.”

He was a good lawyer, was he not?

Next question, I will get Him.

Next question, “Who is my neighbor?”

Do you remember the answer Jesus gives to
that question?

I mean not just the fact that He tells this
great story of the good Samarian, but really

the thing that I think must have made people
in the back of the crowd give one another

high fives at the end was the way Jesus turned
around and said to the lawyer, “Now tell

me Mr. Lawyer, which of these three men proved
to be the neighbor to the man who was in need?”

You see how He turned around?

The man was wanting to know, “How can I
limit my responsibilities?”

“What is the circumference within which
people are neighbors and outside of which

they are not neighbors?”

Jesus is saying, “No, the really significant
thing is you are the neighbor, and everyone

who is in need is the person to whom you are
to be neighbor.”

So rather than, “Love your neighbors,”
and therefore, “Hate your enemies,” Jesus

is saying, “Yes, love your neighbors and
be neighbor even to those who turn out to

be your enemies.”

Now if we are to understand this aspect of
living out the Christian life, theologians

often say to us that there are three principles
that help us in the matter of practical Christian

living, or the Christian ethic.

One is this, what is the goal that is involved?

The second is, what is the motive that is
involved?

The third is, what is the standard that is
involved?

You will see, I think, all three of these
in Jesus’ teaching here.

The goal is to love our neighbor to such an
extent that we love even those who have become

our enemies.

Jesus is able to say this because He teaches
us that love is not something that overwhelms

us and controls us.

Love is a disposition of the Christian which
the Christian exercises towards others.

You know how we sometimes use this expression,
“I fell in love.”

Why do you need fall in love, what do we mean
by saying, “I fell in love”?

We mean that something overwhelmed us, don’t
we?

We were mastered.

Our hearts were captured.

That is love at the level of romantic experience,
but the Bible’s understanding of love is

not dominated by the sense of “I am overwhelmed
by the loveliness of this object,” but “I

am determined because Christ has given Himself
for me that I will give myself for and to

others.”

Now, here is the million-dollar question,
how do you do that when the person who is

in view really is your enemy?

How do you love your enemy?

Do you see the point I am making?

You know it is one thing to be told to love
your enemy.

Just imagine we were sitting here in a Bible
study group and you are the leader and you

say, “Now what is that Jesus is teaching
here?”

and Mrs. Jones says, “Oh, He is teaching
us here to love our enemies.

We must love our enemies.”

We are all sitting nodding our heads, “Yes,
that is wonderful.

We must all love our enemies.”

And then as the leader you say, “How do
you do that?”

“How do you do that?”

You know what happens in the group.

Either the very verbal person will give you
all the answers, or every eye will turn back

to the Bible study leader and say, “Well,
you tell us how we do it.

You tell us how we do that.

We have not read that book.”

But Jesus tells us.

Do you notice how He does it?

He does not say, “Now pay attention.

I am about to announce to you how you do it.”

But He is telling us how we do it, “I say
to you love your enemies and pray for those

who persecute you.”

Notice that this is a radical difference from,
“Get your own back on those who persecute

you, hate your enemies,” but it is not just
an exhortation to pray, you know, so I must

pray.

It is that the Lord Jesus understands.

The moment you begin to pray for somebody
who is your enemy, you can no longer treat

them the way you naturally would have treated
them.

It does several things.

For one thing, it removes you from a situation
in which you are trying to defend yourself

against your enemy into a situation which
you are actually, you have become the active

one in relationship to your enemy.

You know, many enemies are intimidators are
not they?

They want to do you down, make you feel small.

They are in control, but you know the truth
of the matter is once you begin to pray for

them, you become increasingly conscious they
are not in control.

The Lord is in control, and as I begin to
pray for them in a sense they are huge in

front of me, but I am looking to one who is,
if I can invent the word, even huger, you

see?

They are dwarfed, and I begin to see them
in their true colors.

They are but a creature of Almighty God, they
are impoverished in life because they are

full of anger and hatred, and as soon as I
begin to turn to the Lord about them and pray

for them and ask the Lord to intervene in
their lives and in their situations and instead

of that native instinct to get our own back,
we begin to see them in a different light

altogether.

We begin to pity their situation because,
you see, the tragedy is they do not really

understand themselves.

They think they are big when actually they
are small.

I remember visiting very a high-end part of
a particular city in the United States where

I was preaching for a weekend, and it happened
to be the weekend, I think it was a Reformation

conference and happened to be of course the
weekend of Halloween as well.

Outside one of these very substantial, large
houses, this man who I was told, also flew

in snow for Christmas Day, had this huge kind
of giant King Kong, the size of his house

out there.

You know, wow!

He is really something.

And as I was driven to church on the Sunday
morning and looked over to the man’s house,

I realized somebody had come along with an
air pistol and shot King Kong and King Kong

was, you know, he was just slumped in front
of the man’s house.

Prayer is that air pistol when it comes to
our enemies, not they may necessarily realize

what has happened but they do begin to realize
that we are not as small as we seemed because

we have a great God, and they are not as big
as they seemed.

What Jesus is driving at here is that we are
turned outwards from ourselves.

You see, our instinct when somebody becomes
our enemy is self-defense, is it not?

It is to withdraw, not to open our hearts.

But now when we begin to pray, we have looked
up to the Lord, we have seen their need, and

the wonderful thing is we begin to focus more
on their needs than on our weakness.

Is this not what Jesus did on the cross?

Here, He is surrounded by His enemies.

He does this Himself.

They seem so strong.

He seems so weak and frail.

“Father, forgive them because they do not
actually realize what they are doing.”

So first of all Jesus gives us the goal, love
for neighbor, extends even to our enemies.

This so radically different from thinking
that “Love your neighbor” means “Hate

your enemy.”

And then He gives us the motive.

What is the motive?

“Love your enemies and pray for those who
persecute you, so that you may be sons of

your Father who is in heaven.”

Now in a sense when we read that in the light
of the whole Sermon on the Mount, we need

to say, “Just hold that thought for a moment
because we are going hear a great deal more

about this.”

So while Jesus is dealing with this issue
of loving even those who are our enemies,

He is giving us just a little, He is opening
the door to what is going to be the big emphasis

in chapter 6 that we understand we are not
only blessed, but we understand that we are

actually children of the heavenly Father.

Why does that make a difference?

Well He tells us, you pray for those who persecute
you because you are sons of a heavenly Father,

and that heavenly Father makes His sun rise
on the evil and the good, and sends rain on

the just and the unjust.

And what does it amount to if you love those
who love you?

It was when we were still His enemies that
God showed His love for us in giving His Son

for us, and if at the end of the day we think
our neighbors are just the people we like

and therefore are to love, we are no different
from the tax collectors.

We are no different from the people we despise.

They like each other.

There is honor among thieves.

No, He says, here is the wonderful motive
the gospel gives to you.

You are a child of a heavenly Father who is
generous to His enemies.

Remember what family you are in.

A friend told me years ago of an incident
that took place in Buckingham Palace where

one of the royal chaplains just happened to
be in the palace when the present Queen Elizabeth

and her late sister were just small girls.

They were dressed up in party frocks, and
they were being put in to the royal limousine

to be driven away to a party.

The queen’s mother, the late queen mother
was standing besides the rather grand limo

waving the royal finger at the royal children.

The chaplain was nosy enough to stop and listen
to what the royal voice was saying behind

the royal finger, and the royal voice was
saying, “Now remember girls, royal children,

royal manners.”

Royal children, royal manners.

Is not that what Jesus is saying?

You know, when you realize who you are as
a child of the heavenly Father, you have all

the motivation in the world to ask the Lord
to help you to be like Himself with respect

to your enemies.

So do you notice now what is happening?

We are beginning to see our enemies for who
they really are, they are desperately needy

people.

And we are beginning to see ourselves for
who we really are, we are royal children and

we have this wonderfully generous heavenly
Father.

In that sense, this is the mystery of the
Christian life to those who do not experience

it.

We can afford to be generous.

We can afford to be super-generous to our
enemies and disarm them by that generosity

because we belong to the heavenly Father.

So there is a goal and there is a motive,
and do you notice there is also a standard?

The goal is to love our neighbors and that
includes our enemies.

The motive is to live as the kingdom children
that we are, and so what is the standard?

Well, the standard appears in verse 48, “You
therefore must be perfect, as your heavenly

Father is perfect.”

Now, the word that is translated “perfect”
here is a word that elsewhere in the New Testament

we would translate “mature.”

It almost means “grown up,” and we might
think of that is what He means.

He does not really means that we should be
perfect.

He just means that we should be grown up,
you know that we should be generous like grown

ups should be generous and not like children
always bickering over who owns what.

But the fact of the matter is that the heavenly
Father is perfect.

It is not just that He is mature, that He
is older than we are, and He has been around

the block.

It is that He is absolutely perfect, and He
shows that perfection in His generosity even

to His enemies.

Jesus is saying to us, “Actually, that is
the standard.”

A man who was a minister, the first minister
of the church that we belong to in Scotland,

Robert Murray M’Cheyne, once wrote in his
diary by way of prayer, “Make me as holy

as a saved sinner can be,” and I think of
that in contrast with that prayer that Augustine

wrote in his Confessions in an earlier part
of his life, “Lord, give me chastity but

not yet.”

“Give me chastity but not yet.”

And Jesus is saying that we need to understand
that as children of the heavenly Father, He

is our model.

He is our standard.

You know, if you are a dad, it is always with
mixed emotions that you hear somebody say,

you know, “I met your son, and he is just
like you.”

I think he does not look anything like me
at all.

What it means is because I happen to preach
often, you keep looking this face and you

have picked out things in his face and you
know where he got them.

Well, we are not yet perfect, but if we are
children of the heavenly Father then in our

love even for our enemies, people begin to
see, “Oh, he is so like the heavenly Father.”

May God help us to live that way for His glory.
