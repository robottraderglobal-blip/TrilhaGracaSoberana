# Elohim: God’s Plural Name - The God We Worship with R.C. Sproul

I want to look at one of the names of God that 
has become very, very controversial in Biblical  

studies. And it is one of the oldest, maybe 
even the oldest name that we have for God. It  

is the name Elohim. Well, the name Elohim is 
controversial because of something that jumps  

out at you right away if you are familiar at 
all with the Hebrew language. Does anybody know  

what it is that’s so troublesome about this 
name for God? It’s in the plural form. The  

generic name of God in all of the ancient Semitic 
peoples was the name El, even Ba-al or Baal is a  

derivative from that generic term for God among 
ancient people—the name El. And there’s even  

differences of opinion as to what this root name 
meant in antiquity. Some say that it comes from  

the Semitic root that means strong. Others say 
no, it’s from a root that means in front of,  

someone that is in an advanced position or 
in a leadership position, and even a third  

meaning is that El meant binding force, that 
which gives cohesive togetherness to a people.  

We’re not sure which, if any of those, is the 
correct original Semitic meaning of the term,  

but the major problem that we find with this name 
of God, as I say, is when the name El appears in  

the plural form. Why would the name of God 
appear in a plural form in a religion that  

is distinctively famous supposedly for being the 
original religion of genuine monotheism? I mean,  

at the heart of the Old Testament Judaism is the 
affirmation, as we find in the Shema, “The Lord,  

our God” is what?—“is one God. And Thou shalt 
love the Lord thy God with all thy heart and all  

they might,” and so on. And so if there’s anything 
that seems incongruous with monotheism it would be  

the appearance of the name of God in the plural 
form. So we’re left with the thorny question of  

how we are to understand that appearance of the 
plural form. Well there are different theories,  

and I’ll go over them briefly before I let my 
cat out of my bag as to where I stand on this  

question. In the 19th Century we saw the emergence 
of a new science, a new discipline within the  

broader field of theology called the science of 
comparative religions. As the world grew smaller  

through more advanced modes of transportation and 
cultures bumping into each other more and more,  

scholars becoming more and more aware of 
the literature of various world religions,  

there was this quest for the very essence of 
religion, the common denominator that could be  

found in Buddhism, in Hinduism, Islam, Judaism, 
Christianity, and so on. And so we saw this  

great activity and excitement about the 
comparison of world religions. And also in  

the 19th Century we saw the development in the 
philosophical world of the central importance  

of the concept of evolution. Now we think of 
that normally in terms of its impact in biology  

and anthropology. But it was not just Charles 
Darwin who worked with the concept of evolution,  

but the major philosophers of the 19th Century 
gave a philosophical understanding of world  

history that was basically evolutionary. Perhaps 
the giant thinker in that period was a man by  

the name of Hegel whose philosophy is very, very 
abstract and difficult for the layman to follow,  

even difficult for the professional to follow, and 
a reaction to Hegel’s evolutionary view of history  

was brought to us by somebody that everybody 
has heard of, and that’s by Karl Marx,  

who also gave an evolutionary view of world 
history. And we saw political theories emerge,  

economic theories emerge, all centering on 
the concept of evolution. Well this also made  

a tremendous impact in Biblical studies and 
in the realm of theology. Again, the working  

hypothesis was everything, everything—economics, 
political structures—everything in history has  

undergone this process of development that 
we call evolution, and the way that it moves  

is always from the simple to the complex, from 
the single cell amoeba or whatever to a highly  

complex organism like man. And we don’t see these 
theories saying that you start with the complex  

and then get to the simple. But everything moves 
from simplicity to multiplicity and complexity,  

not only biological organisms or political 
structures, but also religions. And the theory  

was this that the religious development of man 
is one that moves in a progressive activity from  

the very simplest forms of religion that 
we find in animism, which are still found  

in primitive peoples around the world today, 
and then goes through stages. The next stage  

is polytheism. The next stage is what is called 
henotheism, and the next stage is then monotheism.  

One of the reasons for the documentary hypothesis 
was, of course, that this working philosophical  

assumption was that the Old Testament must have 
followed the same pattern just as the religions  

of other nations. And so we couldn’t legitimately 
expect to find monotheism as early as Moses. So  

anything that would appear as monotheistically 
oriented in those ancient books must have been  

something that a later editor wrote back into 
their own history to make the history more  

credible. So don’t, I mean, there are literally 
arguments as well for the documentary hypothesis,  

but I urge you to remember that the basic theory 
rests upon the philosophical assumption of an  

evolutionary process in theology and religion as 
well as in all other forms of life. Well again the  

principle of animism that Joyce Welhausen, for 
example, found in the Old Testament is based on  

the idea as the word animism suggests, coming 
from the Latin which means anima, which means  

life or spirit, and the idea being that there are 
living spirits that inhabit … objects … impersonal  

objects like rocks and trees and crocodiles 
and so on. If you go to primitive tribes today,  

you’ll find these people worshiping totem poles 
or bee trees, as I mentioned the other night,  

because they believe that there are spirits that 
live or inhabit these things. Then Welhausen,  

for example, found animism present in the 
Old Testament patriarchal narratives where  

the angels of God meet Abraham by the Oaks of 
Mamre. Do you remember? The men of God appear  

to Abraham by the Oaks of Mamre, and Welhausen 
says see, what they’re really saying there is  

that Abraham was talking to the trees, and he was 
having an encounter with the god in the trees,  

the spirit in the trees. And Moses was talking 
to a god where?—in a bush. Animism—bingo. See, so  

now we have, we move from animism to polytheism. 
Now polytheism, animism is a form of polytheism,  

really. But where we talk about polytheism where 
the gods don’t dwell necessarily in the objects.  

They have their independent existence, but you 
have many, many gods for each nation. Like the  

Romans and the Greeks had their pantheon of gods 
and goddesses—Zeus and Hera and Juno and Athena  

and Minerva and Hestia and Vesta and so on. I had 
to get Vesta’s name in there. And so you had a god  

for fertility, a god for war, a god for wisdom, a 
god for the hunt, a god for the hearth and home.  

You had a god who had a specified job description 
for some important dimension of human life. And  

each god had sort of sphere sovereignty over his 
own area. Athena was the goddess over wisdom,  

and she took care of that, and Mars takes care of 
wars and so on. That’s polytheism. And then as a  

transition from polytheism to monotheism is an 
intermediate step called henotheism or sometimes  

monarchical polytheism. How many of you have never 
heard the term henotheism before? Most of you have  

not. Ok. Henotheism means literally one god, but 
the way it functions in religious language is the  

idea of a god who reigns supreme over all areas 
of life such as agriculture, war, marriage, and  

so on. You don’t have a god for each one of those 
dimensions of life, just one god over all of those  

things, but each god has a defined territory or 
people over which he reigns. In other words, it’s  

like having a king over a nation. Israel has its 
God, and the Philistines have Dagon, their god,  

and other people, the Canaanites have Baal, and 
the Babylonians have their god so though there are  

many gods, but only one god for each nation. But 
you still don’t have the concept of the Most High  

God, the Creator God whose dominion is over all of 
the universe. Of course, you find that concept how  

early in the Old Testament?—the first line. The 
very first line you have the idea of a Creator God  

who is in dominion over the whole creation. And 
see, and that was just more than the evolutionist  

could handle. They said that can’t be that early 
in ancient history, because all the patterns show  

in the rest of the religions something like this 
kind of a progression. And so Elohim, since it is  

in the plural form is shown as an example of early 
polytheism within the Jewish faith by the critics  

of the 19th Century. But there have other … been 
other explanations rendered. Some have said that  

this is simply a reference to the imperial plural 
where those who are kings or are in elevated  

positions of majesty and authority instead of 
saying I want you to do this, they say we want you  

to do that. The pope in Rome, for example, uses 
the imperial plural when he speaks of himself.  

And kings in antiquity frequently did this. It’s 
not the same thing, but it’s comparable to the use  

of the editorial “we” that we use in literature 
today. However, the best linguists of Semitic  

languages and of Old Testament history argue, and 
I think convincingly, that that particular usage  

of the imperial plural was not found among Semitic 
people, and was not found among the Jews. And so  

though it’s a pleasant hypothesis it has very 
little evidence in history or in language to back  

it up. Well, of course, the Evangelical favorite 
explanation of this is that here is a cryptic  

reference to what?—to the Trinity, and that God 
is revealing very early the plurality of His  

personal being. Well certainly the concept of the 
Trinity is compatible with the word Elohim and  

the name Elohim is compatible with the concept of 
the Trinity. But I, for one, would demur at this  

point. I don’t think that that was the purpose of 
the use of the term Elohim for the simple reason  

that we find in the Bible what we call progressive 
revelation generally speaking. Progressive  

revelation, now we have to be very careful here. 
Progressive revelation refers to an unfolding of  

God’s self-disclosure, where more and more and 
more and more information is given. I don’t mean  

progressive revelation in the sense that some 
use it when they suggest that later revelation,  

like the New Testament revelation, corrects or 
contradicts the Old Testament understanding of  

God. That would do violence to our concept of 
Scripture and to the truthfulness of any of God’s  

revelation. But it is a classic position within 
Christendom to understand that God doesn’t reveal  

everything that He reveals in sacred Scripture on 
the first page. There is a progressive unfolding  

through types and shadows and promises … become 
more and more clear as more information comes  

to us. In light of that progressive character of 
Biblical revelation it seems to me, and I’m giving  

a shorthand answer here at this point, it seems to 
me and to I would say most Old Testament scholars  

that it’s just very unlikely that smack dab in 
the second page of Scripture you have a revelation  

of the Trinity in this very hidden cryptic sense. 
Possible, yes, but there are better explanations,  

I think, for the use of the plural than 
to suggest an indication of Trinity, and  

that is that we do know that within the ancient 
Hebrew and Semitic peoples there was the use of  

what is sometimes called the abstract plural 
or elsewhere called the plural of intensity.  

The plural of intensity or the abstract plural 
calls attention not to polytheism or even to  

the notion of God as a composite being. Let me 
just, I know that’s a technical point, but let me  

just stick it in there for a minute. One of the 
things that the church has resisted forever in  

understanding the character of God is that when we 
look at the attributes of God, God is invisible,  

He’s immutable, He’s omnipotent, He’s this, He’s 
that, and we talk about those attributes of God;  

it’s not like God is a single being made up of a 
composition of several attributes added together,  

like holiness, justice, grace, mercy, 
immutability, so on and so on. And you take  

a little bit of immutability and you cement it on 
to a little bit of holiness, and cement that on.  

That would make God disunified. Our concept of God 
is that God is His attributes. God is altogether  

holy, and His holiness is an immutable holiness, 
and His immutability is a holy immutability. And  

His justice is a just holiness and a holy justice 
and a just immutability and an immutable justice.  

Am I making myself clear? Ok. So we don’t want 
to think of God as being made up of a bunch of  

parts tacked on to each other that make a whole. 
All right? But God is altogether holy, altogether  

immutable, all of those attributes describe in one 
dimension the sum of God. But at the same time as  

we don’t want to understand God as a composite 
being made up of the sum of His parts, and we  

want to understand that He is a being of unity, 
a unified being, yet God is not one dimensional.  

There is a totality of His inner self that is 
expressed Biblically here by holiness, here by  

justice, here by immutability, and over here by 
infinity, and so on, and eternality. So that God  

is not a one-sided, flat-sided being, but there 
is, as the Scriptures tell us a fullness to God.  

The oldest philosophical problem of metaphysics 
that just drove the ancient philosophers berserk,  

the fundamental question was this was what is the 
relationship of unity to diversity. The ancient  

philosophers said what’s it all about, Alfie? And 
when he said what is it all about? He was asking  

this fundamental question. How can I make sense 
of the whole of my experience as a human being?  

Because just as a human being just take me as a 
man. I have a biological dimension to my life. I  

have an economic dimension to my life. I have 
a psychological dimension, a sexual dimension,  

a vocational dimension, you know, a physical 
dimension. I have all these different aspects  

that are very much a part of who I am as a 
person. In fact, we even are preoccupied in  

the 20th Century to discover what it means to 
be a whole person. And we say in colloquial  

slang that we’re trying to get it all together. 
Right? Because we recognize as the existentialist  

does that there is a wide variety and diversity 
of dimensions that make up not just my personal  

existence but this whole universe. There are 
dogs, there are kangaroos, there are lilies,  

there are butterflies, there are turtles, there 
are trees, there are oceans, there are clouds,  

there are mountains, and I could go on all night. 
And the ancient philosopher said I see all these  

different things around me. Life is filled with 
diversity. Is there anything that makes sense out  

of all of these particulars? Is there a universal 
that brings unity out of all of this diversity?  

How can we have the many just dangling in space 
with no ultimate point of reference? And this is  

where modern man finds himself when he embraces 
what is called relativism or pluralism where he  

says well there’s all these different aspects and 
there’s all these different ideas, and there are  

all these different theories and all of that, but 
there is no absolute. There’s no ultimate point of  

reference that makes sense out of everything, 
because everything is ultimately—let me back  

up a minute—If you have diversity with no 
unity, the bottom line is this. C-h-a-o-s,  

chaos. Irrationality meaninglessness. Ok? And 
so the ancient philosopher was looking for that  

transcendent point of unity that would draw all 
the strands of life and experience together. What  

man was seeking was not just unity but a universe. 
What are the two words that come together in the  

word universe? Not one truth—unity and diversity. 
And so the very word says we believe that in all  

of this diversity there is some sense of unity. 
And the concept of God in the abstract form among  

the Greeks functioned in precisely that way. 
Aristotle had to have something; Plato had  

to have something that tied it all together, that 
orchestrated it all, that gave purpose and reason  

and order and harmony to all of the strands of 
the particulars of our life and of our experience.  

And here the Hebrews understood very early that 
the diversity of life and the unity of life finds  

it ultimate resting point in God Himself, who 
Himself is unity in diversity. And in practical  

terms the church is called to manifest that. 
The body of Christ is to be one in one Lord,  

one faith, one baptism, but can the eye say 
to the ear I have no need of you? You have  

all of the diverse gifts, the diverse offices, the 
diverse tasks, and the diverse personalities. And  

it’s a sad thing that when we lose this as modern 
man seems to have lost it, the push of history is  

always towards uniformitarianism. Nature abhors 
a vacuum. And politics abhor pluralism. The great  

tragedy of a place like Red China is that when you 
see pictures of millions of people wearing exactly  

the same clothes, you notice that? Where there 
is an enforced conformity and uniformity so that  

persons particular identities are diminished or 
destroyed. Not so in the church of Jesus Christ.  

There is unity in the body of Christ, but there is 
also diversity—not diversity of faith, pluralistic  

theology, no—one faith, one Lord, huh, one 
baptism, but different personalities, different  

gifts, different tasks, and so on. So that we find 
this rooted in the very nature of God Himself.
