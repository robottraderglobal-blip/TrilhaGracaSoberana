# Scripture Alone – Interpreting the Bible: God Alone with R.C. Sproul

SPROUL: We continue now with our study of the Solas
of the Reformation. In our last session we

looked at the historical development of the
concept of sola scriptura and I want to look

a little further today at its theological
significance. You see that throughout the

solas the accent is on the qualifier 'alone.'
The Roman Catholic Church believes that justification

is by faith but not by faith alone. They believe
it's by grace but not by grace alone; they

believe it's by Christ, because of Christ
but not through Christ alone and at this point

I need to say that the Roman Catholic Church
also affirms the authority of sacred Scripture,

teaching that the sacred Scriptures are indeed
inspired by God the Holy Spirit, even using

a very strong term that for the most part
is avoided in the history of defending the

source of inspiration, they use the term dictante
for dictating. I don't believe that when the

church used that term dictante or dictating
that they meant thereby to convey a full orbed

system of dictation as a theory to explain
how the Bible was inspired but I only point

attention to this -- point out that this word
is used in the fourth session of the Council

of Trent to show what a high view of sacred
Scripture that the church has affirmed consistently

through the ages. And so the dispute between
the Reformers and the Roman Catholic Church

in the sixteenth century was not about the
authority of Scripture or about the inspiration

of Scripture but it was about, in the first
instance, whether that authority was through

Scripture alone; and then that spilled over
into the question of how the Scripture is

to be interpreted and so we'll take a few
minutes to look at these things.

Let me begin by looking briefly at the opening
statements of the fourth session of the Roman

Catholic council of Trent which was an ecumenical
council called in the middle of the sixteenth

century, designed specifically to respond
to the so-called errors of the Protestant

movement. And the fourth session took place
in 1546 and it opened in April of 1546 and

here's what it says concerning the canonical
scriptures: "The Holy, ecumenical and general

council of Trent, lawfully assembled in the
Holy Ghost, the same three legates of the

Apostolic See presiding, keeps this constantly
in view, namely that the purity of the gospel

may be preserved in the church after the errors
have been removed. This gospel of old, promised

through the prophets in the holy Scripture,
our Lord Jesus Christ, the Son of God, promulgated

first with His own mouth and then commanded
it to be preached by His apostles to every

creature, as the source at once of all saving
truth and rules of conduct. It also clearly

perceives that these truths and rules are
contained in the written books and in the

unwritten traditions which received by the
apostles from the mouth of Christ Himself

or from the apostles themselves, the Holy
Ghost dictating, have come down to us transmitted

as it were from hand to hand." Now, this portion
of this fourth session is opened to some significant

scrutiny and has been the subject of great
debate because the question is this: does

the Council of Trent teach that there are
two sources of written or special revelation;

one being the Bible and the other source being
the tradition? Now, if you read what I just

read in the Latin, you would see that the
text of Trent says that the truths of the

gospel are contained in sacred Scripture et
the tradition, written and unwritten. So that

that -- what does the word et mean here -- in
English, it's translated by the -- I mean

the word's translated into English by the
word "and." Now, let me back up for a second;

suppose if somebody came to me and said, "Where
can I find the truth of Christian theology?"

I'd say, "Well, in the first instance you
would go to the sacred Scriptures but if you

want a short hand introduction to the truth,
let me suggest that you read the Westminster

Confession of Faith or look at the Apostle's
Creed or look at some other creedal statement

of church history that also contains the truth
of the Christian faith." Now, if I said that,

I would not thereby be indicating that these
confessions or creeds are at the same level

of authority as the primary source which is
the Bible. I'm just simply saying what's found

in the Bible can also be found in some of
these creeds. That doesn't make the creeds

adding anything to sacred Scripture. And so
the question is: what was in view here in

the Council of Trent? Was Trent trying to
say there are two distinct sources of divine

truth -- Scripture and tradition -- or only
one source that is then repeated in the tradition?

That's all the difference in the world. Now
the thing gets more fascinating because in

the twentieth century an Anglican scholar
was doing his Ph.D. dissertation, his name

was Palmer, and he was studying the archives
about the machinations that were going on

behind the scenes at the forth session of
the Council of Trent and he discovered something

amazing, and that is in the first draft of
the fourth session the text of Trent read

that the truths of God and so on are found
partly in the Scriptures and partly in tradition,

the Latin being simply partim. Now, that would
clearly indicate two distinct sources of revelation;

you get part of the truth from Scripture,
you get the rest of it from the tradition.

And when that draft was presented to the whole
council, two priests, one of their names was

Bonucci, the other one's name was Nachianti
-- don't ask me why I remember their names,

I wasn't there. But Bonucci and Nachianti
jointly lodged a protest against the wording

partim partim -- or partly in Scripture, partly
in tradition saying that formula compromised

and undermined the sufficiency of sacred Scripture.
In a word, Bonucci and Nachianti were fighting

for sola scriptura at the Council. Now then,
here's what happened -- war broke out, the

session was hastily dismissed, the language
was changed partim partim to et, and there's

nothing in the historical record that tells
us why the change was made. So, some scholars

believe that it was made to acquiesce to the
protest of Nachianti and Bonucci and that

the church then voted against two sources
of revelation and left it stated in the ambiguous

way that it is with the et. Others argue in
the Roman Catholic Church that the change

of wordage was simply stylistic, that it didn't
indicate any substantive change in the thinking

of the Council. And there's still a third
alternative and that is the alternative that

the Council was undecided and so intentionally
used what is called a studied ambiguity, where

they left the issue open for future consideration
as to exactly how tradition and Scripture

are to be related. However, there is one thing
about which there is no dispute: we look at

the text in the sixteenth century and we can
argue until doomsday whether the church intended

to teach two sources of revelation, but there's
no doubt that that's what the church subsequently

taught as recently as the twentieth century
in the forties in the Papal Encyclical Humani

Generis, which clearly, unambiguously teaches
two sources of revelation, Scripture and the

traditions. So, what that does when Protestants
are involved and engaged in discussions, theological

disputes, with Roman Catholic theologians
and we may say, "Well, where did you get this

idea of the bodily ascension of Mary into
Heaven? We can't find that in the New Testament."

Well, it doesn't have to be in the New Testament;
it can be rooted and grounded in the tradition

of the church. Which also has, you know, binding
authority upon the conscience but where it

gets even more complicated is in the role
that the church has in its magisterial authority

to interpret the Scriptures. A corollary to
sola scriptura in the Reformation was the

principle of private interpretation. Remember
at the Leipzig debate, Luther made the observation

that seemed outrageous at the time that the
simple peasant armed with one text of Scripture

has more authority than church councils or
Popes who have no Scripture to back them up.

What he was getting at here is that the simple
believer has the right before God to come

to Scripture on his own, privately and to
interpret the Bible. Now, I'm going to comment

church responded to that corollary to sola

the penalties prescribed by the law." Now,
again there's a little bit of a problem with

Well, we would certainly agree with that;

Private interpretation was not seen as a license

hear so often in the church today. I mentioned

I don't think that's what they mean; usually

Now, I know a seminary professor who gave

that the depth and the riches that are contained

its application is almost infinite. However,

there may be a thousand applications of one
text of Scripture but only one correct meaning

unless God speaks with a forked tongue or

Now, so again the church misses the Reformation

the right to distort, nobody has the right
to distort it. But it goes further than that;

it's not simply condemning private interpretation

is not judged by the Scripture, which is what

But there is certainly no dispute that Rome

church here today.

Now, opposed to the church being the supreme
interpreter of the Scripture, the Reformation

view was the view called analogia fide, or
the analogy of faith, which teaches that sacred

Scripture is its own interpreter; that you
interpret the Scripture by the Scripture.

If something is obscure in one place in Scripture,
you understand it in light of other portions

of Scripture where the same question is unambiguous.
And so you go to the author of Scripture,

the Holy Ghost, to the Scripture itself, not
through mystical apprehensions, you don't

get the right interpretation that way, but
you get it by searching the Scriptures and

interpreting the Scripture by the Scripture.
And that principle assumes the unity and the

coherency of sacred Scripture; that if it
is the word of God, what it teaches in one

place will not contradict what it teaches
in another place. And if you interpret a passage

in Scripture in such a way that brings it
into contradiction to another passage of Scripture,

that would indicate that you've misunderstood
at least one of those two Scriptures because

the Scripture cannot be broken, and you interpret
the Scripture by the Scripture, and the church

is supposed to do that as well as the individual.
