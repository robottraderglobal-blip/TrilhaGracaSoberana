# Stephen Nichols: Reforming Vocation

NICHOLS: I think one of the great doctrines of 
the Reformation is the doctrine of vocation. Of  

course, at the center, as we've been talking 
about, are sola fide, sola Scriptura, and the  

doctrine of God. But when we think in terms of 
implications, probably the doctrine of vocation,  

for our practical living, is a 
true gift of the Reformers to us.

Now, I am a professor by trade, so I'm going 
to give you a quiz. Get out your Scantrons  

and your number two pencils. It's a one-question 
quiz, and it's very simple. It's true or false.  

Don't you love those, true or false? You get 
the paralysis halfway through. True or false:  

"God only cares about what I do on Sunday."

That's how important this doctrine of 
vocation is. It is patently false that  

God is unconcerned with our work, with 
our relationships, and that He only cares  

about the spiritual and religiously formal 
aspects of our life. God cares about every  

square inch of our lives and every moment 
of every day of every year that He gives us.

And the Reformers rescued the doctrine, 
not only of Scripture's authority,  

not only of justification by faith alone, but 
the Reformers rescued the doctrine of vocation.

In the medieval world there was a sharp 
distinction between the spiritual and the  

secular. There were two entirely different worlds, 
and those who inhabited the spiritual world were  

a higher class. They were the priestly 
class, from the nuns and the monks all  

the way through the hierarchy to the Pope. 
They had a calling. Everyone else worked.

"Labor" comes right from the Latin labora. Do you 
know what's in the middle of that word? "Boring."  

Now, that may not be the etymology of labora, 
but as I've studied the etymology of labora,  

linguists don't have any idea where this word came 
from, so I think my theory is just as good as any.  

It's about toil, labor. It's about hardship. 
It's about difficulty. It's not very exciting.

The other word for work doesn't help us 
either: opera. Oh yes, my friend Dr. Thomas,  

it's now coming to him. Do you know why 
we call it "opera"? Because you have to  

work really hard to try to enjoy it, 
right? That's our little private joke.

In the medieval world, if you were outside the 
monastery or outside the clergy, you were just  

putting in time. You were just putting in time. 
There was no meaning in that work you were doing.  

So, the Reformers come along, and they do two 
things for us. One is a wonderful doctrine,  

the priesthood of all believers. Now we 
get this directly—I'm going to give a  

walk through three texts very quickly. This 
is one of the foundations to the Reformation  

understanding of vocation. And I want to do 
some historical theological study with you,  

and then we go straight to a Psalm 
and spend some time in a Psalm.

But first, 1 Peter 2:9. Peter here is giving this 
persecuted, marginalized, infantile church an  

identity, and the appearances are the contrary. 
"Here's who you really are in 1 Peter 2:9: 'You  

are a chosen race. You are a royal priesthood. 
You are a holy nation, a people for His own  

possession, that you may proclaim the excellencies 
of Him who called you out of darkness into His  

marvelous light.'" What a beautiful verse. 
And here we are, all of us, a priesthood. 
 
 

We can go back a few books to 1 Timothy chapter 
2, and we see in 1 Timothy chapter 2 at verse  

5—again, a text, implication, theology that was 
obscured by the medieval Roman Catholic Church:  

"For there is one God and there is one mediator

We do not need another mediator. There is no 
other mediator. The priest—in fact, the entire  

church itself—was a mediator, and through her

the grace of what Christ did on the cross. That's

And then, of course, we can go to Hebrews,

The priesthood of the believer has a problem with 
this sharp distinction between the spiritual and

citizens in the kingdom of God, those who do not 
have the callings of the church attached to them.

But secondly, that's the first foundation, 
the second foundation is simply understanding  

this word, which comes straight from the 
Latin "to be called," as an English word,  

"vocation," and that all of us have 
a calling. All of us are called.

And so, in 1520—1520 was a very busy fall 
for Luther. He's getting excommunicated,  

and he's given a certain time to 
repent, and he will be relieved,  

or he will have to come and give an answer. 
We call it the papal bull, or papal decree,

That's how Luther sort of responds to things.

So, he's a very busy man. He's getting 
excommunicated, he's building bonfires,

The Freedom of a Christian Man,  

Address to the German Nobility, and The 
Babylonian Captivity of the Church. And  

in all three of those, he's working out his 
priesthood-of-the-believer doctrine. He's also,  

in all three of those, working out this doctrine 
of calling. He says all of us are called.

First of all, we are called in the sphere of the 
home, in our roles. Being a son or a daughter is  

a calling. Being a husband or a wife is a calling. 
Being a mother or a father is a calling from God.  

We have our gifts and our professions 
and our abilities that God gives us,  

and that is a calling. And then all of us are 
called as Christians to proclaim the gospel. So,  

Luther, far from being restrictive and 
narrowing this doctrine of vocation and  

this use of the word "calling," wants to 
be generous and wide in its application.

Now, once we call work a calling, it sets off 
a few dominoes, some implications of that.  

The first is that work has dignity. Work has 
dignity. We forget that the cultural mandate,  

and Adam and Eve actually working, precedes the 
fall. Now, what does the fall do? It brings about  

sweat, thorns, and weeds. And in Florida, 
like in California, it grows year-round,  

these weeds. And are you like me? You're pulling

cultivating this garden that we have been placed 
in, having dominion, stewardship over this,  

that is a creation ordinance. It is a good 
thing. So, Luther wants to say work has dignity.

The next thing Luther teaches, and it's very 
obvious, God does not need our work. But who  

does? Our neighbor. And so, work is our 
way of loving our neighbor. Our friend  

Dr. Godfrey's wife, Mary Ellen, is being cared

And so, in a hospital from top to bottom, and 
whether they're pagan or Christian or not,

there's a way, especially we as Christians,

But Luther goes a step further, and he speaks 
of God as hidden masks in our work. And so,  

he'll say God feeds us through the calling of the

and through these services. But it not only goes  

down the chain; it goes back up the chain. Because 
now the farmer, the surgeon, the orderly—now what  

are they doing but being instruments in the hands 
of God, in serving God. When we realize what work  

truly is, we see it in all of its fullness 
as a true calling and a true gift from God.

Calvin reflects on work in his Institutes, 
Book III, somewhere in there, he writes:  

"The Lord bids each of us in all life's 
actions to look to his calling, vocatio,  

for God knows how much the human mind is on fire 
with restless curiosity. Therefore, lest we wander  

about in uncertainty all our life long, the Lord 
has assigned to each one of us a sentry post."

Now think of that. Think about that for a 
moment. Those of you that are in the military,  

you know this. The sentry post was a very crucial 
job. If the sentry drops off just for a split  

second, whatever is being guarded—the barracks, 
whatever it may be—is at total risk. You had to  

be diligent. You had to be attentive. You had to 
be among the best. And whatever our job—Calvin  

will go on to say, whether you are a preacher or 
a pot-washer—that's your sentry post. The Lord  

has assigned each one of us a sentry post, 
and He bestows the name "calling" upon it.

So, now we understand where this comes from 
biblically, don't we? It comes from that little  

phrase in Ephesians. You could have all kinds of 
conversations about work. You could have all kinds

in the workplace and integrity and honesty. And  

it's not just our work that gets a paycheck—that 
is an error, to think the only thing that counts

we think of our work; all we need is this little  

phrase "as unto the Lord." That's what Calvin 
is saying. This is your sentry post, and God  

has given it to you. And to what level, to what 
degree? How do you do this? "As unto the Lord."  

Well, that's the standard, isn't it? We don't 
really need any other standards after that one.
