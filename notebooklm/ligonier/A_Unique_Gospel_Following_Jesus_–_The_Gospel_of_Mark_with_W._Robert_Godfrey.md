# A Unique Gospel: Following Jesus – The Gospel of Mark with W. Robert Godfrey

GODFREY: Welcome to the beginning of our study of 
the Gospel of Mark. I suppose I should begin with  

a confession. When I was younger, I had trouble 
with the Gospels. They were not my favorite part  

of the Bible. And I think the reason for that 
was I couldn't feel how they held together.  

It seemed like just one thing after another, 
and they were good things one after another,  

but I guess I have an excessively orderly 
mind. I want to know, "Why is this story  

being told the way it is being told? Why does 
one thing follow another?" And with the Gospels,  

a lot of the time, I just felt I 
don't get it. You read an epistle;  

you can tell how Paul is developing his 
argument. You read history in the Old Testament,  

and you can see why things are being told in 
the order and in the way they are being told.

For a long time I felt about the Gospels, I didn't 
get that feeling. Now, you can see how the Gospels  

moved. You know, they started early in Jesus' 
ministry or maybe even before Jesus' ministry, and  

they moved through His ministry and they moved to 
the end of His ministry. So, in that sense, there  

was a chronological motion in the Gospels, but why 
a lot of the elements were put quite where they  

were put? It seemed elusive to me and annoying. 
Alright, there I said it. I have gotten over it.  

But I would think of the end of John's Gospel 
where John says, "If I told you all that Jesus  

did, it would take books and books to fill the 
world." But why did he tell us what he told us?  

How did he decide what he left out and what he 
put in? And why did the Lord give us four Gospels?

So, I always had these questions, 
and in coming to study Mark's Gospel,  

I felt I made a breakthrough for me. 
And the breakthrough comes in terms  

of the word—yes, you have to submit 
to it—chiasm. I think I finally found  

the organization of Mark's Gospel through chiasms. 
Now, some of you have heard me often enough that  

you know what a chiasm is, but some of you may 
not. A chiasm is an ancient literary form followed  

in the Hebrew Bible as well as in the Greek 
Bible, and, of course, in much other ancient  

literature outside the Bible. And it is simply 
a way of organizing material that I think helped  

ancient people remember it. It may not initially 
strike us as all that memorable or helpful,  

but it helped ancient people. And I have what 
I see as the first chiasm in Mark's Gospel here  

on the board to give you an idea of it. I have 
often said that a chiasm is like a step pyramid,  

except here the step pyramid is upside down. So, 
I am not being a very helpful teacher that way.

But a chiasm has a beginning. It moves up to 
a center, then moves down to the end, and the  

beginning and the end corresponded some way as 
each step corresponds in some way. You can see  

that visually here that Mark's Gospel in the first 
section begins with the gospel and ends with the  

gospel. It moves on to talk about preaching. It 
also has a section on preaching near the end;  

wilderness, wilderness, and in the center Jesus' 
baptism. And what this did for me is begin to  

answer the question, not only, "What is Mark 
teaching in his Gospel?" but it helps us see  

how he is teaching in his Gospel, and it helps 
us somewhat see why he talks about things in  

the order and way in which he does. So, it really 
gives us insight into the structure of the Gospel  

that I think will be illuminating. I think it will 
be helpful. It will not give us discoveries that  

no one has ever heard of before. So, don't worry. 
I am a little crazy, but I am not that crazy.

So, we are not going to discover, you know, 
the date of Christ's return this way. We are  

not going to discover doctrinal truths that have 
never been affirmed before. The what of the gospel  

remains what we have always known is in the 
gospel. The truth of the gospel will remain  

absolutely the same, but this approach gives us a 
different angle on it. When I undertook to teach  

this series, one of my concerns was some people 
will say, "The Gospel of Mark? I already know  

that. I have studied through the Gospel of Mark. 
I have read the Gospel of Mark a lot of times.  

What is the point of doing the Gospel 
of Mark all over again?" Come on,  

did you think that way? Were you tempted to think 
that? I think this is going to make the Gospel of  

Mark fresh for us and help us see aspects of it, 
angles on it that we have never seen before. So,  

I hope you will go along with me on this 
journey, that you will be patient as we  

talk about structure, because I think it 
really will be helpful and illuminating.

Now, what is Mark's Gospel like apart from 
structure? We need to say the traditional things  

at the beginning. We know first of all that Mark 
is the briefest Gospel. It is only 60% of Luke's  

Gospel. Luke has the longest Gospel. And so, 60%, 
you know, Mark is only slightly more than half  

the length of Luke. So, it is brief. He tells 
his stories most of the time more briefly than  

the other Gospels. As we go along, we will see it 
isn't always true that it is briefer. Sometimes he  

is longer. And, of course, in a brief Gospel, if 
you have a deliberate longer telling of the story,  

we should notice that, right? Because he 
is making a real point by slowing down  

and telling the story at greater length, 
but in general it is certainly true that  

Mark is the shortest Gospel, and 
in most of the stories he tells,  

he is briefer than the other Gospels. For 
example, about the beginning of Jesus' ministry,  

which is basically what we are looking at here in 
this first section, Mark has fifteen verses about  

the beginning of Jesus' ministry. Matthew has 
eighty-two verses about the beginning of Jesus'  

ministry, and Luke has a hundred and eighty-two 
verses about the beginning of Jesus ministry.

So, you get a sense of really how much Mark 
has consolidated or focused in a laser-like  

way on what he wants to tell us. Especially 
at the beginning of the Gospel, Mark loves to  

use the word "immediately." "And immediately Jesus 
went here," "And immediately Jesus went there." He  

wants to establish a sense that this story he is 
telling is moving right along, is moving quickly,  

and he draws us with him then from event to 
event in the sense of rapid movement. And so,  

it gives a definite tempo to the story. 
And the temptation then is to think,  

since Mark is moving rapidly through the story, 
maybe he is moving carelessly through the story.  

When you and I tell a story with a purpose of 
being rapid, I think often we just kind of hop  

from this to that and maybe don't plan as well as 
we might if we were going more slowly. But that,  

of course, is not the case with Mark. His 
speed doesn't mean he is being careless.  

In fact, what impresses me as we go through the 
Gospel is how very careful he has been. He is very  

deliberate, very thoughtful, very intentional and 
carefully planned in all that he is doing. And so,  

while we want to go with him as he has been kind 
of speedy, we may want to slow down at points  

and think, "What is he doing? What is he really 
saying? What does he want us to get out of that?"

Another way that his style is impressive is 
the vivid quality of his writing. Even though  

he is brief, he adds certain details that 
we don't find in other Gospels. Obviously,  

they are brief details, because they 
haven't made the Gospel super-long,  

but when Jesus is being tempted in the 
wilderness, which is over here on our outline,  

the recording of the temptation in Mark is very 
brief. But he adds the little detail that in the  

wilderness there were wild animals. He is the 
only Gospel writer that says that. It makes the  

experience of the wilderness pretty vivid all 
of a sudden when we think in those terms. So,  

he adds those little details. When in the next 
section we see Jesus calling His first followers,  

we are told that they left their father with 
the hired servants and followed Him. Well,  

that little detail "hired servants" is unique 
to Mark. So, there are these vivid elements that  

intensify the picture even though the picture 
is most of the time being rather briefly told.

Another element is that his writing is kind of 
abrupt. It will just start and just stop. And  

sometimes you will be surprised, "That's not where 
I would have started it. That's not where I would  

have finished it. Why is he doing that?" And we 
will kind of look at that as we go along. Think  

of the way the Gospel begins: "The beginning of 
the gospel of Jesus Christ, the Son of God." It is  

not even a whole sentence. It just has a kind of 
abrupt beginning, and it will cause us, as we will  

see in a moment, to slow down and ask, "Why does 
he start that way? What does he mean us to take  

away from that?" But when we get to the end of 
the Gospel, we will be shocked at the abruptness  

with which he ends his gospel. We're not going 
to talk about that now. I am afraid you might  

not come back, but it is a very abrupt ending. Why 
does he end abruptly? What does he want to teach  

us by doing that? So, there is an abruptness 
that will arrest us and cause us to think.

There is also in Mark an allusiveness. He is 
alluding to other things as he goes along. He  

is not being explicit about it. He is wanting us 
to be meditative enough in our reading to stop and  

think, "Now, wait a minute. That makes me think of 
X or of Y." For example, in the very beginning of  

Mark, he says, verse 2, Mark 1, "As it is written 
in the prophet Isaiah," and then most of our  

Bibles have a quotation. It is indented to show us 
he is quoting. And what do you think he is doing?  

Alright, then you think he is quoting the 
prophet Isaiah, but the first quotation,  

the first part of the quotation is not from 
the prophet Isaiah; it is from Malachi. Malachi  

3 verse 1: "Behold, I send my messenger before 
your face who will prepare your way." Well,  

the liberals used to have great fun with 
this: "You see, Mark doesn't know what he  

is doing. He is attributing a verse that is in 
Malachi to Isaiah." Well, I think the liberals  

just don't understand the way Mark writes. I think 
Mark is sort of asking as he goes along, "So, how  

well do you know your Old Testament? I am going 
to go on to quote Isaiah, but I am first quoting  

Malachi. Did you notice? I am alluding to Malachi. 
I am quoting Isaiah later, but I am doing both of  

them. So, how smart are you? Do you know that? Are 
you following along? Are you paying attention?"

Or in the next section that we look at, we will 
read about Simon Peter's mother-in-law being  

sick with a fever. You remember that? 
"And immediately," there is that word,  

"they told Him about her." This is Mark 1 verse 
30: "And He came up and took her by the hand and  

lifted her up." Well, what is that describing? 
Well, it is describing she is in bed, sick, and  

He came and He healed her. See, I told you there 
wouldn't be anything shockingly new in this study,  

but it is also allusive. Over and over again in 
Mark's Gospel, we will see people being raised up  

by Jesus. Well, what should you think about when 
you read that? Well, He raises them up to heal  

them, but it is also what I like to call a "mini 
resurrection." He is already illustrating that He  

is the Lord of life. He is already illustrating 
that He can raise the sick and the dead. And so,  

Mark wants us to think about these things 
as we go along. He is alluding to truths  

that are beyond the simple immediate truth. The 
simple immediate truth is true. It is important.  

It is the primary thing we ought to see. But I 
think Mark is teaching us other things as well  

and wants us to see that as we go along. 
So, Mark is speedy, but careful, thoughtful,  

and that is why I think these structures and these 
chiasms are so important to notice as we go along.

Now, these chiasms are all my idea. And I went to 
a New Testament professor at our seminary and I  

said, "Tell me what you think of this? I'm not 
asking you to say you think I'm right or that  

you agree with me. I would just like you to tell 
me if you think I am absolutely crazy because I  

really don't want to be crazy." And he looked at 
it and he said, "This doesn't look crazy." So,  

that is the best encouragement I have 
gotten so far. So, this is my crazy idea,  

and I think it will be helpful. I think it is 
pretty convincing, but you should know it is  

just me. So, if you don't like it, it is okay. 
You are not disagreeing with anybody important.

So, here we have the beginning, and I believe that 
Mark's Gospel is itself a chiasm as a whole and  

that it really is divided into nine sections. 
Now, you know that our Bibles as we have them,  

the chapter divisions and the verse 
divisions are a very late addition.  

The chapter divisions and the verse divisions 
were added centuries after the Bible books were  

written to help us be able to find things. If I 
wanted you to turn to Mark 2 verse 1, you could  

do that very easily in your Bibles, right? 
But if you had Mark on some kind of a scroll,  

I would have to say to you, "Make two turns in the 
scroll and you'll be at about where I want you to  

read." And people realized that was an awkward way 
of trying to help people find things in the Bible.  

So, they added chapter divisions and verse 
divisions, but there is nothing divine about  

that. It is helpful, but sometimes it is wrong. 
Sometimes they make a chapter division right in  

the middle of a story that shouldn't be separated 
at all. I sometimes tell people in our church,  

"Read through a book of the Bible sometimes 
from the middle of one chapter to the middle  

of another chapter." You will get a whole 
different angle on how it is put together. So,  

what I am really suggesting to you is we probably 
should have divided Mark into nine chapters,  

but I am going to call them sections so we don't 
get all confused. And these nine sections then  

are also a chiasm. So, the first section about 
foundations has relationship to the last section  

which is about the future and the resurrection. 
And this chiasm, this chiastic structure has a  

center. And we will talk about that when we 
get there, but the center is about faith.

And so, it is not surprising that what 
Mark is after is faith; faith in Jesus,  

faith in those following Jesus. And when 
we begin to look at the Gospel this way,  

one of the things we will discover as we go 
along is the great struggle to find faith  

amongst the disciples of Jesus, so much so 
that in talking about this with Chris Larson,  

he suggested that maybe we ought to call this 
Gospel, "The Gospel of 'What's the matter with  

you people?'" Because what the Gospel really 
says is, the disciples never quite get it. Now,  

why would you write a whole Gospel about people 
who never quite get it? Well, you might do that to  

encourage people who are struggling to get it. If 
it took the original disciples a while to get it,  

then maybe we shouldn't be discouraged if it takes 
us a while to get it, or if it takes people we  

know a while to get it. This is a great call to 
faith with the recognition that the call to faith  

is not always easy. It is not always a call easy 
to hear or easy to understand or easy to fulfill.  

And so, I hope this will be a great encouragement 
to us as we go along and see the disciples  

struggling in ways that will help us see how 
we can make progress and we can move along.

The Gospel was probably written in the fifties 
or very early sixties. Most scholars think this  

is the first Gospel written. It is written twenty 
to thirty years after the ascension of Jesus. Now,  

that is not a super long time at all, but it 
is time passed. It is probably written for  

the Christian churches, which means Mark has 
in mind, as he sits down to write, that he is  

writing to people who are already believers, 
who have already heard the story of Jesus,  

who have already heard through preaching the 
gospel of Jesus. And he is writing to help them.  

He is writing to encourage them. He is writing to 
flesh out the story for them, because, you know,  

who knows how thorough the preachers have been, 
who knows how much of the story the preachers  

have known. And so, he is trying to provide a 
resource that will be encouraging, but he doesn't  

have to assume that they know nothing as he is 
writing. And that is why he can be allusive. He is  

writing to people who have already heard, who have 
already believed, and he is writing to help them,  

but not writing just to initiate them to 
something they don't know anything about  

at all. And that I think will be influencing 
the way we can and should read the story.

As I had been working on this, I heard 
a television interview with a woman who,  

as far as I know, is a secular reporter and has 
been interested in mental illness and treatments  

that work and treatments that don't work. And 
she had written a study that some people found  

helpful. And she had one sentence in her interview 
that really struck me. She said—and now she's  

talking about mental illness. I am taking her 
sentence and applying it to something altogether  

different. But she said there are stories that 
save us and stories that trap us. And relative  

to mental illness she was suggesting, 
you know, if the psychiatrist tells you,  

"You are crazy,"—of course, they never knew 
that—but if the psychiatrist tells you, "You  

are a victim," and your story traps you, she said, 
"It's unlikely you will ever get out of that trap,  

but there are stories that save us and deliver us 
from the traps that have held us." And I thought,  

"What a wonderful way to talk about 
the gospel! The gospel is a story,  

not that traps us, but saves us, liberates us, 
frees us, and that is what we are hoping to find  

as we go through the Gospel of Mark, that 
great story that saves us and liberates us,  

and leads us to faith. So, we are starting that 
adventure together and we will go on next time.
