# Ecclesiastes: Dust to Glory with R.C. Sproul

SPROUL: One of my favorite books in the Old Testament
is the small book of Ecclesiastes. It may

sound strange to people, but my work in my
career as a professor of philosophy and of

apologetics is part of the reason why this
book is so important to me, because it expresses

the wisdom of God over against a prevailing
cultural atmosphere of skepticism. Another

reason why I love this book is personal. This
was the book of the Bible that was used by

God in my own personal conversion. In fact
the only text of Scripture that the person

who told me about Christ mentioned in passing
was an obscure reference from the book of

Ecclesiastes that said, “A tree falls in
the forest, and where it falls it lies." And

I -- I'm probably the only person in the history
of the church that was converted to Christ

by that verse, but it was a profound experience
of illumination as I sat and listened to that;

and as it was applied to my life I saw myself
going nowhere, lying on the floor of the forest

having fallen, and I was rotting and disintegrating.
And that is the vision I saw of my own soul

when this text was opened up to me. So my
personal love for the book is without end

for that reason.

But the book of Ecclesiastes is puzzling to
many people because so much of it is downbeat,

and it has an air of cynicism at times and
skepticism; and it's one of the most difficult

books in the Old Testament to interpret, for
this reason. And there are all kinds of questions

about exactly who wrote the book. It's attributed
to the preacher, or the qoheleth of Israel,

and illusions to Solomon, and tradition has
it the Solomon wrote the entire book, but

there are questions about that. And one of
the theories is that there's kind of a dialogue

going on in this book, and the qoheleth is
more or less the foil for the believer; and

so the charges of the skeptic are raised,
and then the response from the wisdom of God

is given. And that kind of approach to wisdom
literature was not unprecedented in the ancient

world, but for these and other reasons the
book is a little bit difficult from time to

time to understand; but it's -- it's almost
like reading a twentieth-century existential

philosopher and discovering such philosophies
millennia ago. It's at times like reading

Sartre and Camus, or Kirkegaard, or some of
the other atheistic thinkers of our day.

I think, for example, of Ernest Hemmingway,
who sometimes has been described in American

literature as one who embraced pessimistic
naturalism. He saw the futility of human existence,

and he complained about the problems of meaninglessness
that assaulted his own soul. He ended his

own life, as you know, with suicide. One of
the books for which he is famous was titled

The Sun Also Rises. The title for that book
is taken directly verbatim from the text of

the book of Ecclesiastes.

Well the book begins with a sense of a cyclical
view of life, which was common in the ancient

world, particularly in elements of Greek philosophy,
where the idea was that everything in this

world is simply going around and around in
a never-ending circle; and there is no ultimate

beginning to human experience and no terminal
point of triumph, but everything is locked

in this never-ending circle that is going
nowhere. What goes around comes around.

I think of the famous movie that communicated
this idea years ago, where Jane Fonda played

in this movie called They Shoot Horses, Don't
They? And in that movie the whole story was

focused on poverty during the Depression,
and a prize was offered to people who were

struggling economically, to whoever could
win a dance marathon. So all these couples

enter into this marathon, and they danced
for hours and hours, and some get tired and

drop out; and some last more than a day and
into the second day, and in order to hurry

the thing along, the MC in the movie would
speed up from time to time the pace of the

dance. And as the people were rushing around,
dancing in a circle, he would holler into

the microphone, “Round, and round, and round
they go, and where they stop nobody knows."

And the whole motif of that movie was to communicate
the excessistential principle of meaninglessness,

the rat race.

Well the book of Ecclesiastes begins with
these words: “'Vanity of vanity,' says the

preacher. 'Vanity of vanities; all is vanity.'"
Now understand the phrase “vanity of vanities,"

or “vanity of vanity --vanities" is a common
literary form in the ancient world. You find

it in the New Testament when Jesus is called
the Lord of Lord or the King of Kings. It's

a way to express the superlative degree of
something, and so what the book begins with

is a statement in the superlative degree about
some idea, and the idea that is stressed here

is the idea of vanity.

Now we use the term “vanity" to refer to
egotism or pride. I remember we used to have

a little piece of furniture that was in my
grandmother's bedroom at home where she would

sit in the morning and look into the mirror
and apply her makeup, and that little piece

of furniture was called a vanity because it
was a piece of furniture designed to enhance

a person's being vain about their looks; but
that's not the connotation of vanity here.

Here the term “vanity" refers not to pride,
but to that which is done in vain -- that

which is futile. And so we could translate
the idea here, “Futility of futility. All

is futile."

Now that's the central motif here that the
book is addressing, and we notice in the book

of Ecclesiastes, poetically there are two
different points of reference. On the one

hand, there is the phrase, “under heaven,"
or, “under the sun." Now “under the sun,"

it is thought by many commentators, refers
to the spheres of this world. “Under heaven"

refers to the sphere of the domain of God,
and so what is going on here is the conflict

between two realms -- the realm of this world
and the perspective of this world, vis-à-vis

the realm of God, the eternal realm, which
gives a completely different perspective.

You know we're told that we live in the age
of secularism, and we always hear about secular

humanism, or humanistic secularism. You put
that little “ism" on the end of the word

secular, and any time you have an “ism,"
you're talking about an ideology, a philosophy,

a system of thinking. The word “saeculum"
is one of the Latin words that refers to this

world and differs from the word “mundus."
“Mundus" in Latin describes this world spacially.

“Saeculum" describes this world with respect
to time or temperament, and the basic motif

of secularism is we live in the here and now
in this saeculum, in the temporal, and we're

cut off from the eternal. The heart of secularism
teaches there is no eternal. There's only

the temporal, and so whatever meaning we can
discover in this world has to be discovered

there in this world, and the axiom is, “You
only live once, then you die. So you might

as well do it with gusto." That's the creed
of modern man, and that creed is as old as

people, and what the -- what the author is
doing is exploring the implications of life

in this world when it is cut off from the
eternal or from the transcendent.

So he says, “Vanity of vanities; all is
vanity. What profit has a man from all his

labor in which he toils under the sun?" You
see he's asking the question, “What use

is there for all of the pain and the effort
and the labor of my hands? All life is as

grass. We work as hard as we can, but in the
final analysis, we perish, and our labor perishes

with us."

“One generation passes away, and another
generation comes, but the earth abides forever.

The sun also rises, and the sun goes down
and hastens to the place where it arose. The

wind goes toward the south, turns around to
the north. The wind whirls about it continually.

All comes again on its circuit. All rivers
run into the sea, yet the sea is not full.

To the place from which the rivers come, there
they return again. All things are full of

labor. Man can't express that the eye is not
satisfied with seeing, nor the ear filled

with hearing. That which has been is what
will be, that which is done is what will be

done, and there is nothing new under the sun."

That is exceedingly pessimistic, and every
human being has experienced the threat of

that -- of that pessimism -- and the doubt
that is raised when you wake up and look at

yourself in the mirror, and you say, “Why
am I doing this?"

At my age I run into people who are my contemporaries,
and many of them have gone through the crisis,

as they come into their twilight years, and
they look at me, and they say, “R.C., there

must be more to it than this!" and I say,
“There is more to it than this," and that's

what this book is about.

And so at the beginning we see this cry of
futility, and then as the book proceeds, various

things are examined that people pursue in
order to find happiness, to find meaning and

significance to their lives. They look for
pleasure; hedonism is evaluated. Hedonisms

is that philosophy that says that the purpose
of life is to maximize pleasure and minimize

pain. We hear our own generation being described
as the pleasure seekers -- those who are looking

from one party to the next, from one drug
to the next, from one high to the next, from

one entertainment or amusement to the next.
Physical pleasure becomes the narcotic of

choice for people who have no hope.

The apostle Paul understands that in the New
Testament when he writes to the Corinthians,

who were convinced that there was no life
after death -- or some who were raising that

question -- and Paul was answering them. He
cites one of the axioms from the epicurean

philosophers: “Let us eat, drink, and be
merry, for tomorrow we die." Now Paul was

not saying to the Christian community, “Let
us eat, drink, and be merry." He prefaces

that by saying, “If Christ is not raised,
then let us eat, drink, and be merry, for

tomorrow we die, because if God is not home,
if we are bound to the secular experience,

and that's all there is, then it makes sense
to be a hedonist, to eat, drink, and be merry,

for tomorrow you die." There is always a price
tag -- what the philosophers call the hedonist

paradox: that if you pursue pleasure, and
you succeed in finding it, you will be bored.

If you seek pleasure and don't succeed in
finding it, you will be frustrated, so that

the end of the Hedonist is either boredom
or frustration. And what is being exposed

here in this wisdom literature is the folly
of hedonism, the folly of seeking to find

meaning to your existence by simply having
a big party.

Then he goes and says in chapter three, “To
everything there is a season." Want to fill

that in? “Turn, turn, turn." It is amazing
to me that in this age of pessimism, even

our popular music has been informed by something
as ancient as the book of Ecclesiastes. “To

everything there is a season, a time for every
purpose under heaven." You see now we see

the different motif that is set over against
it, the motif of the significance of the diverse

things that we experience in this world. Here
the temporal -- time -- is given purpose under

heaven, and we don't do the same thing all
the time. At some times certain things are

appropriate; at other times they're not appropriate.
You don't laugh at a funeral; you weep. And

you don't mourn at a birth. There's a time
to laugh; there's a time to cry. There's a

time to plant, there's a time to reap. There's
a time to mourn; there's a time to dance,

and the wise person, who evaluates his life
in this world from the perspective of God,

has the wisdom to know the difference about
what time it is and what's appropriate in

a given moment.

Herman Melville, in his book Redburn, made
this observation: He said, “It is not until

we learn that one grief outweighs a thousand
joys that we will ever understand what Christianity

is trying to make us." And at that point his
reflections were based on a text from this

book. “It is better to go to the house of
mourning than to spend your time with fools."

You see, God is a God who is intimately involved
with human suffering, and the gospel is a

gospel that is pronounced to people who have
experienced the grief of human suffering;

and the people of God are to be people who
are there at the point of pain.

I look under the sun, and it seems that injustice
prevails -- verse sixteen of chapter three.

“I saw under the sun, in the place of judgment,
wickedness was there, and in the place of

righteousness, iniquity was there." And so
it goes, throughout the book, folly of seeking

meaning through riches, through knowledge,
through fortune, through fame, and all the

rest.

And then we come to the conclusion that death
comes to all, but God is the answer to all

of the questions that people suffer. Chapter
twelve -- the final chapter -- begins with

this admonition: “Remember your Creator
in the days of your youth, before the difficult

days come and the years draw near when you
say, 'I have no pleasure in them,' and while

the sun and the light and the moon and the
stars are not darkened and the clouds do not

return after the rain."

Then in verse nine, “Moreover, because the
preacher was wise, he still taught the people

knowledge. He pondered and sought out and
set in order many proverbs, and the preacher

sought to find acceptable words, and what
was written was upright, words of truth. The

words of the wise are like goads, and the
words of scholars like well-driven nails given

by one shepherd."

And then he says in verse thirteen, “Let
us hear the conclusion." That's what you're

always looking for, when you're studying any
philosophy. What's the bottom line? What is

the concluding principle? So we come to the
book. The book starts with the declaration,

“Futility of futility; everything is futile."
All right, what's the conclusion? “Fear

God, keep His commands, for this is man's
all. For God will bring every work into judgment,

including every secret thing, whether good
or evil." That's the conclusion. He's saying,

“It's the conclusion to your work. It's
the conclusion to your pain. It is the conclusion

to your joy. Fear God, keep His commands,"
because what he's saying is, “Your life

will be evaluated, not in terms of this world,
or the saeculum of the here and now, or of

the cyclical pattern of this world, but our
lives -- their significance, their value,

and their worth -- will be judged by the one
who judges from an eternal perspective, and

who will say to us, 'Depart from me; I never
knew you,' or He will say, 'Well done, thou

good and faithful servant.'" And when He says
that, every ounce of sweat that has come from

the brow of your head in your labor, every
ache in your heart, every pain in your soul

will find its fullness of meaning and significance,
and you will discover that everything -- the

experiences -- that you experienced in your
life counts. Nothing is futile under the heavens.
