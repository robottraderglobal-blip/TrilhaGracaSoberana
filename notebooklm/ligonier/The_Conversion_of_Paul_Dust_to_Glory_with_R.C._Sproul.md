# The Conversion of Paul: Dust to Glory with R.C. Sproul

SPROUL: Today we're going to try to answer the question,
at least in part, why did Luke write the Book

of Acts in the first place? Now we've already
suggested that we change the title from the

Acts of the Apostles to the Acts of the Holy
Spirit, and obviously one of the chief concerns

that Luke had was to give us a record of the
expansion of the early church. But there's

a lot of speculation in another direction.
There are many who believe, and not without

some solid reason, that one of the main concerns
that Luke had for writing the Book of Acts

was to give the church an apologia, an apology,
an apologetic, or a defense of the authenticity

of the apostleship of Saint Paul. Because
just as in Luke's first volume, in the gospel

of Luke, the central figure is Jesus, so the
most dominant figure that we encounter in

the Book of Acts, which is volume two from
Luke's pen, is the apostle Paul.

Well, why should the apostle Paul need a defense
or an apology written in his behalf? Well,

we remember that at the end of Jesus' life,
one of His twelve committed suicide; his name

was Judas. And the early church then gathered
to select a replacement for Judas, and in

the opening chapters of the Book of Acts,
we learn something of the process, or the

procedure, the church went through to make
that selection. And by reconstructing the

whole scenario, we understand that there were
basic criteria that had to be met in the early

church for a person to be qualified as an
apostle. And basically, those requirements

could be reduced to three. And the first was
that before a person could become an apostle,

they had to have first been a disciple, and
you recall when we had our lesson where we

explained the difference between a disciple
and an apostle. A disciple is a learner, and

an apostle is one who is commissioned with
authority and sent and empowered to speak

in behalf of the One who authorizes him. But
to be an apostle, first one had to be a disciple;

second, that person had to be an eyewitness
of the resurrection; and thirdly, the person

had to have a direct and immediate call from
Christ.

Now you remember in the Old Testament when
we looked at the prophets, we saw that there

was an ongoing struggle between those who
were genuine prophets who were endowed by

God with His Holy Spirit, who were agents
of revelation who could preface their remarks

by saying, "Thus sayeth the Lord" -- that
is, those whom God had selected and empowered

and sent forth to preach His word to the nation
-- and the false prophets who told their own

dreams and gave their own opinions, and who
simply gave human perspective, not the authoritative

truth of God. And so to distinguish between
the true prophet and the false prophet in

the Old Testament, one of the most important
tests for the true prophet was that he could

articulate and verify the circumstances of
his call. That's why so many of the prophets,

such as Ezekiel, Isaiah, Jeremiah, Amos, just
to name a few, are jealous in the Old Testament

to report for us the circumstances of their
call, because that divine call is what gave

them their authority to speak the word of
God.

Now we are told about the church that the
foundation of the church is the prophets and

the apostles, Christ being the chief cornerstone.
And so there is a sense in which the apostle

in the New Testament occupies a parallel office
of authority to the prophet in the Old Testament.

And the New Testament apostle is God's agent
of revelation, who announces the word of God

with nothing less than the authority of God.
And so it is imperative that to be an apostle,

that apostle had to be called and sent, commissioned,
and authorized directly and immediately by

Christ.

Now one of the reasons that the debate continues
into our own day about apostolic succession

has to do with these criteria. I mean, there
are many believers, many Christians, who believe

that there are still apostles running around
in the church today, whereas other Christians

do not believe that there are apostles, in
the ultimate sense, with a capital A, in the

church today. And some argue that it couldn't
possibly be so for there to be an apostle

today because you have no one alive who was
once an ap -- disciple -- of Jesus or who

was a first-century eyewitness of the resurrection
or who was directly and immediately called

of Christ in the first century.

Now as soon as somebody makes an argument
like that, some wi -- someone will immediately

respond and say, "Well, wait a minute. What
about Paul?" Paul wasn't a disciple, and Paul

wasn't an eyewitness of the resurrection in
the same manner that the other disciples were,

and so he loses on two of these three credentials.
What is claimed for Paul in the New Testament,

and what Luke is so jealous to attest, is
that Paul received a direct and immediate

call from Christ, and that it is that call
from Christ from which he receives his authority.

Now let me just add to this. Someone might
say, "Well, then that means that somebody

today can have a direct and immediate call,
just as Paul did, and so who's to say that

there aren't any apostles today?" Well, we
also understand that when Paul received his

call, he was immediately sent back to the
church in Jerusalem, and in a sense, was confirmed

by that group of men about whose apostolic
authority there was no question. Now Paul

was jealous in his letters later on to say
that doesn't mean that he derived his authority

from them. His authority came from Christ.
Nevertheless, that his authority was given

to him by Christ was confirmed by the other
original apostles, and that's a significant

point here.

But in any case, Luke is so jealous to give
us the credentials for the apostle Paul that

not just once or twice, but several times
in the Book of Acts, he gives us or -- either

a direct account or allusions to the circumstance
of Paul's apostolic call. Let's take some

time and look at that record as Luke provides
it for us in the ninth chapter of the Book

of Acts. Verse one of chapter nine begins
as follows, "Then Saul, still breathing threats

and murder against the disciples of the Lord,
went to the high priest and asked letters

from him to the synagogues of Damascus, so
that if he found any who were of the Way,

whether men or women, he might bring them
bound to Jerusalem."

Now this little introductory statement by
Luke gives us some more insight as to why

the question of the authenticity of Paul's
apostleship was necessary, because he had

a reputation among the believing community
as the scourge of the Christian community.

We are told he breathed out fire as he exhibited
his passionate desire to rid the world of

this new heretical sect that he believed was
undermining the purity of Judaism. We know

that Saul had been trained in rabbinic instruction.
He had studied under the most important and

famous rabbinic scholar of his day, Gamaliel,
and he called himself a Pharisee of Pharisees.

It's been said by historians that Paul had
the equivalent of two PhD's by the time he

was twenty-one years old, and was the most
educated man in Palestine. But his education

only made him more fiercely opposed to this
sect who were called people of the Way that

he wanted to wipe them out. And so he became
the Adolf Eichmann of the first century church,

who was seeking a final solution for these
Christians, that he might exterminate them.

And then to hear that this Eichmann, this
Hitler, who had carried on such a merciless

campaign of persecution against the people
of God, was now passing himself off as the

commissioned spokesman for Jesus Christ made
it very, very difficult for some of those

first century believers to accept. And so
again, one of the issues that Luke has to

address in this book is this radical change,
this radical conversion in standpoint and

in passion, of Saul of Tarsus.

And so we read in verse three of chapter nine,
"As he journeyed he came near Damascus, and

suddenly a light shone around him from heaven.
Then he fell to the ground, and heard a voice

saying to him, 'Saul, Saul, why are you persecuting
Me?'" Let me take a minute to give some exposition

of that particular question that he hears.
The first thing is that Paul sees a blinding

light that elsewhere is called more brilliant
than the noonday sun, and the response that

he has is to fall to the ground. Now we know
that the light that he beheld was the radiant

glory, the Shekinah glory of God Himself,
of -- to which the Scriptures attest over

and over again as the outward display or manifestation
of His inner character. And so Saul sees this

blinding light in the sky, and falls to the
ground, and he hears, as we are told elsewhere,

he hears this voice speaking to him in the
Hebrew language, and the voice says to him,

"Saul, Saul, why are you persecuting Me?"

Now there are two things I want to say about
that voice that he hears. The first thing

is that when Saul is addressed by the heavenly
voice, he's addressed in terms of the repetition

of his name, "Saul, Saul." Search the Scriptures
and see how many times in the Bible anybody

is addressed for any reason by the repetition
of their name. You will discover that it occurs

less than twenty times in the whole scope
of redemptive history. And if you would examine

each of the occurrences that are recorded
for us, you would see something emerge very

clearly. When Moses was called in the Mideonite
wilderness, God called out of the burning

bush, "Moses, Moses"; when Abraham was at
Mount Mariah and was just about to stab his

son on the altar, the voice of God came to
him, saying, "Abraham, Abraham"; when Elisha

stood on the earth and watched the ascent
of Elijah, he said, "My Father, my Father,

the chariots of Israel"; when David mourned
the loss of his son, he cried "Absalom, Absalom,

my son, my son"; Jesus on the cross, "Eli,
Eli, lema sabacthani," over and over and over

again. We see this, and it's always an expression,
in the Hebrew tongue, of profound personal

intimacy. That's why Jesus at the end of the
Sermon on the Mount said that many people

on the last day would come and say, "Lord,
Lord" -- that is, their hypocrisy would be

clothed in this hypocritical form of the repeated
address, claiming intimacy with Jesus, not

just a passing knowledge of Him. They'll say,
"Lord, Lord." Jesus will say, "Depart from

Me; I never knew you." And so what this voice
is saying to Saul is, "Saul, I know you; I

know everything about you. I know you intimately,
and I love you. But why are you persecuting

Me?"

Now notice the question isn't, "Why are you
persecuting My church"; it's, "Why are you

persecuting Me?" Because the church is the
body of Christ. It is the bride of Christ,

and anyone who attacks His bride is attacking
Him; anyone who assaults His body is assailing

Him. And even in these words, we learn much
of Jesus' view of His relationship to His

body, the church, because Saul had not been
personally attacking Jesus -- He had never

met Jesus before -- but He was laying waste
the body of Christ. And so now the voice says

to him, "Why are you persecuting Me?" And
he said, "Who are you, Lord?"

I don't know which form of the concept, "Lord,"
he was using; this may simply be the polite

form of address saying, "Who are you, sir,"
but given the circumstances of the blinding

radiance of the refulgent glory of God, I
suspect that Paul, or Saul at this point,

knew instantly whoever it was who was speaking
to him was his sovereign Lord.

"And the Lord said, 'I am Jesus, whom you
are persecuting. It is hard for you to kick

against the goads.'" Now in the ancient world,
most of the carts that were drawn for labor

in the fields were drawn by oxen. The oxen
were yoked and then fastened to the cart,

and sometimes the oxen behaved somewhat like
mules and became stubborn, and when they were

prodded to move forward, their rebellious
reaction would be to kick their feet back

against the cart, and they could be in danger
of destroying the vehicle that they were supposed

to be pulling. And so some of the farmers
attached some rather severe spikes at the

front of the cart, so if the ox kicked in
retaliation, it would injure its own foot

and get the message, "It's time to move."
But then there were those that were considered

the super dumb oxes, who would kick against
the ox goad and hurt their foot and become

enraged, and they'd get so mad they'd kick
again harder; and the more they kicked, the

more damage they did to themselves. And that's
the image that is used here by Jesus when

He says, "Paul," or, "Saul, you stupid ox,"
he said, "your fury knows no bounds. You go

from city to city trying to destroy My church.
You're kicking against the ox goad, but all

you're doing is hurting your own foot."

"So he" -- that is, Saul -- "trembling and
astonished, said, 'Lord, what do You want

me to do?'" which indicates that conversion
has already taken place. What do You want

me to do? This is so typical of the circumstances
of a call to a prophet in the Old Testament

-- what do You want me to do? "And the Lord
said, 'Arise and go into the city, and you

will be told what you must do.' And the men
who journeyed with him stood speechless, hearing

a voice but seeing no one. And Saul arose
from the ground, and when his eyes were opened

he saw no one. But they led him by the hand
and brought him into Damascus. And he was

three days without sight, and neither ate
nor drank. And there was a certain disciple

at Damascus named Ananias; and to him the
Lord said in a vision, 'Ananias.' And he said,

'Here I am, Lord.' 'Arise and go to the street
called Straight, and inquire at the house

of Judas for one called Saul of Tarsus, for
behold, he is praying. And in a vision he

has seen a man named Ananias coming in and
putting his hand on him, so that he might

receive his sight. And Ananias answered, 'Lord,
I have heard from many about this man, how

much harm he has done to Your saints in Jerusalem.
And here he has authority from the chief priest

to bind all who call upon Your name.'" It's
like Ananias is saying, "Lord, do You know

what You're doing? I know who this fellow
is," and he takes it upon himself to instruct

God. "But the Lord said to him, 'Go, for he
is a chosen vessel of Mine to bear My name

before Gentiles, kings, and the children of
Israel. For I will show him how many things

he must suffer for My name's sake.'"

This experience was the decisive moment in
Saul's life, and throughout his activity,

throughout his writings, he continually came
back to this moment and talked about his call,

talked about his being chosen of God, commissioned
and set apart, to be poured out as an offering,

to participate in the afflictions of Christ,
to participate in the humiliation of Jesus,

that he may also participate in the exaltation
of Christ. And the record is is that there

was never on the face of this earth, apart
from Jesus Himself, a more devout or obedient

servant of God than this man whose activities
in behalf of the Christ, who was the lover

of his soul, were, to say the least, prodigious.
And later on as he stood in chains before

King Agrippa and defended his ministry, he
told Agrippa this very story, and when he

was finished, he said to Agrippa, "Whereupon
oh, king, I was not disobedient to this heavenly

vision." He had the vision, he had the call,
and he obeyed the call until the very second

that under the decree of Nero his head was
separated from his body, and he went home

to be with Christ.
