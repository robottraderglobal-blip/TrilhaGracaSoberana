# Paul at Malta (Acts 28:1–15) — A Sermon by R.C. Sproul

SPROUL: Today I’m going to start at verse one 
of chapter 28 and read to the end of verse 16. 

Now when they had escaped, they then found 
out that the island was called Malta. And  

the natives showed us unusual kindness, for 
they kindled a few and made us all welcomed,  

because of the rain that was falling and because 
of the cold. But when Paul had gathered a bundle  

of sticks and laid them on the fire, a viper 
came out because of the heat and fastened on  

his hand. So when the natives saw the creature 
hanging from his hand they said to one another,  

“No doubt this man is a murderer. Though he 
has escaped the sea, yet justice does not allow  

him to live.” But he shook off the creature 
into the fire and suffered no harm. However,  

they were expecting that he would swell up or 
suddenly fall down dead. But after they had looked  

for a long time and saw no harm come to him, they 
changed their minds and said that he was a god. 

On that region there was an estate of the leading 
citizen of the island, whose name was Publius,  

who received us and entertained us courteously 
for three days. And it happened that the father  

of Publius lay sick of a fever and dysentery. 
And Paul went into him and prayed, and he laid  

hands on him and healed him. So when this was 
done the rest of those on the island who had  

diseases also came and were healed. And they also 
honored us in many ways, and when we were departed  

they provided such things as were necessary.
After three months we sailed in an Alexandrian  

ship, whose figurehead was the twin 
brothers, which had wintered at the  

island. And landing at Syracuse we stayed three 
days. From there we circled round and reached  

Rhegium. And after one day the south wind 
blew, and the next day we came to Puteoli,  

where we found brethren and were invited to stay 
with them seven days. And so we went toward Rome.  

And from there when the brethren heard about 
us they came to meet us as far as Appian Forum  

and Three Inns. When Paul saw them, he thanked 
God and took courage. And when we came to Rome,  

the centurion delivered the prisoners to the 
captain of the guard, but Paul was permitted to  

dwell by himself with the soldier who guarded him.
This is the infallible Word of God for the people  

of God. Thanks be to God. Let us pray.
Once again, Father, as we listen to this  

narrative of the journey to Rome of the apostle 
Paul we stand amazed at the singular care of your  

providence upon this man, whom you had called 
for this mission. And we pray as we examine  

his journey this morning that we may also be 
inspired to put our faith and confidence in  

your invisible hand of providence. For 
we ask it in the name of Christ. Amen.

Chapter 27 ended by telling what happened after 
the ship floundered that was carrying the 276  

passengers and how when the ship broke up those 
who could swim made it to shore on their own.  

Those who could not swim held onto pieces of wood 
from the broken ship, and they were washed up on  

shore. And exactly as Paul had prophesied in their 
midst, not a single person onboard the ship lost  

his life, but they all reached the beach alive. 
But they reached that beach exhausted, cold, and  

wet. Remember. It’s now November, and it’s a very 
cold time of the year on Malta. Now, the local  

natives that the Greeks here call barbarians, 
only because they considered anybody who wasn’t  

Greek or wasn’t Jewish to be barbarian, indicated 
that they hardly act like vicious barbarians but  

instead were extremely hospitable towards these 
people who had been shipwrecked. And they dragged  

them out of the water, brought them safely to 
shore. And they built a bonfire to give warmth to  

those who were standing around shivering, wet, and 
also to cook food to feed them in their hunger. 

And the text tells us that as Paul’s normal 
behavior was he just didn’t stand around and  

watch what was happening. He participated in this 
ministry of mercy. Went to and gathered some twigs  

for the fire, and added them to the fire. And when 
that happened an extraordinary event took place.  

Suddenly a viper that apparently had been torpid 
due to the cold weather, as snakes will be. It’s  

not safe to go into the woods looking for golf 
ball, men, during the summer in central Florida.  

But when you get in December and January you can 
usually tromp around among the palmettos without  

fear of rattlesnakes because they’re either 
asleep or very torpid, indeed. But in any case,  

the warmth of the fire revived this viper. 
And as Paul was placing the twigs on the  

fire this viper struck him and clung to his hand.
Now that incident that is described here has been  

the target of certain critics throughout church 
history. One criticism is this: that there are no  

poisonous snakes, no snakes at all on the island 
of Malta. Just like there aren’t in Ireland,  

but there used to be. And even though we have 
no record of St. Patrick every visiting Malta,  

we do have abundant records from antiquity 
testifying that at that time there were  

poisonous snakes there, but after the island was 
completely populated, the snake disappeared. As  

they lost their natural habitant that happened. 
The other complaint is they say if this is a viper  

the viper doesn’t strike and hold onto its victim, 
but it strikes suddenly, pulls its fangs back. And  

so Luke is incorrect in describing a viper biting 
Paul and holding on. However, there are numerous  

examples in nature and in history of vipers that 
strike and when they strike get their fangs caught  

on the clothing or boots or whatever and are not 
instantly removed. I know a man who was stricken  

by a six-foot long diamondback rattlesnake here 
in central Florida. He fortunately was wearing  

boots. The good news was the boots saved him 
from having the fangs penetrate too deeply into  

his foot. The bad news was this fang stuck in 
the boot, and he couldn’t get rid of the snake,  

as much as he tried to shake it loose. And so 
it’s very possible that this snake was one who  

caught himself on Paul’s sleeve or whatever while 
he was striking him. The other argument is that  

these natives who identified the snake as a viper 
didn’t know what they were talking about. Now  

this from critics who look back over two thousand 
years. They weren’t there, and they’re thousands  

of miles removed. They don’t know anything about 
the ancient flora and fauna of Malta, and they  

doubt the word of the eyewitnesses of the account. 
But the reason why they struggle so much with the  

story is obvious. It’s a miracle. And that’s the 
way Luke records it, as the sober truth of what  

happened by the intervention of Almighty God.
But one thing about these natives, as kind,  

as generous as they were. They were also, like 
many of us, swift to make judgments that were  

incorrect. The first thing they assumed when 
they saw that snake on Paul’s hand was this: Ha,  

he must be a murderer. Because how else can you 
explain that he survives this shipwreck and now  

in this quirk of events he’s bitten by a deadly 
snake. This they attributed to Nemesis. Who was  

the mythological god for vengeance. That’s where 
the word comes over into our language. Somebody  

that’s constantly giving us a hard time and 
making our lives miserable we say that person  

is our nemesis. Well, they attributed this work to 
Nemesis, whose job it was, was to bring justice to  

people where other attempts to bring justice had 
failed. And so that was their first assumption,  

and so they prepared for Paul’s imminent demise. 
They watched him waiting for him to swell up and  

to drop over dead. But he didn’t swell, and he 
didn’t die. And so now they realize that they  

had made a mistake. Paul wasn’t a murderer 
at all. He was a god. They were wrong in the  

second count. At first they thought he was a 
murdered. Then they thought he was a god. And  

Paul was neither a murderer nor a god. Rather, 
he was an apostle of Jesus Christ fulfilling  

the mission that Christ had given to him.
And it calls to mind the long ending of  

the Gospel according to St. Mark where we read 
in Mark 16:15 the words of Jesus with respect  

to the great commission. Here’s what he said:
Go into all the world and preach the gospel to  

every creature. He who believes and is baptized 
will be saved; but he who does not believe will  

be condemned. And these signs will follow those 
who believe: In my name they will cast out demons;  

they will speak with new tongues; they will 
take up serpents; and if they drink anything  

deadly it will by no means hurt them; they will 
lay hands on the sick, and they will recover. 

And virtually every one of these signs that Jesus 
said that would accompany the apostolic mission  

and the spreading of the gospel literally 
took place in the life and mission of the  

apostle Paul. By the way, that’s the text that 
the snake handlers use as their proof text for  

their religion where they like to play with the 
rattlesnakes and so on. Paul wasn’t playing with  

snakes that day, by the way, just in case 
you’re inclined towards snake handling. 

But anyway, we read that in that “region there was 
an estate of the leading citizen of the island,  

whose name was Publius.” Luke said he “received 
us and entertained us courteously for three days.  

And now it happened that the father of Publius lay 
sick of a fever and dysentery.” We know what that  

disease was. It was a recurring illness called the 
bacteria Malta disease caused by the bacteria that  

was common to the goats that were there on the 
island. And Luke correctly diagnosed the problem  

that the man had, and in some respects it was like 
malaria in that there would be an acute phase when  

a person would be desperately ill from this 
disease, this Maltese Fever, as it was called,  

and then they would recover and maybe six months 
later or a year later there would be a recurrence  

of it and again in another six months. And through 
a person’s entire life they would be plagued by  

reoccurrence of Maltese Fever from the goat milk.
Well, Luke diagnosed it; Paul cured it. Paul came  

to the father of Publius. He laid his hands on him 
and cured him of this dreadful disease. And when  

the word of that spread around the countryside 
people with all kinds of diseases came into Paul,  

and Paul laid his hands on them, and they 
were healed. So we see that even while he’s  

a prisoner and his missionary journeys, so to 
speak, are over, Paul is still being used of  

Christ in the ministry of the apostolic faith.
Then what follows from this is a description  

of the rest of the voyage, and I’d 
like to spend a few moments on that. 

Verse 11 says “After three months we sailed in 
an Alexandrian ship.” Another Alexandrian ship,  

previous like the one that wrecked, and it was 
probably a grain ship, part of that fleet. And  

it said, “…whose figurehead was the twin 
brothers.” Now Luke tells us the name of  

the ship. It wasn’t the Queen Mary 2 or the QE 
2 or the (Von Dam?) or the Van Dom or any of  

the other Dom ships that they had in all American 
line, what they call. But every ship had a name,  

and they would take their name from the decoration 
or adornment that was carved into the prow of  

the ship. And this particular ship had in its 
prow the decorative carving of the twins. What  

twins? Not the Minnesota Twins. These twins 
were the mythical twins Casper and Pollux,  

who had their correspondence in the sky in the 
constellation that is called Gemini. Now of  

course nobody in this day and age believes that 
the constellation Gemini has any influence over  

the lives of people. Only those who ask each 
other “What’s your sign?” And only those who  

look every day at the horoscope to see 
what kind of a day fate has prescribed  

for them. But ancient mariners believed that that 
particular constellation of Gemini had particular  

jurisdiction over the sea and over mariners. Much 
like in our own day that patron saint of sailors  

is Saint Elmo. You hear of St. Elmo’s fire. In 
any case, Paul gets onboard this Alexandrian ship  

named after the twins from the Gemini, and 
all the good luck and good fortune blessing  

upon this ship, and it sets sail for Italy.
And we read here in the text “And landing  

at Syracuse,” which was the major 
city of Sicily. “Landing at Syracuse  

we stayed for three days. From there we 
circled round and reached Rhegium.” Now,  

Rhegium is on the toe of the boot of Italy. So 
they sail from Sicily, from Syracuse, to Rhegium. 

Now in order to make that journey by the sea 
they have to pass through the Strait of Messina,  

and the Strait of Messina was a very narrow 
passageway and was famous at that time for  

the treacherous waters that were there in that 
narrow passage. In fact, the treacherousness of  

that portion of the sea has been immortalized for 
all time in Greek literature and on into English  

history through the immortal work of Homer. You 
remember Home wrote The Iliad and The Odyssey.  

The Iliad was about the Trojan War and how all of 
these great heroes entered into combat with each  

other, and the second volume is after the Trojan 
War is over. Homer gives us the perilous journey  

of Odysseus or called normally Ulysses, back home 
to Sicily. Remember that? Some of you old timers  

remember the Hollywood move with Kurt Douglas, 
who takes on the Cyclops and the sirens and the  

furies and the fates and all of that business. 
Well, if you recall Homer’s story of The Odyssey,  

Odysseus has to sail his ship through the Strait 
of Messina, and he has to sail between—See if you  

remember this, now, from your literature.—between 
Scylla and Charybdis. You ever heard that  

expression? Certainly you’ve read it on more than 
one occasion. Any time a person has a dangerous  

voyage to take a difficult decision to make and 
there’s no easy solution, the simplified version  

is we say we’re between a rock and a hard place. 
Well, the ancient people would say we’re between  

Scylla and Charybdis. Scylla was this mythological 
monster dog who lived in a cave by the shore of  

the Straits of Messina. And the mythology went 
like this: If a ship unawares sailed too close  

to that shore, then Scylla would jump out of the 
cave and reach out and snatch one or two sailors  

from the ship, take them back into his cave and 
devour them. On the other hand, Charybdis, not  

an animal or a monster, Charybdis was a whirlpool, 
and the whirlpool appeared three times every day.  

And it was a violent whirlpool that if a ship 
sailed near that whirlpool got caught up in the  

eddy. It would then be taken down to the bottom of 
the sea, losing the ship and all hands. So if you  

remember the story of The Odyssey, Odysseus 
or Ulysses, was very careful when he sailed  

through that strait to favor the side of Scylla 
rather than Charybdis, because the worst that  

could happen on the Scylla side was he might lose 
one or two sailors, but if he gets too close to  

Charybdis, all hands go to the bottom of the sea.
But this wasn’t Ulysses, this wasn’t Odysseus,  

and there really wasn’t any Scylla. That’s 
silly. And there really wasn’t any Charybdis,  

just treacherous sea. And on this occasion 
between the two shores the ship that was  

carrying the cargo of Paul the apostle.
Moving from Sicily to the toe of the boot  

and then sailing again from Rhegium further on 
up the Italian coast that brought him to Puteoli,  

which was on the coast of Italy. And there they 
found, Luke says, “brethren and were invited to  

stay with them for seven days. And so we went 
toward Rome.” Let’s just stop for a minute. 

Finally after all of these hardships, the ship 
bearing Paul from Jerusalem, Caesarea, finally  

arrives in Italy. They are on dry ground now, and 
the rest of the trip will be on land. And Paul is  

finally in Italy, and when he arrives in Italy 
he’s greeted by Christians, because the gospel  

had already been published there in Rome. And Paul 
had written to the Roman Christians three years  

earlier, but he had never yet been there. But you 
remember in the first chapter of Paul’s letter  

to the Romans he made this poignant statement. He 
said “I make mention of you always in my prayers,”  

directing this to the Romans, “making request 
if by some means now at last I may find a way in  

the will of God to come to you, for I long to see 
you that I may impart to you some spiritual gift,  

so that you may be established.” Do you hear the 
passion coming through the pen of the apostle  

when he writes to the Christian community that’s 
already established in Rome? Listen to it again.  

He’s praying that if by some means, whether by 
shipwreck, whether it’s in chains, whether it’s  

by fire, I don’t care, if just by some means at 
last I may find a way in the will of God to meet  

you face to face. And now by the invisible 
hand of providence the means had been found,  

the way had been given at long last, in the 
will of God, for his apostle to go to Rome. 

So after staying a few days on the coast, they 
began the overland journey to the eternal city.  

And we’re told that on the way they got as far 
as the Appian Forum and then the Three Taverns,  

if you will. And you know the story of Rome—that 
Rome was founded in the same year that Isaiah saw  

the Lord high and lifted up, and that the Roman 
Empire lasted for over a thousand years, and Rome,  

the eternal city, was basically the central city 
of the world for over a millennia. And even to  

this day, when you go to Rome and you see the 
structures that remain from the early days of  

the empire, when you see the Circus Maximus, when 
you see the Colosseum, when you see the ruins of  

the temples, when you see ruins of the Forum, when 
you see the Mamertine Prison, where Paul spent his  

last hours, all of that is still there. But you 
know the old statement was All roads lead to Rome.  

Why was that? Because the Romans were famous not 
for their art but for their engineering skills,  

for their building skills, for making arches, 
making aqueducts, building roads. They didn’t  

build roads that had to be replaced every two or 
three years like ours. If you’d go to Rome today,  

you can still drive your car or drive your 
bus or drive your scooter on the Appian Way  

or on the (Ider?). Little bumpy, but the road is 
still there. In fact, one of the most fascinating  

experiences I ever had in my life, certainly the 
most memorable lunch I’ve ever had, was an outdoor  

lunch that we had on the Appian Way right near 
where these caverns are that Luke mentions here  

in this book. Now obviously it wasn’t the place 
where Paul ate on the way. That place isn’t still  

there. But to think of the history as we were 
sitting there right alongside the Appian Way. 

And that was Paul’s experience (as he?) long 
last he came to his destination. And you know  

how he got to his destination? We’ve talked about 
the ships. We’ve talked about the travels. We’ve  

talked about the beatings. We’ve talked about 
all of these things. Ladies and gentlemen,  

he came to his destination by 
the sovereign providence of God. 

What can you learn from this? Every person 
in this room who is in Christ has his own  

or her own manifest destiny. And God is 
going to bring you to that destiny. We  

don’t believe that our ultimate destiny 
is the hands of blind fate or the furies  

or arbitrary promiscuous deities. But this is 
our father’s world and we are his children,  

and he had appointed for every one of us a final 
terminal point, and he’s the one who will bring us  

there. And in the midst of the storm, in the midst 
of the shipwreck, in the midst of the beatings,  

in the midst of the pain, when we want to lose 
courage and we want to lose hope and think that  

the invisible hand of God has let us go, we 
remember his servant Paul, who took courage  

when his feet landed safely on Roman soil. 
That’s the God that we are here this morning  

to worship, who has each one of his people in the 
hallow of his hand. And he will not let you go. 

Let’s pray.
Our Father and our God,  

we thank you for this record, for this stirring, 
extraordinary record of how your hand was upon  

your apostle to bring him to that appointed 
place for his destiny. We thank you that the  

future that is unknown to us is known perfectly 
by you, and that our future is in your hands.  

There’s no place we would rather have it, and 
we thank you for it in the name of Jesus. Amen.
