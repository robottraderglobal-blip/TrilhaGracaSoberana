# The Demise of Puritanism: The English Reformation and the Puritans with Michael Reeves

REEVES: A very warm welcome back to this last lecture
where we're going to be looking at the final

demise of Puritanism. Before we got to John
Owen, we were looking at Charles II and if

you remember he returned from exile and began
a clamp-down on the Puritans. He re-imposed

the prayer book, forced all clergy to declare
that it contained nothing contrary to the

words of God. Many who couldn't declare that
were then, some two-thousand, ejected from

their ministries. And then do you remember
religious assemblies of more than fine people

outside the Church of England were banned
and public preaching outside the Church of

England was made illegal? That's where we
pick up in this last lecture; how the iron

fist of the government slowly strangled Puritanism
to death.

Now, times of persecution, of course, are
usually when you see the most stirring courage

displayed and that's exactly what we see here.
Let's take, for example, possibly the most

celebrated figure from this period, John Bunyan.
Now at the time -- we're talking about Charles's

return onward from 1660's -- Bunyan wasn't
known as a Puritan. We often call him a Puritan

today; at that time, he was called a mechanic,
not because he fiddled around with cars of

anything, but because he wasn't like invariably
Puritan preaches were. He wasn't educated

and so he was known as a mechanic. He was
a tinker by trade and a tinker was someone

who would mend pots and pans. They would walk
from village to village and if there was something

wrong with your pots and pans, he would sort
them out. This was a physical, demanding trade

and he was a big, muscular man with reddish
hair and exuberant personality and a highly

developed sensitivity and he, in this period,
had taken to preaching in the open. He wasn't

a Church of England minister ever and he took
to preaching in the open air.

Right by my parents' house, there's a woods
and in the middle of this wood, there's a

little hallow called Bunyan's Dell and I've
been down in there and I've done some sampling

myself. The acoustics are particular good
in there. And there he would preach. It was

one of the many places he would go and preach
in the outdoors and people would come from

miles to hear Bunyan preach, all illegally.
For this illegal preaching, Bunyan was eventually

arrested and he was confined to a damp little
jail cell on top of the river in Bedford prison

for some twelve years. There, he used his
time to conceive what is almost certainly

the Puritan literary classic, 'Pilgrim's Progress.'
Now, Pilgrim's Progress -- it's an allegory

about the journey of every Christian from
the City of Destruction to the Celestial City

but it's very reflective of Bunyan's own personal
experience.

So Bunyan, remember, he was a tinker. Now,
what that meant was he would wander from village

to village with a hefty 17th century toolkit,
all his daily necessities, and as well as

pots and pans, a sixty-pound anvil on his
back. He became a model for the great burden

of sin that Pilgrim carried on his back until
he comes to cross where it is finally loosed

from off his shoulders and he cries with relief.
Now, in prison, as well as writing Pilgrim's

Progress, Bunyan wrote of his own conversion
in a work called 'Grace Abounding to the Chief

of Sinners' and this work -- you see the similarities
to Pilgrim's Progress and Pilgrim's own journey.

This work of Grace Abounding gives us a highly
illuminating personal example of the sort

of introspective moralism we've seen Richard
Sibbes dealing with. And in Grace Abounding,

Bunyan describes how he used to despair in
his youth when he thought of heaven and hell

for, again this is so tragic, he said he believed,

"It was too late for me to look after heaven
for Christ would not forgive me." And when

he tried to do better, he said "My peace would
be in an out, sometimes twenty times a day;

comfort now, trouble presently. But," he says,
"one day, I was passing in the field, and

that too with some dashes on my conscience,
fearing that all was not right, suddenly this

sentence fell upon my soul, 'Thy righteousness
is in heaven.' And my thought, I saw, with

the eyes of my soul, Jesus Christ standing
at the right hand of God; there, I say, is

my righteousness; so that wherever I was,
or whatever I was doing, God could not say

of me, 'He lacks my righteousness,' for my
righteousness was just before Him. I saw,

moreover, it was not my good state of heart
that made my righteousness better, nor my

bad state that made my righteousness worse;
for my righteousness was Jesus Christ Himself,

the same yesterday, today, and forever (Heb.
13.8). Oh, now did my chains fall off my legs

indeed, my temptations fled away and, from
that time on, those dreadful scriptures of

God left off to trouble me now; and now I
went home rejoicing, for the grace and love

of God."

It was this message that permeated all of
Bunyan's preaching; preaching that apparently

was of such high order that not only did it
bring the crowds, but when Charles II spoke

of that illiterate tinker in the hearing of
John Owen, the one-time Vice Chancellor of

Oxford University, the great scholar replied
"Please, your Majesty, if I could possess

that tinker's ability for preaching, I would
gladly relinquish all my learning." It wasn't

actually his ability for preaching; it was
his message that was so exceptionally good.

But despite those late blooms on the tree
of Puritanism, Charles II's regime was attacking

the very roots of Puritanism with withering
effect, and it wasn't just the gagging of

the preachers; soon it was public law that
public offices could only be held by Anglicans

those in the Church of England and only Anglicans
could go to university. Now, it wasn't just

that that made the non-conformists, second
class citizens incapable of social advancement

and influence, the real problem was this;
the universities, Oxford and Cambridge we've

seen, had been the Puritan seminaries and
education and training grounds. And when the

next generation were barred from such learning,
the men of theological caliber died out, leaving

Puritanism to be an increasingly shallow movement
that would never be taken seriously again.

Puritanism, after all, had been a movement
concerned with words and with the word of

God and so when Puritans were no longer educated,
the muscle of the movement wasted away. And

worst, without strong biblical education,
over the years that followed, many of the

Puritans' followers, descendants, successors,
they began to drift outside of orthodoxy and

began to deny such Christian basics as beliefs
in the Trinity.

Now, because it died such a slow death, it's
hard to say quite when the Puritan era ended.

There was no final cataclysm, no last stand,
but there was still evangelicals in the Church

of England yet so many have been ejected,
gagged, suppressed, that the old movement

found itself evermore scattered and leaderless
until by 1700 or so. Nobody spoke much of

Puritans anymore. Instead, people spoke scornfully
of dissenters; an ostracized, impotent, second

class group that was easily dismissed. There
were no Sibesses, Goodwins, or Owens anymore.

Now, there are a few great ones since that
time who informally have been called the 'Last

Puritan.' So I think, for example, of Jonathan
Edwards, a great British theologian in New

England, who was very much of the same cloth
as the Puritans but he wasn't living in Britain,

he doesn't really fall into the same group
properly; Charles Spurgeon, the 19th century

Baptist preacher, again very much of the same
cloth but 200 years late. Different concerns

of his time, not really true Puritans. But
in another sense, if Puritanism was, as John

Milton put it about the reforming of the Reformation,
then to ask when the Puritan era ended is

really to ask when or if the reformation ended.

So as we finish this series of lectures on
a part of the reformation, I want to ask this

question: Is the Reformation over? And to
answer that question, well you have to know

what the Reformation was about. Now, it started,
do you remember, with Erasmus and the New

Testament, the New Testament being made available.
It was a bible movement. Have you wondered

this: Erasmus is never counted as the first
reformer, have you wondered why? Well, because

of how he treated the scriptures. You see,
Erasmus made the scriptures available to the

world, with that addition of the New Testament,
but he himself gave to the Scriptures or accorded

the scriptures little practical, let alone
governing authority. And the result was for

Erasmus, the Bible was just one voice among
others. It could tailored, squeezed, adjusted

to fit his own understanding of what Christianity
which meant Erasmus could never see things

really changed. The Bible wouldn't reform
him because it would always be tailored, squeezed,

adjusted. And to break out of that suffocating
scheme to achieve substantial Reformation,

it took Luther's attitude that Scripture is
the only sure foundation for belief, 'Sola

Scriptura.' It is the only supreme authority.
And the Bible had to be acknowledged as the

supreme authority to be allowed to contradict
and overrule all other claims or else itself

it would, as Popes did, overrule it; itself
could find that its message was hijacked.

In another words, a simple reverence for the
Bible, an acknowledgment that the Bible has

some authority was never enough to bring about
Reformation. It took Luther's view, 'Sola

Scriptura.' That was the indispensable key
for Reformation.

But it wasn't just the question of the authority
of the Bible. The reason that Luther started

the Reformation and Erasmus didn't was because
of the difference they saw in the content

of the Bible. So for Erasmus, the Bible was
really little more than a collection of moral

exhortations helping us to copy Christ, to
be like Him. For Luther, that was to turn

the gospel on its head. It displayed a naïve
optimism about our abilities. For Luther,

what sinners need, first and foremost, is
not an example but a Savior. And so the Bible,

first and foremost, is a message of salvation
and so salvation by grace alone, justification

by faith alone was what made the Reformation
the Reformation. It was what Luther and Tyndale

fought to make known, it was what Goodwin
and Owen sought to recover and keep bright

in their day. As Richard Sibbes lamented a
century after Luther, it was all too easy

to lose that controlling focus on Christ and
his free gift of righteousness and yet that

was the very heart of true reform. For all
that the Bible was opened without the message

of Christ's free gift of righteousness, there
could be no Reformation. And if that's the

case, if that's what the Reformation was about,
God saving us entirely because of his own

gracious kindness to be received by simple
faith, then, well, as long as the truth of

that gospel is diluted, for as long as justification
by faith alone is important, for as long as

the church needs to be purified in any way,
the Reformation is not over.

Now, the suggestion that the issues of the
Reformation might still be live, sits very

uncomfortably with most people today. When
the Reformation is spoken of in the public

moment of unnecessary division. I think most

Christians today would far rather say what
Dr. Samuel Johnson, "For my part, sir, I think

think. And it's not just that we lament the
ongoing divisions in the church, I think the

modern reaction betrays something that's more
important than Protestant-Catholic relations.

You see, to modern ears, the debates of the
Reformation sound like rather pernickety wars

worth squabbling of whether justification

is by faith, as Rome agreed. Would you have
to say it's by faith alone, as the Reformers

Is it that important?" Surely, people feel

that's just very prickly, over sensitivity.
And as for the strong language we've heard

because these are hardly small concerns that

some fussy little desire to cross the 'T'
and dot the 'i' of doctrine.

Here was what each of the great Reformers
and Puritans saw. The Reformation was not

principally a negative movement about simply
reacting to abuses in Rome. The Reformation

was a positive movement about moving ever
closer to the gospel as revealed in God's

word. And that is precisely what preserves
the validity of the Reformation for today.

If the Reformation was a mere bit of historical
reaction to a historical situation five hundred

years ago, if it was just a bit of 16th century
politics, then, of course, one would expect

it to be over. But if it's a program to move
ever closer to the gospel, it cannot be over.

Now, the state of things today testifies as

The doctrine of justification is routinely

that, given up or compromised.

And it's not just new reading of the Bible;

Bunyan's problem of being tortured by guilt

Now, that may not be God-related but it is

loved. They are not loved because they are
attractive.

Only this message of the counter intuitive
love of Christ offers a serious solution,

a profoundly relevant, beautiful, sweet message,

There is no wonder, that Richard Sibbes called
