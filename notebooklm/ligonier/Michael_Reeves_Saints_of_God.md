# Michael Reeves: Saints of God

REEVES: Holiness. Holiness, we have seen is 
of the essence of God's character. Holiness  

is also of the essence of the Christian life. 
"Be holy as I am holy," says the Lord. But just  

as God's holiness is commonly, perhaps even 
usually, misunderstood and so unappreciated,  

so what holiness means in the Christian 
life is very commonly misunderstood.

Perhaps the most widespread error is the 
idea that holiness is something that we  

clothe ourselves with. So, we work hard to 
wrap ourselves in moral purity. And it's on  

that basis that we stand before God and men. And 
it's those who get to excel at that holy behavior,  

they are the ones who can be known as saints, holy 
ones who belong in heaven. And so, many Christians  

have at least something of that lie buzzing around 
in their heads. They see holiness as this strange,  

alien quality that they must somehow achieve 
for themselves. And it's entirely up to them  

to achieve it, and only then, when they 
achieve it, will they be right with God.  

That is not how Scripture speaks 
of holiness in the Christian life.

Would you come with me to 1 Corinthians 1, 
and verse 2, to see how the Apostle Paul  

speaks. I'm going to read to you from the English 
Standard Version. And here's what the ESV says:  

"To the church of God that is in Corinth, to 
those sanctified in Christ Jesus, called to be  

saints." Now there are a couple of different ways 
to read that last phrase, "called to be saints."  

You could read that in an aspirational way—that 
Christians are called in the hope that,  

with the intent that, they will go on to 
become saints, called to become saints. You  

could read it that way, but that cannot be Paul's 
meaning here, because he's already called them,  

the church of God in Corinth, "those who are" 
(past tense) "sanctified in Christ Jesus." They  

have been sanctified, or made holy, past tense. 
In fact, when we read that phrase "called to be  

saints," you do not need the words "to be" 
to be there. They're not there in the Greek.  

Paul is simply saying that the Christians in 
Corinth are those who have been made holy,  

called "saints," or "holy ones." Now, that 
is quite something to say when you're talking  

about the Christians in Corinth of all places, 
because they were hardly models of holy living.  

They were worldly, immoral, confused on 
the gospel, yet Paul calls them saints.

And to ensure we are clear that this is Paul's 
meaning, have a look at 1 Corinthians 6,  

verse 11. Paul says of them again, in 
the past tense: "But you were washed,  

you were sanctified, you were justified in 
the name of the Lord Jesus Christ and by the  

Spirit of our God." It's just as it was in the Old 
Testament. There, the Lord took Israel, the fewest  

of all the peoples, not the most brilliant, 
and in His love, made them a holy nation  

set apart for Himself to be a light to 
the nations. They were not holy because  

they had acted holy, but because the Lord 
loved them and set them apart. And just  

so in the New Testament, holiness is not a 
state that Christians work themselves into.  

It is a state we already exist in because of 
what the Lord has done. To be a Christian is  

to be a saint who has been sanctified. In fact, as 
Paul sees it, sanctification—to be set apart like  

this—is necessary to being saved. If you have not 
been sanctified, you are not saved. Here's how he  

puts it in 2 Thessalonians 2, verse 13: "We ought 
always to give thanks to God for you, brothers,  

beloved by the Lord, because God chose you as 
firstfruits to be saved through sanctification by  

the Spirit and belief in the truth." So, we have 
been saved through sanctification by the Spirit.

What we're going to see now is how this 
can be true—that Christians are already  

saints who have been sanctified—because 
it doesn't feel true, does it? Because we  

know that we are not holy as we would love 
to be in our behavior, in our lifestyle.

So, what does this mean? How can this be 
true? Well, one of the marks of a Christian  

is that the Spirit has opened our eyes to an 
awareness of our sinfulness. And therefore,  

of course, we don't feel very sanctified 
because we're aware of the sin in us.  

We feel unholy. When we encounter God, 
as believers, we say, "Woe is me. I am a  

man of unclean lips." So, to understand this, 
come back with me to 1 Corinthians chapter 1,  

and let's just look at the last few verses 
from verse 28—1 Corinthians 1 from verse 28:  

"God chose what is low and despised in the 
world, even things that are not, to bring to  

nothing things that are, so that no human being 
might boast in the presence of God. And because  

of him you are in Christ Jesus, who became to us 
wisdom from God, righteousness and sanctification  

and redemption, so that, as it is written, 
'Let the one who boasts, boast in the Lord.'"

You see, the holiness that all Christians have 
is not something that they've worked at so that  

they might boast of their goodness and purity. 
Our holiness, verse 30, is in Christ Jesus,  

who "became to us wisdom, righteousness, and 
sanctification." All Christians are saints, not  

because of anything in themselves, but because of 
Jesus Christ. He has become to us sanctification.

Now in saying that, Paul is actually pointing 
us to God's great purpose in the incarnation.  

In the incarnation, Jesus Christ took 
our human nature in order to sanctify it,  

that He might cleanse, restore it. So, there's 
a beautiful moment in John 17 where Jesus,  

in His high priestly prayer, prays to 
His Father and says, John 17:18–19,  

"As you sent me into the world, Father, I've 
sent them," the disciples, "into the world.  

And for their sake I consecrate" or sanctify, 
"myself, that they may be sanctified in truth."  

So, from the moment He took our human nature, the 
Son of God was perfect, holiness in the flesh.

Announcing His birth, the angel Gabriel said 
to Mary, "The Holy Spirit will come upon you,  

and the power of the Most High will overshadow 
you; therefore the child to be born will be called  

holy—the Son of God." And from that moment, 
He lived a life of utter, beautiful holiness,  

resisting all temptation—pride, selfishness, 
faithlessness. He loved the Lord His Father;  

He loved His neighbor as Himself. And when 
He died, He died innocent, saying to God,  

"Your will be done," and showing to people 
that love—that greater love than which no  

one can have. He laid down His life for His 
friends. He fulfilled all the law in Himself:  

loving God, loving people. And therefore, even 
the demons confessed it. When the demons met Him,  

they said, "You are the Holy One of 
God." They recognized who He was.

And what I want to press into now is, what does it 
mean that Jesus Christ is the Holy One of God? So,  

I want to step back into the Old Testament to do 
an examination of what it means to be the Holy One  

of God. Would you turn with me to Psalm 15, where 
we read, "O Lord, who shall sojourn in your tent?  

Who shall dwell on your holy hill?" Now, what is 
the psalmist asking? What is this holy hill? Well,  

in one sense, the holy hill is a description of 
Eden. Ezekiel 28 describes Eden as "the holy mount  

of God," which is why the rivers flowed from 
it. It was a garden on a hill. Now, of course,  

Eden was the place where Adam and Eve were 
banished when they sinned, and so, driven out,  

they were barred from coming back up the holy 
hill into the garden by flaming cherubim.  

They could no longer be with the Lord in His 
presence because of these flaming cherubim.

But elsewhere in Scripture, we see the holy 
hill is also a description of Mount Zion. So,  

Psalm 2 describes the Lord's "holy hill," Zion. 
And there were cherubim there too. For it was to  

Mount Zion that David moved the tabernacle, the 
tent of the Lord, and there in the tabernacle,  

and later the temple, woven into the veil 
that barred access to the Lord's presence,  

were the guardian cherubim again. In other 
words, the Holy of Holies in the tabernacle,  

and later the temple, was a picture of Eden. 
And just as Adam and Eve were barred from going  

back up the holy hill into the Lord's presence 
in Eden, so the people were barred from going  

into the holy hill of the Lord's presence 
in the Holy of Holies in the tabernacle.

So, when Psalm 15 asks here, "Who shall sojourn 
in your tent? Who shall dwell on your holy hill?"  

It's asking the ultimate question: who may go 
in to be with the Lord and eat from the tree  

of life? And the answer, verse 2: "He who walks 
blamelessly and does what is right and speaks  

truth in his heart; who does not slander with his 
tongue and does no evil to his neighbor, nor takes  

up a reproach against his friend; in whose eyes 
a vile person is despised, but who honors those  

who fear the Lord." In other words, only a holy 
one can dwell with the Lord on His holy hill.

But that description is none of us, of course, 
because Psalm 14 had already told us, verse 2,  

Psalm 14, "The Lord looks down from heaven on 
the children of man, to see if there are any  

who understand, who seek after God. They've all 
turned aside; together they've become corrupt;  

there is no one who does good, not even one." 
So, who can go up on the holy hill? And so,  

we cry with David at the end of Psalm 14:7, 
"Oh, that salvation would come out of Zion!"

And then, in Psalm 16, we meet one who says, 
verse 8, "I have set the Lord always before me;  

because he is at my right hand, I shall not be 
shaken." Pause for a moment. That phrase "not be  

shaken"—those are the exact words used in Psalm 
15, verse 5, of the one who walks blamelessly.  

"Who may dwell on the Lord's holy hill?" "He 
who does these things shall never be shaken."

So, the one speaking now in Psalm 16 is the one 
who may dwell on the holy hill. And he says,  

"I have set the Lord always before 
me; because he is at my right hand,  

I shall not be shaken. Therefore my heart is 
glad, my whole being rejoices; my flesh also  

dwells secure. For you will not abandon my soul to 
Sheol, nor let your holy one see corruption." So,  

there is a holy one—and who is He? Ask 
any Israelite, and they could tell you.

Everyone knew who the holy one was, because there 
was one person in Israel who had it written on  

his head. You couldn't miss him. He was the high 
priest. And in Exodus 28, you might want to turn  

to it, we're told one of the most important parts 
of the high priest's uniform was a gold plate  

that was tied to the front of his turban that 
read "Holy to the Lord (small caps)." The high  

priest was the Lord's holy one, and only he could 
enter the Holy of Holies of the Lord's presence,  

past the cherubim. This is what it means to 
be the holy one. Now, of course, those high  

priests in Jerusalem were just pictures—a copy 
and shadow—of our great High Priest, Jesus Christ,  

the Holy One of God, the last Adam, who won the 
right to go back up the holy hill and dwell there  

in the Lord's presence. There is not one of 
us who is holy in ourselves who can do that,  

but Jesus Christ, the Holy One, has sanctified 
Himself for us that we may be sanctified.

And what that means is shown to us in another 
key feature of the high priest's garments. If  

you're still in Exodus 28, read from verse 15. 
So, we know he has this plate, "Holy to the  

Lord (small caps)." He also has a breast piece of 
judgment. Verse 15: "In skilled work, in the style  

of the ephod, you shall make it—of gold, blue and 
purple and scarlet yarns, and fine twined linen  

shall you make it. It shall be square and doubled, 
a span its length and a span its breadth." And,  

listen to this: "You shall set in it four rows 
of stones: a row of sardius, topaz, and carbuncle  

shall be the first row; the second row an emerald, 
a sapphire, and a diamond; the third row a  

jacinth, an agate, and an amethyst; the fourth 
row a beryl, an onyx, and a jasper. They shall  

be set in gold filigree. There shall be twelve 
stones with their names according to the names  

of the sons of Israel. They shall be like signets, 
each engraved with a name for the twelve tribes."

Do you see it? The high priest would enter 
the holy place of the Lord's presence with a  

breastplate bearing twelve jewels inscribed with 
the names of the twelve tribes of Israel. And so,  

he would go into the Lord's presence, symbolically 
carrying the children of Israel on his heart  

before the Lord. Just so now, our great High 
Priest carries His own and lifts them before  

His Father as jewels that belong on His heart. 
We enjoy the Holy One's right of entry, for He  

carries us. The saints are the jewels He lifts 
before His Father and brings into His presence.  

The Holy One has been sanctified for us. And 
as Hebrews 10:10 says, "By that will we have  

been sanctified through the offering of the 
body of our Lord Jesus Christ once for all."  

In Him, the Holy One, we are holy and 
brought in with Him. Where we have failed,  

He is our righteousness and our sanctification.

In other words, brothers and 
sisters, it is through our  

union with Christ that Christians are 
sanctified and declared to be saints.  

Now this is something so pastorally momentous that 
it forms the hinge of Paul's argument in almost  

all his letters. Let me quickly give you a couple 
of examples. Galatians 2, verse 20: "I have been  

crucified with Christ. It is no longer I who live, 
but Christ who lives in me"—crucified with Christ.  

That is, because of our union with Christ, 
we have died and entered a new life.

Or take Ephesians. Ephesians 2, verse 4: "God, 
being rich in mercy, made us alive together with  

Christ, and raised us with him, and seated us 
in the heavenly places with him." See again,  

Paul's whole understanding of who we are, and so 
the life we have is founded on being in Christ.  

We are in Him, and so we share what is His. We are 
brought with Him. Or Colossians—Colossians 3: "If  

you have been raised with Christ, seek the things 
that are above, where Christ is, seated at the  

right hand of God. Set your mind on things that 
are above, not on things that are on earth. For  

you have died, and your life is hidden with Christ 
in God." So, the reason you seek the things above  

is not to earn them by your seeking. You live 
not to acquire, but to live out the truth of your  

union with Christ—that you have been raised with 
Christ to new life. Your life is now with God.

Now, I have rattled very fast through Paul just 
to show you really how this is in all his letters.  

So, I want to slow down now and get ourselves 
clear. So, it is through our union with Christ,  

the Holy One, that Christians are 
sanctified, declared to be saints.  

What exactly does that look like? What does it 
mean, practically, to be sanctified in Christ?  

I've machine-gunned you with verses from Paul just 
to show you how this is everywhere. Would you now  

turn with me to Romans chapter 6, which is perhaps 
the chapter to meditate on to understand this.

Romans chapter 6: "What shall we say then? 
Are we to continue in sin that grace may  

abound? By no means! How can we who died to sin 
still live in it? Do you not know that all of  

us who have been baptized into Christ Jesus were 
baptized into his death? We were buried with him  

therefore by baptism into death" (past 
tense), "in order that, just as Christ  

was raised from the dead by the glory of the 
Father, we too might walk in newness of life."

Now here's the question Paul is answering right 
at the beginning. He's answering this question:  

"Are we to continue in sin that grace may 
abound?" In other words, if all my efforts  

to grow in holiness don't get me saved, why 
should I bother? Isn't that a question many  

ask? They think, "He likes forgiving and I love 
sinning, so why don't we both do what we enjoy?"

But that is a complete 
misunderstanding of Paul's gospel,  

because God has not just thrown out a declaration 
of forgiveness. The reason we have forgiveness  

of sins, righteousness, new life, peace 
with God, is because we died with Christ.  

What happened to His body happened to 
all the members of His body. In Him,  

we were crucified. In Him, we have 
received the punishment for our sin,  

and in Him, we have received the reward 
of new life in the resurrection. More,  

in Him, we have died to sin—raised to a new 
existence in which we are alive to God. And  

isn't that a beautiful description of 
holiness—dead to sin, alive to God. 
 
 

And yet, I wonder if you're feeling this. I think 
reading Paul like this is like being in a mental  

car crash. Our whole mental direction of thought 
comes to a shuddering halt, and we're left with a  

sort of mental whiplash. We're feeling concussed 
and disoriented by Paul, because we thought, "I  

am what I do." Everything depends on our behavior 
is what we thought. Paul says, "No, you died." And  

this is such a radically different way of seeing 
ourselves that we struggle to understand it.

And so, Christians very commonly subtly change 
what Paul says to get him to say something that  

feels more at home. So, we read what Paul says, 
and many Christians imagine that what Paul really  

means—if he'd written a little more clearly—what 
Paul really means is, "Live as if you were dead to  

sin. Pretend you are dead to sin, and that'll help 
you a bit—that'll help you become dead to sin."  

They imagine Paul is giving a command: "Go 
and die to sin." That is not what Paul says.  

He doesn't start by giving us something to do. He 
starts by telling us a truth: you died to sin. It  

is something that has already happened. He's not 
giving us good advice; he's giving us good news.

Now, of course, the reason that Christians don't 
believe what Paul says here is because we know our  

sin has not just gone away. And as you sit 
there, you're thinking, "Do you know what,  

I don't feel very dead to sin," because you 
think, "I still have vicious thoughts. I still  

have evil desires. Every day we go on acting 
proudly, selfishly, lustfully, greedily." Yes,  

we do. We just don't feel very dead to sin. And 
Paul knows that, and that's why he goes on. Read  

on in Romans 6, verse 12: "Let not sin therefore 
reign in your mortal body, to make you obey its  

passions. Do not present your members to sin as 
instruments for unrighteousness." This is why  

all his letters are filled with exhortations to 
fight sin: "Put to death what is earthly in you."

So, if Paul knows sin has not just gone away, 
what does he mean that we are already dead to  

sin? Well, the answer comes in verse 17. Romans 
6, verse 17: "Thanks be to God, you who once  

were slaves to sin have become obedient from 
the heart to the standard of teaching to which  

you were committed, and having been set free from 
sin, have become slaves of righteousness." Do you  

see it? Christians have been set free from sin. 
Now, once there was nothing we could do to stop  

sinning. We were slaves to it, and even when we 
tried to be good—actually what was going on? It  

was a selfish, self-dependent effort to show 
off how lovely we think we are. That's what  

was really going on. We were trying to show we 
can create life for ourselves. We're wonderful.  

We didn't give God the glory for all 
that wonderful beauty and goodness  

in ourselves. We were glorifying 
ourselves. And all the deeds for  

which we patted ourselves on the back were 
pure self-deification, self-worship—pure sin.

And that was us at our best. The rest of the 
time it is much more obvious. We followed the  

course of this world, the passions of the 
flesh, carrying out the desires of the body  

and the mind. We were slaves, helplessly 
addicted to our lusts and our selfishness.  

But then, "God, being rich in mercy, made us alive 
together with Christ." He sanctified us through  

the Spirit. He raised us up with Christ to a new 
life. And that moment, having been made alive,  

for the first time we believed, and instead 
of looking to ourselves as we always had, for  

the first time we actually looked to Jesus Christ. 
We'd never been able to do that before. We'd never  

been interested in turning to Him, coming to Him 
before. We used to be very alive to sin but dead  

to God. But that moment of His mercy, our eyes 
were opened, and we were made alive to God. Jesus  

Christ, for the very first time, became beautiful 
to us—desirable. We wanted to run to Him.  

Sin, for the first time in that moment 
when Jesus became desirable, sin,  

for the first time ever in our lives, became an 
enemy, not a friend. We were set free. Oh yes,  

sin still held its allure. It didn't go away, 
but it lost its mastery over us—its domination.  

We were no longer slaves to it. For the first 
time, we began to want holy things like we'd never  

wanted before. We wanted Christ, because for the 
first time we were alive—we were a new creation.

And this is the good news Scripture gives 
the Christian: you are not a slave to sin  

trying to be something that you are not. Can 
I say that again, because that is critically  

important. Christian, you are not a slave 
to sin trying to be something you are not.  

You are a saint, sanctified in Christ 
Jesus. You were in Adam, a slave to sin;  

but you have died to sin. You've been raised 
with Him to a newness of life. And if you  

are to live in holiness, you must realize this 
truth about yourself. It is a truth made real,  

not by anything you have done, but by Jesus 
Christ, so it is secure. And because your  

sanctification is made real by Him and not by you, 
it is a truth to be known by faith, not by sight.

You know, right now I'm experiencing some of this 
tension between what is true and what I right now  

feel to be true. So right now, it is coming up to 
ten o'clock in the morning, and you say, "Well,  

of course it is." But while that is the truth, 
my body is telling me it's three o'clock in the  

morning. That's the problem with jet lag. Because 
I have rapidly changed location, my body is out  

of step with the reality of a new location. Just 
so, all Christians have changed location rapidly.  

We've been taken out of Adam—out of slavery 
to sin—and we've been relocated in Christ,  

free from sin's mastery. And our bodies struggle  

to adjust to that. We are all 
experiencing spiritual jet lag.

But our new identity as saints in Christ—as 
holy ones in the Holy One—friends, it is as  

certain as Christ is alive. Let me ask you: 
do you believe that when Jesus Christ died,  

that there were two criminals, 
one on either side of Him,  

who died with Him? Do you believe that? Do you 
think that's true? Why do you believe that?  

Because the Bible tells you so. Many, 
many more times the Bible tells you,  

you were crucified with Christ. Christians, in 
Him you have been transferred from death to life,  

from slavery to freedom—and that truth is the 
foundation for all attempts to live in holiness.  

The power of sin in our lives is broken, not by 
our efforts. It is broken by Jesus Christ. And so,  

we are left, not attempting the impossible—to 
be what we are not. We are living out, as we  

strive to live in holiness, we are living out 
a Christ-effected reality. One old hymn writer,  

John Berridge, put it like this: "'Run, John, 
work,' the law commands, yet it finds me neither  

feet nor hands; but sweeter news the gospel 
brings: it bids me fly, and lends me wings."

So, know who you are, saints of God.  

Your calling in life—to grow in holy behavior 
and living—fits with your holy identity. You  

are jewels on the heart of the Holy One who 
has taken you into the presence of His Father,  

at whose right hand are pleasures forevermore. 
Because of Jesus Christ, and not how you feel,  

in Him you have died to sin. Sin no longer has 
the mastery over you. You are alive to God. And so  

now, walk in this happy newness of life. Holiness 
is the new air you breathe. Friends, holiness is  

the atmosphere of home. So, do not present your 
members to sin as instruments of unrighteousness,  

because that is not who you are. Why obey your 
old master? You've been set free from that tyrant,  

that slaveholder. Be who you are. And I 
am sure of this, that He who began a good  

work in you will bring it to completion 
at the day of Jesus Christ. Let's pray.

"He breaks the power of canceled sin, He sets 
the prisoner free. His blood can make the foulest  

clean; His blood availed for me. To God, all 
glory, praise, and love be now and ever given,
