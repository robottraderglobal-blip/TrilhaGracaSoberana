# A War of Ideas: When Worlds Collide with R.C. Sproul

SPROUL: At this very moment as I am speaking the United
States of America is at war. Now, it’s very

possible that when you hear this series of
lectures or view them on television, that

by that time the war will be over. And I certainly
hope that that will be the case. But we have

been told by the President of the United States
in these days that we should anticipate a

long and protracted effort, as we are engaged
in an unusual kind of warfare. We’ve been

told over and over that the United States
is not involved in a conventional war against

a particular nation or against a particular
ethnic group nor against a particular religion

and the adherents of such a religion, but
rather this has been defined as a war on terrorism.

Now, the question I want to address in our
first session today is: What is it that causes

wars such as these to begin? We look
over past history and the historians will

say World War I was caused by the assassination
of an arch duke in the Balkans, World War

II, at least the United States' involvement
in it, was provoked by sneak attack on Pearl

Harbor. We can go back even earlier in our
own history to the artillery attack on Fort

Sumter which provoked the conflict that we
call the civil war. And even before that we

can go back to the shot heard round the world
fired by a patriot minuteman in the state

of Massachusetts provoking the Revolutionary
War.

But this war, of course, was provoked, externally
at least, by the terrorist attack on the World

Trade Center in New York City and on the Pentagon
in Washington, DC. But here the historian

is focusing attention on the historical provocations,
the physical causes for the war. But where

wars really begin, ladies and gentlemen, is
in the mind.

Physical war is a direct consequence of conflict
that begins in the mind, a conflict of ideas.

Many wars have been fought because people
differed radically on what form of government

they should have, how that government should
be structured, how that government should

function. So that political ideology produced
actual, physical conflict. There have been

other occasions where war has been provoked
by how economic considerations take place,

how commercial activity is to be handled,
or how religious expressions are to be maintained

or in some cases suppressed.

We are certainly aware that in the 20th century
that reached a point of two billion people

who were in prison behind what Winston Churchill
described as an Iron Curtain. There ultimately

as the result of the philosophical reflections
of Karl Marx. So that these conflicts begin

with ideas in the mind.

Now, when we talk about the conflict of ideas,
what we’re talking about here is a collision

between worldviews. And when we use that term
worldview, we’re describing how people understand

their position, their significance, their
meaning, their place on this planet. Sometimes

we refer to it as a life and worldview. Where
basically there are three ingredients that

make up one’s life view. There’s the understanding
of God. There’s the understanding of man.

And there’s the understanding of the earth
or the world, which incorporates within it

an understanding of the relationship between
God and man and between man and this world

and between this world and God. These are
the different ways that we understand our

lives on this planet.

Now, since the attack on the World Trade Center
in New York and on the Pentagon in Washington,

there has been much public discussion about
the role of God in our lives. And we have

seen an unprecedented response of the American
people to prayer and public worship. We’ll

talk more about the significance of that in
the second address when at the current contemporary

understanding of the providence of God and
how God’s sovereignty extends over the every

day matters of human life. But while we’ve
seen this tremendous expression of concern,

renewed concerned for God and people flocking
together for worship services, we’ve also

seen on the downside a manifestation of American
syncretism.

American syncretism is that principle by which
it is assumed that though people have different

religions and different religious backgrounds,
that in the United States people are guaranteed

through the first amendment the free exercise
of religion. And we take pride in this freedom

of religion in our country that she teaches
and describes the principle that all religions

have equal protection under the law. That
the civil law of the United States of America

grants religious freedom to Buddhists, to
Hindus, the Muslims, to Jews, to Christians,

to Protestants, Catholics, people of all different
religious persuasions. And in the wake of

that guarantee of religious freedom has come
the idea, that is deeply rooted in the American

culture that because all religions are equal
in terms of their protection under the law,

that therefore all religions in the final
analysis are equally valid.

We have seen several community worship services
that have featured representatives from Catholicism,

from Protestantism, from Islam, from Judaism,
and a host of different religions. And the

news commentators tell us over and over again
how wonderful it is that people from all these

different religious groups come together to
pray for God’s mercy on our nation and after

all we all believe in the same God. And though
we are praying and carrying out our religious

activities in slightly different forms and
come from different traditions, at the bottom,

at the root we all basically are praying to
the same God and believe the same thing.

Now, that’s one of the consequences of American
public religion. But if one is a serious student

at all of world religions, even a cursory
glance at the content of world religions will

display that there are radical differences
among the various religions.

I saw a talk show recently that featured a
discussion between a Protestant Christian

and a Jewish rabbi. And in that discussion
people again -- the moderator was saying that

the two views at the bottom and then at the
end of the day both believe in basically the

same thing. And I thought now, that’s a
strange thing. It certainly keeps peace and

preserves American unity, but is there no
difference between an affirmation of Jesus

of Nazareth as the long promised and awaited
Messiah of Israel, is it possible that Jesus

could be at the same time the true Messiah
and Redeemer of His people and not the Messiah

but a false teacher and pretender to that
Messianic vocation. Those positions cannot

possibly both be true. They cannot possibly
be reconciled one with another. The only way

we can say that Judaism and Christianity at
root are the same is if we completely ignore

the central position of Jesus to the Christian
faith, where the first affirmation of Christianity

is the affirmation that Jesus is the Christ.
But you see that is a distinction that distinguishes

two great religions and it is a real difference.

But what about the idea that we all believe
in the same God? Again if you study the Christian

concept of God and put it next, for example,
to the Buddhist concept of God, you will see

a radical difference in their understanding
of theology. If you place the Christian concept

of God vis-à-vis the doctrine of the Islamic
notion of Allah, you will see that the Father

of the Lord Jesus Christ has very little in
common with the Islamic concept of Allah.

But what about Judaism and Christianity who
both come from the Bible? Certainly they worship

the same God. No they don’t.

This was indeed the dispute that Jesus had
with the Jewish authorities of His own day.

Ladies and gentlemen, again at the heart of
the Christian life and worldview is the concept

of God by which essential to Christianity
is the idea that God is triune.

And if there is anything that classical Judaism
categorically rejects, it is any idea of trinity.

So how could a thinking person say that we
worship the same God. When in fact our understanding

of God is on a collision course. And how you
understand God is the most crucial ingredient

and element of how you understand man and
how you understand the world. So what I’m

saying is the most significant element to
the construction of a life view -- a life

and worldview is your understanding of the
nature of God.

Now again, let me repeat this. Because what
I’m saying swims against the strong tide

of public opinion. What I’m saying here
is radically, politically incorrect, because

American civil religion teaches as its central
axiom that all religions are the same and

that everybody is worshipping the same God.
And what Christianity is saying -- no. We

do not worship the same God. There is a radical
difference among the various competing views

of religion. Now, step back for a moment to
the pages of the Old Testament. Consider the

experience of the people of Israel who entered
in a special covenant relationship to their

God -- Yahweh, -- who embraced monotheism
and yet wherever they were engaged with their

neighbors, they were constantly being involved
in this problem of syncretism -- that is where

they would borrow elements from the pagan
religions that surrounded them. And in so

doing they were violating the first commandment
that God gave to Israel through Moses, which

first commandment said what? “Thou shalt
have no other gods before me.”

Now, nothing is more un-American than to have
an exclusive understanding of God. And yet

nothing is more fundamental than the Biblical
concept of monotheism than the exclusivity

of the God of heaven and earth. Can you imagine
the prophet Elijah engaging in a contest with

the priests of Baal and after that great contest
at Mount Carmel, giving the interview to the

media who were there watching this contest
between the priests of Baal and Elijah representing

the nation of Israel. And Elijah speaking
into a microphone saying well, you know, at

the end of the day, I and the prophets of
Baal really are worshipping the same God.

We really believe in the same… and we do
it differently. Our religious activity is

not the same. There are elements in the religion
of Baal that are different from the elements

of the religion of Israel. And certainly the
God of Israel doesn’t mind. In fact, He’s

honored when we celebrate our unity of religion.

Can you imagine anything more foreign to the
teaching of sacred Scripture than that. In

fact, it has been said by Biblical scholars
that the greatest weakness that led to the

destruction of the Jewish nation in the Old
Testament was not a military weakness but

a theological weakness. Again what got the
people of Israel in trouble with God over

and over and over again was their blending
and mixing the faith that God had delivered

with pagan religion.

Indeed, if we look at the book of Romans that
Paul wrote, in the very first chapter we see

that the primordial sin that defines all subsequent
human sin is the human penchant for idolatry

-- for distorting the concept of God. And
that is true not only in pagan religion but

Christians can be guilty of idolatry when
they distort the Biblical revelation of the

character of God.

So we cannot assume at the outset of the construction
of a life and worldview that everybody believes

in the same God -- because they don’t. In
fact, there are few according to the Scriptures

that have a sound and Biblical understanding
of the character of God. And my question for

me is not -- does everybody else in the world
follow the true God but do I? -- am I consistent

in my thinking and in my living with the God
who is?

Again, as I said, how we understand God has
a profound impact on how we understand ourselves.

If it’s true as Judeo-Christianity asserts
that we are created in the image of God. If

we have an incorrect understanding of the
archetype, we will therefore, consequently

misunderstand and distort the type that is
based upon it. And so if we don’t know who

God is, how can we know what it means to be
in His image.

Now, one of the things that I’m grateful
for as a consequent of the tragedy that has

befallen the United States in the radical
attack against our culture is what I’m seeing

on television, as I stated the very first
day of this tragedy on September 11, the year

2001, when I watched those buildings implode
I said, “Right there marks in American history

the end of moral relativism.” This is a
deathblow to relativism, because the response

of Americans and the response of people all
over the world, after looking at this heinous

attack on human life -- the response is this.
This is evil. And everybody knows it.

Just recently I saw that same affirmation
made by a national news service, where they

put a bulletin --- a headline bulletin on
the screen during the newscast saying -- The

end of moral relativism. I said, “I hope
people will remember that.” Because you

can’t have such a shocking encounter of
pure evil and walk away saying, well it’s

a relative thing.

Here you see the absolute wickedness of this
kind of assault on human life. But in addition

to that assault we also saw with the implosion
of those buildings the practical end of macroevolution

as a defining theory for the human species.
Who really believes that man is a grown up

germ. Who really believes anymore that we
are nothing but cosmic accidents emerging

fortuitously from the slime and destined to
annihilation. If you really believe that,

when you see pictures of thousands of people
destroyed by an act of violence and terrorism,

it should make you yawn. Because the destruction
of grown-up germs who have no eternal significance

in the final analysis is no more important
than going out into your yard and using a

cherry bomb to blow up an anthill filled with
fire ants.

But every human being in America knows that
he is not a fire ant. Every person on this

planet knows that he is not a germ. We all
know that human life is sacred and that human

life is meaningful, which it could not be
if there were no purpose for human existence.

And beloved, we all know Dostoyevsky understood
-- that if God does not exist then there is

no purpose to human existence -- that we would
be as John Paul Sartre said -- nothing more

than a useless passion if God does not exist.
But if you understand the existence of God

and relate the existence of human beings to
that existence of God in your life and worldview

then you know that every human life is sacred.
And you know that it matters and it matter

ultimately if you are running around blowing
up people like were murdered in the World

Trade Center in New York.

Finally, the third principle of the worldview
is how God relates to this planet. That is,

how is God related to nature. In our age,
we have a tendency to view nature as functioning

independent from any government of God. If
anything has disappeared from our vocabulary

until such tragedies as these occur, it is
the concept of divine providence. Because

Christianity affirms that in God we live,
we more, we have our being. Nothing can move,

nothing can live, nothing can be apart from
the power of God. If you remove that understanding

of God from the equation, then you see a universe
that operates according to its own internal

fixed laws and the scientist replaces the
priest as the mediator of human existence

and of human meaning.

And so your understanding of God, your understanding
of humanity, inevitably results in your understanding

of nature itself. And we will look at that
in our next lesson.
