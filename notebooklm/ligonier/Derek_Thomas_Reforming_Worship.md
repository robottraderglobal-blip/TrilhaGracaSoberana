The reformation of worship—it's one of the major 
aspects of the Reformation—that Sunday worship,  

and in Geneva, midweek, noon worship, which was 
slightly different—less liturgy. It was really a  

preaching service, but on Sunday in the morning 
and at three in the afternoon, it was worship  

in accord with what Scripture had laid down, 
and nothing more—that there had to be biblical  

justification for every part of what you were 
doing in worship, either by direct commandment  

from Scripture, or by allusion to the practice, 
say, of the early church and how they worshiped.

When you worship here on Sunday—and I've been 
here on a Sunday when you worship, and I've heard  

Bob Godfrey talk about it so many times, you're 
worshiping the same way that the early Christians  

worshipped in the first and second century. So, 
you have this line of tradition that goes back  

almost two thousand years. And you're doing it in 
a different language, and there are more of some  

things and less of other things, but the elements 
of worship are the same. You preach Scripture,  

you read Scripture, you pray Scripture, you sing 
Scripture, and you celebrate the Lord's Supper  

according to the manner in which Scripture 
has laid down—the Reformation and worship.
