# Michael Reeves: The Priority of Worship

REEVES: This evening, we are going 
to look at what we were created for,  

what we were redeemed for. Now, to be created 
means to be given life. To be redeemed means to be  

made alive in Christ when we had been spiritually 
dead in the trespasses and sins in which we once  

walked. We were created and saved for life. But 
Jesus says, "This is eternal life, that they  

know you, the only true God, and Jesus Christ 
whom you sent." Life is found in knowing God. 

And this is what God gives us in the gospel, 2 
Corinthians 4:6, "God, who said, 'Let light shine  

out of darkness,' has shone in our hearts to give 
the light of the knowledge of the glory of God in  

the face of Jesus Christ." We have been created 
and saved to know God. And in fact, this is the  

very life of God we get to enter into, because for 
eternity, the Father has enjoyed knowing His Son.  

The Son has enjoyed knowing His Father in the 
fellowship of the Spirit. But you could get the  

wrong impression from that, because it's not as if 
heaven is a big celestial library where the Father  

does his research on the topic of the Son, just 
to get to know information about the Son. No, the  

knowledge that the Father and the Son have of each 
other is such that they delight in each other. The  

Father is well pleased in his Son. He loved Him 
before the foundation of the world. And the Son  

so adored His Father that He said, John 14:31, "I 
wanted the world to know that I love the Father."  

And we see a glimpse of this in Luke 10 where 
we read, "Jesus, full of joy in the Holy Spirit,  

cried out, 'I praise you, Father, Lord 
of heaven and earth.'" For here is a God  

so lovely, so wonderful, so delightful, 
He cannot be known without being adored.  

That is the knowledge of God that is life.
That is the knowledge we were made for,  

not a cerebral acknowledgement of certain truths 
about God. Jesus said at the end of John 17, "I  

made known to them your name, and I will continue 
to make it known to them that the love with which  

you have loved me may be in them. I make you known 
that the love that was in me may be in them."  

In other words, friends, we were made, we 
have been redeemed to know God in loving  

adoration. To worship God, to worship, friends, 
is not a means to an end. To worship is the end  

for which we were created, to glory in Him. 
And only when we do that do we know Him as  

He truly is. If you know information 
about God and yet you are unmoved,  

and you do not fall on your face in 
wonder, you are blind to His glory. 

Three hundred fifty years ago, Henry Scougal 
wrote a book entitled The Life of God in the  

Soul of Man. It was a book that later convinced 
George Whitefield of his need to be born again.  

And in it, Scougal argued, true religion is, how 
would you complete that? He said, true religion is  

not just a matter of orthodox opinions. You can 
have orthodox opinions and not have life. True  

religion is more than moral behavior because you 
can have moral behavior and not have life. It's  

more than a matter of emotional ecstasy because 
you can have emotional ecstasy and not have life.  

"True religion," he wrote, "is a delightful and 
affectionate sense of the divine perfections which  

makes the soul resign and sacrifice it wholly 
unto God. Desiring above all things to please Him,  

delighting in nothing so much as fellowship and 
communion with Him." That is what it means to  

worship in Spirit and in truth. It means to share 
that Spirit-given delight in God. The light of  

the truth awakens a rejoicing and wondering 
appreciation of who this marvelous God is.  

And that, my friends, is the life we were saved 
for. And that is how to grow strong in your faith. 

Romans 4:20, Abraham "grew strong in 
his faith as he gave glory to God."  

And that is the key to liberation 
from worldliness and the pull of  

sin. Worship God. Love Him more than 
you love sin. Glory in Him more than  

you glory in anything else. And that is when 
you will enter into the joy of your master. 

So, for us to get a taste of this worship, 
not just to understand, but to enter into it,  

let us go to the theological Holy of Holies. 
Would you turn with me to Isaiah chapter 6? 

"In the year that King Uzziah died I saw the 
Lord sitting upon a throne, high and lifted up;  

and the train of his robe filled the 
temple. Above him stood the seraphim. Each  

had six wings: and with two he covered his face, 
with two he covered his feet, and with two he  

flew. And one called to another and said: 'Holy, 
holy, holy is the Lord of hosts; the whole earth  

is full of his glory!' And the foundations of the 
thresholds shook at the voice of him who called,  

and the house was filled with smoke."
This is the worship of heaven. In the  

presence of the Lord, everything trembles. 
The foundations of the thresholds shake. The  

pillars of heaven tremble. Overcome 
by the tremendousness of His glory,  

the holy seraphim cover their faces to shield 
them from the unveiled sight of His majesty.  

The word "seraph" derives from the Hebrew śāraph, 
which means "to burn," for they burn with a holy  

love, the flame of the Lord. And they cry to one 
another in their worship, "Holy, holy, holy." 

Now, in Hebrew, the way to express intensity is 
to repeat a word. So, Jesus uses this idiom when  

He says, "Amen, Amen," or "Truly, truly, I say to 
you." Or you can see the same thing in Genesis 14.  

If you read the English of Genesis 14, it says, 
"Now the valley of Siddim was full of pits." If  

you read it in the Hebrew, it says, "Now the 
valley of Siddim was pits pits," lots of pits  

everywhere, pits pits. Here in Isaiah, we have 
a triple super superlative, "Holy, Holy, Holy." 

But what is this holiness that causes such 
trembling, burning worship? See, many Christians  

today, I think, find the idea of holiness faintly 
off-putting. The words used for holiness in the  

Bible have the basic meaning of being set apart. 
And actually, that's where our troubles with the  

word begin. Here's why. Naturally, I think I'm 
lovely. So, if God's set apart from me, I think,  

"Well, there must be some problem with Him. 
Why would you want to be set apart from me?"  

And so, saying God is set apart from us 
sounds like we're saying God is prickly,  

morose, sour. And that is a problem so many 
Christians have. They do not see or have not  

been shown the beauty of God's holiness. And so, 
holiness to them sounds like divine scowling. And  

Christians often speak like this. I wonder if 
you've heard this, Christians saying, "Oh yes,  

God is loving, but remember He is also holy," as 
if holiness is an unloving thing, the cold side  

of God that stops God from being too loving.
Of course, the reality is the opposite. The  

reality is I am the cold, selfish, vicious one, 
and God is holy, set apart from me precisely  

in that He is nothing like that. He is not set 
apart from us in priggishness, but by the fact  

there are no such ugly traits in Him. The great 
British theologian, Jonathan Edwards, wrote,  

it was before 1776, he was loyal. Edwards 
wrote this. He said, "God is God, and is set  

apart from all other beings and exalted above them 
chiefly by." How would you finish that sentence?  

"God is God and is set apart from all other 
beings and exalted above them chiefly by His  

divine beauty," said Edwards. For God's holiness 
is not just His separateness from sinners in  

His righteousness, because He was holy before 
there were sinners. God's holiness is not just  

His separateness from our creatures as creator, 
because He was holy before there was a creation. 

The holiness of God is the purity, the beauty, the 
absolute perfection of who He is eternally. There  

is no darkness in Him. There is nothing grubby, 
unappealing, abusive about Him. He is holy. See,  

our loves are misdirected, perverted. His 
love is set apart from ours in perfection.  

And so, the holiness of God does not moderate 
or cool His love. His holiness is the lucidity  

and spotlessness of the God who is love.
And this is why holiness looks so beautiful  

and loving in the law. Can you summarize the law? 
"The first and greatest commandment is to love  

the Lord your God with all your heart, mind, 
soul and strength. And the second, likewise,  

love your neighbor as yourself." And if that's 
what it means to be holy in the image of God,  

that tells you something very profound about who 
God is. Love God. Love your neighbor. That is  

being like the holy God. In fact, in the law, 
holiness is unpacked at considerable length,  

and the Lord was quite explicit that the holy 
character of His people was meant to reflect  

His holiness. So, he says in Leviticus 19:2, "You 
shall be holy, for I the Lord your God am holy." 

What does that look like in Leviticus 19? Well, 
if you read Leviticus 19, it's a description of  

holiness. And the first thing it means is, "Do 
not go to idols. Come to the Lord with peace or  

fellowship offerings for holiness looks like peace 
or fellowship with the Lord. Love the Lord your  

God." And then if you read on in Leviticus 19, you 
see holiness looks like compassion for the poor,  

being kind to your neighbor. In fact, 
Leviticus 19 says, "Love your neighbor  

as yourself." Holiness is loving beautifully 
so, purely so, for God is love. And this is  

the holiness that makes the angels worship. It 
is so overwhelmingly perfectly good, unspotted,  

wonderful, that they simultaneously shield their 
eyes and long, long to draw near and delve deeper. 

Later in Isaiah chapter 57, verse 15, the Lord 
says this, "For thus says the One who is high  

and lifted up, who inhabits eternity, whose name 
is Holy: 'I dwell in the high and holy place,  

and also with him who is of a contrite and 
lowly spirit, to revive the spirit of the lowly,  

to revive the heart of the contrite.'" Did you 
see it? The fact that He is high and lifted up,  

inhabiting eternity, the fact that He 
is great does not mean He is remote.  

Because His name is holy, He revives the 
humble. His holiness refreshes us. Oh,  

His greatness does not mean remoteness.
In fact, imagine for a moment. Imagine if  

God was just high and lifted up, but not holy. 
Imagine if God was all powerful, omniscient,  

omnipotent, but not holy. He would be a tyrant, 
a monster. The true greatness and magnificence of  

God is not found ultimately in his transcendent 
power, but in the holy character that wields  

that power. Power alone could be devilish, not 
glorious. Without holiness, we would dread God.  

But He is holy, and this is precisely 
what makes Him so glorious, so beautiful. 

The holiness of God is the luster, the 
wonderful splendor of the divine nature.  

It is the very blessedness and nobleness that 
makes Him so glorious to us. In His holiness,  

we see He is radiant in pure, perfect loveliness. 
And that brightness of holiness actually explains  

why all creatures must hide from it. I wonder 
if you know C.S. Lewis' little book, The Great  

Divorce. It's a story, a short story, all about 
holiness. And it all starts in the grey town,  

which is hell. And everyone there in the grey 
town is afraid of the dark. But they're offered  

a chance to get on board the bus to heaven for 
a brief trip. Bear with him. But as much as they  

fear the darkness, most don't want to get on 
board the bus to heaven because they fear the  

light even more. Well, some go. And when the bus 
arrives in the splendor of the heavenly meadow,  

one of the ghosts from hell screams, "I don't like 
it! I don't like it!" The beauty is overwhelming. 

Then, the solid people, the residents of heaven, 
arrive, at which two of the ghosts scream and run  

back to hide in the bus. Now, it is not that the 
solid people mean any harm. Far from it. They are  

there to help. But their heavenly splendor is 
terrifying to the shrunken wraiths from hell.  

Lewis is showing us sinners dread 
holiness, not because holiness is bad,  

but because the very richness, purity, energy 
of the pure life of heaven is just overwhelming,  

fearsome to those who cling to the darkness. 
Holiness is a pure and beautiful light.  

But light always exposes. And so, that is 
dreadful to those who fear exposure. Indeed,  

the uncreated splendor of God's holiness 
is overwhelming, even for the seraphim. 

But we can go further up and further in. To 
understand holiness and the worship that holiness  

fosters, first, to go further in, see exactly who 
it is that Isaiah looks at and the angels worship.  

"I saw the Lord," he says. Well, John 12 verse 
41 quotes Isaiah 6 and says, "Isaiah said these  

things because he saw Jesus' glory." Yes, 
we see the light of the knowledge of the  

glory of God in the face of Jesus Christ. That's 
where we see the true glory and holiness of God.  

You know, friend, if you try to think of 
God without Jesus Christ, you will only  

see thick clouds and darkness, and you will find 
yourself dreading God only. And you will tremble,  

but you will not worship. Oh, but Jesus is the 
rainbow set in the clouds. He is the one in whom  

the radiant glory of God is seen, and all the 
colors of the beautiful perfections of God are  

blazoned forth in Jesus Christ. In Him, in the 
face of Jesus Christ, there you see the holiness  

of God and the sight that will make you worship.
But in fact, we could go one step further than  

that. Isaiah said he saw the Lord sitting on 
a throne, high and lifted up. Now those words  

get repeated at the end of Isaiah 52. Where the 
Lord says, "Behold, my servant shall be high and  

lifted up." Those words are said at the beginning 
of one of Isaiah's songs of the suffering Servant.  

Shortly, just verses before how we read of this 
Servant who will be high and lifted up, will be  

pierced for our transgressions, crushed for our 
iniquities. Yes, the cross is where He is high and  

lifted up. For in that hour, when Jesus is lifted 
up on the cross, that is when His true nature  

blazed forth most brilliantly. There on the cross, 
the Lord was not doing something out of character.  

In that hour of His glorification on the cross, 
He was showing more clearly than ever who He is.  

And so, He would say, "When you have lifted 
up the Son of Man, then you will know I am." 

And there, high and lifted up on the cross, we 
see more clearly than ever what "Holy, Holy,  

Holy" means. Wearing His crown of thorns, there 
was the Lord of hosts, the King in his beauty,  

lifted up on His throne of grace. There He showed 
the holiness of one who came not to be served,  

but to serve. There on the cross He showed 
the greatness of His love, His righteousness,  

His patience. And in that exaltation in 
that hour, He was being so like Himself,  

that the centurion at the foot of the cross 
said, "Truly, this man was the Son of God."  

This was a window into who He is. That is the 
deepest insight. The hour of His glorification. 

You see, friends, when we consider God's holiness,  

it is all too easy to point people to God's 
grandeur as creator, which is right to do,  

but then fail to press on deeper and point to 
God's grandeur as a compassionate Redeemer.  

But on the cross, we see more clearly than 
ever the sweet and awesome holiness of His  

character. Above all, His creative power, 
high and lifted up, "Behold, your God." 

You know, today I am wearing a tie bearing 
the scarlet and gold of the ministry I lead,  

Union. Scarlet and gold for blood and glory. 
Union is raising church leaders around a world  

that is increasingly hostile to the gospel. And 
we want them to know, and here's where the tie  

reinforces the point, true gold, glory, is not

in following the crucified one through scarlet 
suffering to glory. But more even than that,  

I want them to know that the golden glory of 
God is best revealed in the scarlet wounds  

and death of Christ, the hour of his glory.
That, then, is to be the focus of their life,  

to keep them healthy, that is to 
be the focus of their ministry,  

lifting up Him to be high and exalted. For 
Jesus said, "When I am lifted up, I will  

draw all people to myself." That is the sight 
that causes worship, Him, high and lifted up,  

displaying all his magnificence. There we 
see the sight that elicits fresh worship,  

that refreshes and vivifies. Friend, 
if you feel yourself spiritually cold,  

that is the sight that you need to 
refresh yourself in. That is God's magnet,  

and the deepest insight into the very heart of 
divine glory. Because when you see Him there,  

when you see how perfectly worthy He is, then you 
get to say with the Puritan, Stephen Charnock,  

"Nothing of God looks terrible in Christ. To a 
believer, the sun is risen, shadows are vanished.  

God walks on the battlements of love. Justice 
has left its sting in a Savior's side. The law  

is disarmed, weapons out of His hand, His bosom 
open, His bowels yearn, His heart pants. Sweetness  

and love is in all His carriage. And this is 
life eternal, to know God believingly in the  

glories of His mercy and justice in Jesus Christ."
The cross, the sight that elicits worship. And in  

fact, I hope you've got Isaiah 6 still open. 
If you come back to Isaiah, you see it is in  

the place of atonement that worship is perfected. 
You see, the unveiled sight of God's holiness had  

made Isaiah cry, verse five, "'Woe is me! For I 
am lost; I am a man of unclean lips, and I dwell  

in the midst of a people of unclean lips; for my 
eyes have seen the King, the Lord of hosts!' But  

then one of the seraphim flew to me, having in 
his hand a burning coal that he had taken with  

tongs from the altar. And he touched my mouth and 
said: 'Behold, this has touched your lips; your  

guilt is taken away, and your sin is atoned for.'"
You see, in the presence of the Lord there is an  

altar. The altar is the place where the fire of 
holiness consumes the offering for sin. It is a  

place where the substitute is offered up for the 
sin of the people. And with that blazing coal from  

the altar, atonement is applied to Isaiah so 
that his guilt is taken away. And with that,  

Isaiah sees that this infinitely 
holy God is indescribably gracious.  

He is a God who offers pardon to the guilty. 
There before the one who is high and lifted up,  

Isaiah saw just how wretchedly unclean he 
was. God's purity exposed his impurity.  

So, he came to know himself truly as a sinner and 
cried, "Woe!" And in the same place, Isaiah came  

to experience God's gracious forgiveness.
Friends, that is how it is at the cross.  

For the cross is both where the Lord is high and 
lifted up, and it is the place of atonement. You  

see, without the cross, we imagine we're not 
that bad. We can probably sort ourselves out.  

If you fail to see the blazing glory 
of God in the beauty of His holiness,  

then you will fail to see how monstrous 
sin is. When God is small, sin is small.  

Only when God is high and magnificent will sin 
be vile and odious to you. And only then will  

you realize what we need is not just a little 
bit of help; we need salvation, a true rescue  

of the helpless. We need Him to do it all. It is 
at the cross that we see how serious our sin is,  

because look what had to be done to deal 
with it. We see, "That's how serious my  

sin is. And I cry with Isaiah, 'Woe is me!'"
And yet, like with Isaiah, there at the cross,  

your guilt is taken away. There you 
can abandon your self-reliance and rely  

wholly on Christ. At the cross, you realize, 
"I'm a great sinner and He's a great savior.  

You know, John Bunyan said, "It is at the cross 
that you properly sense two things. You sense  

the filth of your sin, and you sense that God 
has forgiven a filthy sinner." And Bunyan said,  

"That will make you both rejoice 
and tremble." "Oh," he said, "the  

blessed confusion that will then cover thy face."
Do you know that confusion? You go to the cross,  

and you see, "Oh, what my sin has done!" And 
you see His grace knows how great your sin is,  

but the grace is greater. And so, you 
find yourself weeping at your wickedness,  

you’re simultaneously repenting and rejoicing with 
sweet tears because your sin is so evident to you.  

But His grace is marvelous to you, and so you 
decrease, and He increases. That is the blessed  

confusion that Isaiah knew. In the presence of 
the Lord, he came to know himself. He was humbled.  

And at the same time, God was exalted in His holy 
perfection, goodness, and grace, and forgiveness. 

And that is what true worship looks like. We 
encounter the Holy One, and when we encounter  

Him high and lifted up, no longer then can we be 
the darling of our own hearts. When you encounter  

Him and see Him, He becomes marvelous in our eyes. 
That is the worship we were made to enjoy, this  

taste of heaven on earth. And I did say, "enjoy 
worship." For those who know this good and holy,  

gracious and forgiving God find themselves 
singing in the words of the old one hundreds,  

"Make a joyful noise to the Lord, all the 
earth! Serve the Lord with gladness! Come  

into his presence with singing! Enter his 
gates with thanksgiving, and his courts with  

praise! Give thanks to him; bless his name!"
For He is the blessed God whose holiness is a  

happy holiness. The Father is happy in knowing His 
Son. The Son is happy in knowing His Father. His  

holiness is a happy holiness, and to worship Him 
is to enter into His joy. It is to find a source  

of joy that is not dependent on our circumstances, 
but because it is Him, it is the same yesterday,  

today and forever. Friends, if you want 
stable joy, find your joy in Him who is  

the same yesterday, today and forever. In Him, 
we see what Zephaniah describes. Can you believe  

these words? In Him we see, "The Lord your God is

rejoice over you with gladness. He will quiet you

This Holy One is mighty to save. And 
He is so good, so kind, He actually,  

He doesn't just show grace to His people; 
He rejoices over them with gladness. Can  

you believe that? You can imagine Him flicking

"He will exult over you with loud singing." What 
a God! That's a God that makes you in turn rejoice

friends, if you would worship like this, full of  

wonder, love, and praise, Paul says the way to

It is the light of His glory that gives life.
