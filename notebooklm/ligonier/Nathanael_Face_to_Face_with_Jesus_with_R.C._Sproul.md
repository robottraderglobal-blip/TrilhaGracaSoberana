# Nathanael: Face to Face with Jesus with R.C. Sproul

SPROUL: When Peter Marshall was the chaplain of 
the United States Senate, he preached a sermon in  

Washington, in which he told the story of a man, I 
don't remember his name, I think it was Joe Dobbs,  

or something like that, who owned a little store 
in the city that sold fresh fish. Peter Marshall  

told of an experience that this man had one 
morning when he came to work, and he opened up his  

store, and he enjoyed the aroma that greeted him 
as he walked inside, the odor of fresh fish and  

brine, and all the rest. And he was getting things 
in order to open the store to the public. And the  

first customer that came in the door was a strange 
looking man in a blue surge CK suit. And Joe  

noticed something extraordinary about this person. 
He didn't say anything when he came in the store.  

He didn't say, "Good morning," he just looked at 
him with a piercing gaze, and said to Joe, "Joe,  

I want you to come with me." And inexplicably, Joe 
felt like he was drawn like a magnet that couldn't  

be resisted. He took off his apron, hung it up on 
the rack, walked from around the counter, and went  

out to the door, hung the "Closed" sign on the 
door, and walked out in the street with the man  

in the blue surge CK suit, and never went back.
Well, of course, that never really happened in  

Washington. Peter Marshall was playing a little 
game here with his congregation. He was trying to  

put into a contemporary setting the call of the 
disciples of Jesus to ministry. Where Jesus went  

by the seashore, and he found Peter and Andrew, 
and so on, and James and John, and said to them,  

as they were mending their nets, "Follow me." 
And these professional people, for some reason,  

forsook everything that was part of their 
lives, and they left their nets where they were,  

and walked away and followed Christ. Now, when 
we read of the call of the disciples in the  

New Testament, we are reading about people 
who had a face to face meeting with Christ  

and whose lives were never the same thereafter.
One of those episodes that I find particularly  

meaningful is the call of the disciple Nathanael. 
We read that at the latter portion of the first  

chapter of the Gospel of Saint John. I will begin 
reading in verse 43 of Chapter 1 of John's Gospel,  

"The following day Jesus wanted to go to Galilee. 
And he found Philip and said to him, 'Follow me.'  

Now, Philip was from Bethsaida, the city of 
Andrew and Peter. And Philip found Nathanael  

and said to him, 'We have found him of whom Moses 
in the Law, and also the prophets wrote, Jesus of  

Nazareth the son of Joseph.' And Nathanael said to 
him, 'Can anything good come out of Nazareth?' And  

Philip said to him, 'Come and see.'" You get the 
picture. Jesus had already called the first few  

disciples including Philip. Philip ran and told 
his friend Nathanael. He was all excited. He said,  

"Nathanael, we have found the Messiah. We have 
found the one who was promised to us throughout  

the pages of the Old Testament in the Law and in 
the Prophets. We have found the one of whom Moses  

wrote." Think about this for a minute. Philip is 
coming to one of his friends and said, "You know  

the Messiah that you were taught about in Sunday 
School, that you've heard about all of your life,  

and we all looked forward to? He is here and 
we found him." Well, Nathanael, of course,  

wants to know: Where did you find him? Who is 
this fellow? And he's asking these questions  

to Philip. And Philip said, "It is Jesus of 
Nazareth." And Nathanael's heart sinks. He says,  

"Nazareth? Nazareth? Where did you ever get 
the idea that Nazareth would be the place  

where we would find the Messiah? Nazareth isn't 
even mentioned in the Old Testament. I mean,  

you guys must be out of your mind, what are you 
on? Can any good thing come out of Nazareth?”  

Nazareth was the small insignificant village 
that had no rich heritage either religious  

or otherwise. And so the excitement quickly 
turns to skepticism in the bosom of Nathanael.  

Philip did the same thing that Peter and James and 
John did when they were telling others. He said,  

"Well, look, at least come and see. Let me show 
you this man, and then you come to your own  

conclusion. You make your own judgment. Don't just 
rely on my testimony. Come and see." If I can read  

between the lines, I can see Nathanael saying, 
Well, okay, you know, out of my friendship to you,  

I will humor you. I will come and see. That's 
the least I can do, but remember nothing good  

can come out of Nazareth. I'm from Missouri, and 
I'm not going to be quick to jump on this train. 

And so we read on. In verse 47, "Jesus 
saw Nathanael coming toward him,  

and he said of him, 'Behold, indeed, an 
Israelite in whom there is no guile.'"  

The Bible says very little about Nathanael, but 
what a wonderful description of a human being that  

Jesus makes of him. Jesus sees him coming. And he 
says, "Look here comes an Israelite in whom there  

is no spirit of deception." It is as if Diogenes, 
who had roamed the city streets of Athens with his  

lamp looking for an honest man, had finally found 
the honest man that had alluded him for so long.  

Jesus said, "Here is an honest man. An Israelite 
in whom there is no guile." And Nathanael hears  

this, and he is guileless, he's like an infant. 
I mean, he can't imagine how Jesus knows anything  

about him, because he had never met Jesus before. 
So listen to his response. "Nathanael said to him,  

'How do you know me?' Jesus answered and said 
to him, 'Before Philip called you, when you were  

under the fig tree, I saw you.' And Nathanael 
answered and said to him, 'Rabbi, you are the  

Son of God, you are the King of Israel.'" Again, 
this sounds childish, doesn't it? I mean, first,  

the man is skeptical, but he goes along with his 
friend simply because he's a friend, and he says,  

“Okay. I will go and see him.” And Jesus gives 
this cryptic statement about knowing him while  

he was still under the fig tree. And says a little 
bit about Nathanael having not ever met the man.  

And Nathanael is so impressed by this display of 
insight that he regarded instantly as supernatural  

knowledge, that all of the doubts and all of the 
skepticism vanished in an instant. And he makes  

this profound confession of faith: "Rabbi, you are 
the Son of God." And he's ready to follow Jesus. 

Now, some of you may remember the great 
entertainer, Al Jolson. I once spoke about Al  

Jolson in Chicago, and I was getting all excited 
about Al Jolson. And afterwards, the people in  

the congregation came up to me and said, "Who 
is Al Jolson?" And I said, "I'm really getting  

old when people haven't heard of Al Jolson." Al 
Jolson was a fantastic singer and entertainer. And  

his live performances were extraordinary. He was 
as animated as Sammy Davis Junior was. And Jolson  

used to get out there, and he would belt some of 
these songs out, and the people would go nuts,  

they would roar with their approval. And then he 
would come, and he would slide across the stage,  

and get up, and then his famous expression that 
he used over and over again was this, you've heard  

this expression, you maybe haven't attributed it 
to Al Jolson, but you've heard it. Jolson's famous  

line was this: "You ain't seen nothing yet." And 
then he would go on to the rest of his program,  

and wow them and dazzle them even more. Well, it 
was like before Al Jolson was ever born, this is  

how I read this encounter, this face to face 
meeting between Nathanael and Jesus. Nathanael  

see Jesus, and hears Jesus talk to him about 
himself in ways that Nathanael couldn't figure  

out how Jesus could have known anything about him. 
And Jesus said, “You think that is something, you  

ain't seen nothing yet.” Well, that's not exactly 
what Jesus said. Let's look at his exact words,  

but it's the same sentiment here. He said, 
“Jesus answered and said to him, 'Because I  

said to you 'I saw you under the fig tree;' do you 
believe? You will see greater things than these.'  

And he said to him, 'Most assuredly I say to 
you, hereafter, you shall see heaven open,  

and the angels of God ascending and descending 
upon the Son of Man.'" Nathanael, you haven't  

seen anything yet, but here is what you will 
see. You will see the heavens themselves open,  

and you will see angels descending 
and ascending on the Son of Man. 

I feel certain that Nathanael had no earthly 
idea what Jesus was talking about. That was  

something he had to think about. I don't think he 
had to think very long before he got the reference  

however, because when Jesus was speaking in this 
manner, which seems so utterly cryptic to us,  

he was clearly referring to an event of great 
importance that took place in the Old Testament  

that we read about in the book of Genesis. And 
let's turn our attention there for just a moment.  

If we turn back to Genesis 28, beginning at verse 
10, we read these words, "Now, Jacob went out from  

Beersheba, and went toward Haran. And he came to 
a certain place and stayed there all night because  

the sun had set. And he took one of the stones at 
that place and put it at his head, and laid down  

at that place to sleep, and then he dreamed. And 
behold a ladder was set on the earth, and its top  

reached to heaven, and there the angels of God 
were ascending and descending on it. And behold  

the Lord stood above it and said, "I am the Lord 
God of Abraham your father and the God of Isaac,  

the land on which you lie, I will give to you and 
to your descendants. And your descendants shall be  

as the dust of the earth," and so on, "but behold 
I am with you, and will keep you wherever you go,  

and will bring you back to this place, for I 
will not leave you until I have done what I  

have spoken to you." And then the Scripture 
says this, "Then Jacob awoke, and he said,  

'Surely the Lord is in this place, and I knew it 
not.' And he was afraid, and he said, 'How awesome  

is this place. This is none other than the house 
of God, Bethel. And this is the gate of heaven.'" 

Think about what happens to Jacob here in the 
vision in the dessert wilderness at night. He  

stops when darkness falls, because there is no 
inn, and it's not safe to continue traveling  

in the ancient world at night. And he just goes to 
sleep by the road, puts a rock for his pillow. And  

in the middle of the night, he has this vision, 
he has this dream, where he sees the heavens open  

and he sees a ladder connecting earth and heaven 
with angels moving up and down this ladder. We've  

all heard the children's song "We are climbing 
Jacob's ladder." What was the significance of this  

encounter that Jacob has that changes his life at 
Bethel, a place called "The house of God." I think  

this passage is extremely relevant to our day, 
because what I discern, as I go around and about  

this country, is that the collective experience 
of our people is not of a profound sense of the  

daily awareness of the presence of God, but rather 
as the philosophers of our day, people like Camus,  

and Sartre, and others, have indicated that our 
sense today is one of estrangement, of alienation,  

and a profound sense of the absence of God. 
The whole world is still waiting for Godot,  

we feel alone and abandoned, in a vacuous universe 
where there is no sensation of the presence of  

God. We are an extremely empirically oriented 
people, if we can't see it, taste it, touch it,  

feel it, smell it, we don't believe it is real. We 
have lost any connection with another dimension,  

with a supra natural realm. We are not all 
that unlike Jacob. Jacob whose father was Isaac  

and his grandfather was Abraham, who had told 
them stories of their own encounters with God,  

but at this point in his life, Jacob is certainly 
not indicating or manifesting any sense of the  

presence of the God, and he has this vision. 
Now, here is what he says when he wakes up,  

"Surely, God was in this place, and I knew 
it not." God was here, and I missed it. 

Now, what was the significance of the imagery that 
he saw in his dream? He saw a ladder. What's the  

purpose of a ladder? Usually, we use it as a 
tool to climb, to scale into a certain height.  

If I want to get from the ground to my roof 
of my house, I can't leap tall buildings in a  

single bound, I need to climb a ladder, so I 
get that ladder to connect me from the earth  

to a higher point of elevation; that's what a 
ladder is for. And in the Jewish imagery here,  

the ladder is the connecting link between 
the supernatural realm and the natural realm,  

between earth and heaven. And that's what Jacob 
was sensing, that there was no access. Remember  

Sartre's moving play, "No Exit"? He would write 
of this experience, he would write "no access."  

That's what we sense, that there is no access to 
heaven if there is a heaven; there is no access  

to God if there is a God. We remember again Paul's 
teaching in Romans in the fifth chapter after he  

spells out the work of Christ to bringing about 
our justification, and he talks about the fruit,  

or the results of our justification, he says, 
"Therefore we have peace with God," and what?  

"Access into his presence." But you see for 
there to be access between heaven and earth,  

between us and God, there has to be a bridge, 
there has to be a ladder. There has to be  

something that bridges the gap and that is 
a connecting link between these two realms,  

something that the angels can come down 
and go back up on. Jacob never saw it,  

he just had a dream. And his children didn't see 
it. And in a sense, the whole history of the Old  

Testament was a quest for the ladder of Jacob. And 
Nathanael appears before Jesus, and Jesus says,  

"Nathanael, you will see the heavens open and the 
angels of God ascending and descending," on what?  

"On the Son of Man." Do you hear what Nathanael 
is hearing? Jesus is saying to him, "Nathanael,  

you are going to see the veil be removed between 
heaven and earth, and you are going to see angels  

ascending and descending on me." What Jesus is 
saying to Nathanael is, Nathanael, you are all  

excited because I knew something about you before 
I ever met you. You ain't seen nothing yet. You  

are speaking to the incarnation of Jacob's 
ladder. You are speaking to the one who has  

been sent from heaven as a bridge to planet earth 
to provide access for the people of God, so that  

there can be a connection between this world and 
the heavenly places. So in these cryptic words,  

Jesus is foreshadowing, as it were, to Nathanael 
the whole focal point of his earthly ministry,  

because his ministry is to be that of a mediator. 
Jesus is called the mediator of the New Covenant.  

What does a mediator do? The mediator stands 
between two parties that are estranged,  

that are missing each other, that are not 
connecting. There seems to be no access  

of a relationship between the two, 
but the ladder of Jacob bridges the  

gap. And Christ comes to mediate between 
heaven and earth, between God and man.  

The response of Nathanael is somewhat different 
from that of Jacob. When God came in the midst of  

the night to Jacob, Jacob was terrified, and Jacob 
was astonished. Jacob was in awe, and he said,  

"Surely, God was in this place, and I missed 
it. I knew it not." When Nathanael meets Jesus,  

and the living ladder of Jacob appears, 
what is the response of Nathanael? "Thou  

art the Son of God." In a sense, with 
Nathanael in whom there was no guile,  

he saw what Jacob failed to see. He said, "Surely, 
God is in this place, and I know it well."

Have you ever sensed what I was talking about 
in terms of our culture today? A profound sense  

of the absence of God. We're told that 
we're living in the post-Christian era,  

in the age of secularization of the world. And 
to be secularized means to be de-sacralized.  

That is, we have ridden ourselves of the 
sacred, we've lost touch with the holy,  

and we live as if there were no God. I mean, we 
may say that we believe in the supernatural realm,  

that we believe in a creator and that we believe 
in a God, but in practice we are atheists. That  

is, we live as if there were no God. Because 
we don't know how to connect. We don't know how  

to bridge that gap between this world and 
the heavenly places. That's why Christ is  

so important, because he puts feet and arms 
and a face to the invisible nature of God.  

In the incarnation, this seemingly unbridgeable 
chasm is now bridged, and we have access through  

Him to go beyond this world and behind the 
veil and to experience the presence of God.
