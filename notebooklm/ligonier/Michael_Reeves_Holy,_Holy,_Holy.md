# Michael Reeves: Holy, Holy, Holy

REEVES: This evening, we are going to do 
something profoundly counter-cultural. We  

are not going to look at Seven Habits of 
Highly Effective Christians. We are not  

going to think about ourselves, or even the 
Christian life. As we start this evening,  

we are going to start by looking away from 
ourselves to our God. For Christianity, though it  

is the most potent force for transformation in the 
world, is not primarily about lifestyle change. 
 
 

Jesus says, "This is eternal life, that 
they may know you, the only true God,  

and Jesus Christ whom you've sent." Life is found 
in knowing God. And this is what God gives us in  

the gospel, 2 Corinthians 4:6, "For God, who 
said, 'Let light shine out of darkness,' has  

shone into our hearts to give us the light of 
the knowledge of the glory of God in the face  

of Jesus Christ." In fact, this is the very 
life of God that we get to enter into. 
 
 

For eternity, the Father has known the Son, His 
beloved Son, and the Son has known his Father in  

the fellowship of the Spirit. But it is not as 
if heaven is this celestial library where the  

Father goes and does some research on the Son 
so that He can know information about the Son.  

No, the knowledge that the Father and the Son 
have of each other is such that they delight in  

each other. The Father is well pleased in His Son. 
He loved Him before the foundation of the world.  

The Son so adored His Father that He 
wanted the world to know, John, 14:31,  

"I love the Father." And we catch a glimpse 
of that in Luke 10, where we hear Jesus,  

full of joy and the Holy Spirit cried, "I praise 
You, Father, Lord of heaven and earth." Here is a  

God who is so lovely, so wonderful, so delightful, 
He cannot be known without being adored. 
 
 

And that is the knowledge of God that is 
life, the knowledge we were made for. It is  

not a merely cerebral acknowledgment of truths 
about God. Jesus said at the end of John 17,  

"I made known to them your name, and I will 
continue to make it known that the love with  

which you have loved me may be in them." In other 
words, we were made and we have been redeemed to  

know God in a loving adoration. That is the end 
for which we were created, to glory in Him. 
 
 

And only when we do that do we actually know Him 
as He is. To know mere information about this God,  

but be unmoved by Him, is to be blind to His 
glory. Three hundred and fifty years ago,  

Henry Scougle wrote an influential book called 
The Life of God in the Soul of Man. This is a  

book that later convinced the evangelist 
George Whitefield of his need to be born  

again. And Scougle argued, true Religion 
is not just a matter of orthodox opinions,  

because you can have orthodox opinions and not 
have life. He said true religion is not just a  

matter of moral behavior, because you can have 
moral behavior and not have life. True religion  

is not just a matter of emotional ecstasy, because 
you can have emotional ecstasy and not have life.  

"True religion," wrote Scougle, "is a delightful 
and affectionate sense of the divine perfections,  

which makes the soul resign and sacrifice 
itself entirely unto Him, desiring above  

all things to please Him, delighting in nothing 
so much as fellowship and communion with Him,  

and being ready to do or suffer anything 
for His sake or at His pleasure." 
 
 

This is true religion. It is to share 
the Spirit-given delight in God.  

The light of the truth awakens a 
rejoicing, wondering, worshiping,  

appreciation of who He is. And friends, that 
is the life we were made and saved for. 
 
 

This is how to grow strong in your faith.  

Romans 4, verse 20, "Abraham grew strong in his 
faith as he gave glory to God." That is the key to  

liberation from worldliness: worship God. Glory 
in Him more than you glory in anything else,  

and then you will enter into 
the joy of your master. So,  

for us to get a taste of this knowledge 
of God, let us go to encounter Him in the  

theological Holy of Holies. Would you 
turn with me to Isaiah chapter 6? 
 
 

"In the year that King Uzziah died, I saw the Lord 
sitting upon a throne high and lifted up and the  

train of his robe filled the temple. Above him 
stood the seraphim. Each had six wings. With two,  

he covered his face. With two, he covered his 
feet, and with two, he flew. And one called  

to another and said, 'Holy, holy, holy is the 
Lord (small caps) of hosts. The whole earth is  

full of his glory.' And the foundations of the 
threshold shook at the voice of him who called,  

and the house was filled with smoke." This 
is what it looks like to be in the presence  

of God. In the presence of the Lord, everything 
trembles. The foundations of the thresholds shake,  

the pillars of heaven tremble, overcome by the 
tremendousness of His glory. The very seraphim  

cover their faces to shield themselves from the 
overwhelming sight of God's unveiled majesty. 
 
 

The word "seraph" comes from the Hebrew 
saraph, "to burn," for these seraphim  

burn with a holy love, the flame of the Lord, 
and they cry to one another in their worship,  

"Holy, holy, holy." Now in Hebrew, the way 
to express intensity is to repeat a word.  

Jesus uses this idiom when He says, 
for example, "Truly, truly," or,  

"Amen, amen, I say to you." Or you can see 
the same thing in Genesis 14. In Genesis 14,  

the English reads, "Now the Valley of Siddim 
was full of pits," but in the Hebrew, it says,  

"Now the Valley of Siddim was pits, pits," lots 
of pits; pits, everywhere. So, "pits, pits,"  

double the word. But here in Isaiah, we have a 
triple, super superlative, "Holy, holy, holy." 
 
 

But what is this holiness that causes 
such trembling, burning worship? See, many  

Christians today, I think, find the concept of 
holiness faintly off-putting. The words used for  

holiness in the Bible have the basic meaning of 
being "set apart." But actually, that's where our  

trouble starts, with the idea of holiness, because 
naturally, I think I'm lovely. So, if God is set  

apart from me, I think, "What's His problem?" I 
assume the problem is with Him. And so, saying  

that God is set apart sounds like we're saying 
God is prickly, morose, sour. Now, that is a  

problem too many have. They do not see or have not 
been shown the beauty of God in His holiness. 
 
 

Holiness to them sounds like nothing but 
divine scowling. And Christians, therefore,  

sometimes speak like this. And they say things 
like, does this sound familiar? They say, "Oh,  

yes, God is loving, but remember, He is also 
holy." And you can hear it in their tone,  

what they're meaning. It's as if 
holiness is an unloving thing that  

stops God from getting carried 
away and getting too loving. 
 
 

Of course, the reality is we are the 
cold, selfish, vicious, unloving ones,  

and God is holy, set apart from us precisely in 
that He is not like that. He's not set apart from  

us in priggishness, but by the fact that there are 
no such ugly traits in Him as there are in us. 
 
 

"God is God," wrote Jonathan Edwards, "and He 
is set apart from," this is holiness language,  

"He is set apart from all other 
beings and exalted above them,  

chiefly by," how would you finish that sentence? 
"God is set apart from all other beings,  

chiefly by," Edwards says, "He is set apart 
from all other beings and exalted above them,  

chiefly by His divine beauty." For God's 
holiness is not just His separateness from  

us sinners in His righteousness, because He was 
holy before there were sinners. His holiness is  

not just His separateness from us creatures as 
creator, because He was holy before there was  

a creation. The holiness of the triune God is 
the perfection, the beauty, the absolute purity  

of who He is. There is no darkness in Him. 
There is nothing grubby or abusive about Him,  

and thus He is holy. Our loves are all crooked 
and perverse, but His love is set apart from  

ours in perfection. And so, the holiness of 
God does not moderate or cool His love. 
 
 

His holiness is the lucidity and spotlessness of 
the triune God, who is eternally love. And this  

is why holiness looks so beautiful, so loving, in 
the law. Think of the law, and how it describes  

holiness. What is the greatest command? "You 
shall love the Lord (small caps) your God  

with all your heart," and the second, 
"Love your neighbor as yourself." 
 
 

Now, if that's what it means to be rightly aligned 
in the image of God, that says something profound  

about who God is. "Love the Lord your God. Love 
your neighbor as yourself." That's being like the  

Holy God. And in the law, God was quite explicit 
that the holy character of the people was meant  

to reflect the holy character of their God. 
Leviticus 19:2, "You shall be holy, for the  

Lord (small caps) your God is holy. I the Lord 
(small caps) your God am holy," He told them. 
 
 

And as you look at Leviticus 19, as the verses go 
on, what does holiness look like in Leviticus 19?  

Well, the first couple of verses shows holiness 
looks like not turning to idols, but instead,  

this is verses 3 and 4 of Leviticus 19, coming 
to the Lord with peace or fellowship offerings.  

Holiness looks like peace or fellowship 
with the Lord. "Love the Lord your God."  

And you read on later in Leviticus 19, and you 
see holiness looks like compassion for the poor,  

being good, kind to your neighbor. 
In other words, holiness looks like,  

"Love your neighbor as yourself," as Leviticus 
19 says. Holiness is loving, for God is love,  

and this is the holiness that 
makes the angels worship. 
 
 

It is so overwhelmingly wonderful that they 
shield their eyes, and yet at the same time,  

they want to lean in closer, to draw near. 
Later in Isaiah, Isaiah 57:15, the Lord says,  

"For thus says the one who is high and lifted 
up, who inhabits eternity, whose name is holy,  

'I dwell in the high and holy place and also 
with him who is contrite and lowly in spirit,  

to revive the spirit of the lowly, to 
revive the heart of the contrite.'"  

Do you see? The fact that He is high 
and lifted up inhabiting eternity,  

the fact that He is great does not mean 
that He is remote. Because His name is holy,  

He revives the humble. His Holiness refreshes 
us. His greatness does not mean remoteness. 
 
 

In fact, imagine, imagine if 
God was just high and lifted up,  

but not holy. Imagine if God was all powerful, 
omniscient, omnipotent, but not holy.  

He would be a tyrant, a monster. No, the true 
greatness and magnificence of God is not found  

ultimately in His transcendent power, but 
in the holy character that wields that  

power. Without holiness, power alone would 
be devilish, not glorious. Without holiness,  

we would only dread God, but He is holy, and 
this is what makes Him so glorious and beautiful.  

The holiness of God is the luster, the splendor 
of the divine nature. It is the very blessedness  

and nobleness that makes Him so glorious to 
us. In His Holiness, we see He is radiant in  

pure and perfect loveliness. He is the light 
of heaven who drives away all darkness. 
 
 

But that brightness is actually 
exactly why all creatures hide from it.  

I wonder if you know C.S. Lewis' short story, 
The Great Divorce. In The Great Divorce, which  

is a story all about holiness, it starts in "the 
gray town," hell, and in hell, all the residents  

there are, they're afraid of the dark, but they're 
offered the chance to aboard a bus to heaven. Yet  

they fear going to heaven, because they fear the 
light even more. Well, some get on board the bus  

to heaven, but when they get there, to the beauty 
of the heavenly meadow, one of the ghosts from  

hell screams, "I don't like it! I don't like it in 
heaven!" And then the solid people, the residents  

of heaven appear, at which we read, two of the 
ghosts scream and run for the bus for shelter. 
 
 

Now, it is not that those solid people, 
the residents of heaven perfected in glory;  

it is not that they actually mean any harm 
to those who are coming from hell. It is that  

their very splendor is terrifying to the shrunken 
wraiths from hell. Lewis is showing sinners dread  

holiness, not because holiness is a bad thing, 
but because the richness, and energy, and purity  

of the pure life of heaven is so overwhelming 
and fearful to those who cling to the darkness.  

Holiness is a pure, beautiful light, but it 
is dreadful to those who fear exposure. 
 
 

In fact, its uncreated splendor is overwhelming, 
even for the seraphim in Isaiah 6. But we can go  

further up, and further in to understand 
holiness. So, still see in Isaiah 6,  

who is it that Isaiah sees? Who is it that the 
angels worship? "I saw the Lord," he said. Well,  

John 12, verse 41 quotes Isaiah 6, and says, 
"Isaiah said these things because he saw Jesus'  

glory." Yes, "we see the light of the knowledge of 
the glory of God in the face of Jesus Christ." 
 
 

Friends, if you try to think about God 
without knowing Him through Jesus Christ,  

you will see only thick clouds and 
darkness. And what will happen is  

you will tremble before God, but 
you will not rejoice and love Him.  

God will seem dreadful to you, but Jesus is 
the rainbow set in the cloud. He is the one  

in whom the radiant glory of God is seen, and 
all the colors of the beautiful perfections  

of God are blazoned forth. In Him, you see 
the sight that makes you rejoice in God. 
 
 

In fact, we can go one step further. Isaiah said 
he "saw the Lord sitting upon a throne high and  

lifted up." Now those words get repeated at the 
end of Isaiah 52 where the Lord says, "Behold,  

my servant shall be high and lifted up." And 
this is the beginning of one of Isaiah's songs  

of the Suffering Servant. The One who is high and 
lifted up will be pierced for our transgressions,  

crushed for our iniquities. Yes, the cross 
is where He is high and lifted up. 
 
 

For in that hour when Jesus 
is lifted up on the cross,  

then His true nature blazed forth most 
brilliantly. And there you see the Lord  

was not doing something uncharacteristic. In 
that hour of His glorification on the cross,  

He was being most truly Himself, so truly 
Himself, so clearly showing just who He  

is. He said, "When you have lifted up the 
Son of Man, then you will know I am." 
 
 

And there, high and lifted up, exalted 
on the cross, we see what "holy, holy,  

holy" means. Wearing His crown of thorns, there 
was the Lord of hosts, the king in His beauty,  

on His throne of grace. There He showed the 
beauty of one who came not to be served,  

but to serve, who lay Himself down 
that we might be lifted up. 
 
 

And in that exaltation, high and lifted up, He 
was not being different to who He had always been  

in His character. He was being so truly Himself 
that the centurion at the foot of the cross said,  

"Truly, this man was the Son of God." You see, 
when we consider God's holiness, it's all too easy  

to point people to God's grandeur as creator, 
which is right to do, but then fail to point  

people to God's great grandeur as a compassionate 
savior. But on the cross, we see more clearly  

than ever the sweet and awesome holiness of His 
character. We see it above all His creative power.  

There is the hour of His glorification, 
high and lifted up. Behold your God. 
 
 

This evening, I'm wearing a tie that bears 
the scarlet and gold of the ministry I  

lead, Union. Union, as we heard earlier, we're 
raising up leaders around the world, including  

here in Malaysia, and the tie is part of a message 
we want all our church leaders we're raising to  

hear, that true glory, gold, is not found in 
comfort or worldly success. It is found in  

following the crucified one through the scarlet of 
suffering to glory. So, gold and scarlet. But more  

than that, we want them to know that the golden 
glory of God is best seen in the blood and wounds  

and death of Christ. That is to be the focus 
of their life and the focus of their ministry.  

For there, there is the sight that makes 
us rejoice in knowing God. There is the  

sight that refreshes us, that vivifies us. He 
said, "I, when I'm lifted up from the earth,  

will draw all people to Myself." For the cross is 
God's magnet, the deepest insight into the very  

heart of divine glory. For when we see Him there, 
we see how perfectly worthy, beautiful He is. 
 
 

The Puritan, Steven Charnock, wrote, "Nothing 
of God looks terrible in Christ to a believer.  

The sun is risen. Shadows are vanished. God 
walks upon the battlements of love. Justice  

has left its sting in a savior's side. The law is 
disarmed. Weapons out of his hand, his bosom open,  

his bowels yearn, his heart pants. Sweetness and 
love is in all his carriage. And this is life  

eternal, to know God believingly in the glories 
of His mercy and justice in Jesus Christ." 
 
 

In fact, if we come back to Isaiah, we see this 
place of encountering God, of seeing His Holiness,  

is the place of atonement. You see, the unveiled 
sight of God's holiness had made Isaiah cry in  

verse 5, "Woe is me. I am undone. I am 
lost, for I am a man of unclean lips,  

and I dwell in the midst of a people of 
unclean lips, and my eyes have seen the King,  

the Lord (small caps) of hosts. But then one of 
the seraphim flew to me, having in his hand a  

burning coal that he'd taken with tongs from 
the altar, and he touched my mouth and said,  

'Behold, this has touched your lips. Your guilt 
is taken away and your sin atoned for.'"  
 
 

In the presence of the Lord, high and lifted 
up there is an altar, the place where the fire  

of holiness consumes the offering for sin. It 
is a place where the substitute is offered up  

for the sin of the people. And with that blazing 
coal from the altar, that atonement is applied  

to Isaiah so that his guilt is taken away. And 
so, Isaiah sees that this infinitely holy God  

is indescribably gracious in His holiness. He 
is a God who offers pardon to the guilty. 
 
 

There before the one high and lifted up,  

Isaiah saw just how wretchedly unclean he 
was. God's purity exposed his impurity.  

So there, in the light of God's holiness, he 
came to know himself as a sinner. And there,  

in the very same place, Isaiah came to experience 
God's forgiveness. There, that is how it is at  

the cross, the place where the Lord is high 
and lifted up, the place of atonement. 
 
 

See, without the cross, we imagine we're not that 
bad. We can sort ourselves out. If you fail to see  

the blazing glory of God in the beauty of His 
holiness, you will fail to see how repulsive,  

how monstrous sin is. When God is small, sin is 
small, and when sin is a small problem, it doesn't  

take much to sort it out, and then salvation 
could be something where me and God work together,  

and He helps me a little bit to sort myself 
out. He helps the weak be a little bit stronger.  

Only when God is high and magnificent, and we see 
that sin against Him that is so deep and so vile,  

only then can we see salvation must be a true, 
complete rescue of the helpless. It is at the  

cross we see how offensive, how serious our sin 
must be, and so there we cry, "Woe is me."  
 
 

And yet, there at the cross, 
your guilt is taken away.  

There you can abandon your self-reliance and 
throw yourself entirely on the all-sufficiency  

of Jesus Christ. Only when you recognize 
yourself as a great sinner can you enjoy  

Christ as a great savior. John Bunyan said, 
the man who wrote Pilgrim's Progress, he said,  

"At the cross, there you properly sense 
the filth of your sin, and at the cross,  

you sense that God has forgiven a filthy sinner, 
and that double realization," he said, "will make  

you both rejoice and tremble. Oh, the blessed 
confusion that will cover thy face," he said. 
 
 

Friend, do you know that blessed confusion? 
Have you experienced that, you go to the cross  

and God's grace and kindness shown you there 
makes you weep more deeply at your wickedness.  

You realize how vile it is. You're weeping at your 
wickedness, you're finding yourself repenting and  

yet rejoicing with sweet tears because His mercy 
is showing how vile, how wretched your sin is,  

and the wretchedness of your sin is showing 
how great His grace is to deal with that. 
 
 

This is the blessed confusion that Isaiah knew. In 
the presence of the Lord, he came to know himself.  

He found himself humbled, and at the same time, 
God was exalted in His holy perfection, goodness,  

grace, forgiveness. That is what true knowledge 
of God looks like. We encounter the holy one,  

and when we encounter Him, then no longer 
can we be the darling of our own hearts,  

but then He becomes marvelous in our eyes. 
This is what we were made to enjoy: our God,  

to enjoy Him. To gaze upon the beauty of His 
holiness is to taste heaven on earth now,  

and yes, I said in joy, because 
those who know this good and holy,  

gracious and forgiving God find themselves singing 
in the words of Psalm 100, "Make a joyful noise to  

the Lord (small caps), all the earth. Serve 
the Lord (small caps) with gladness. Come  

into his presence with singing. Enter his gates 
with thanksgiving, his courts with praise. Give  

thanks to his name. Bless His name." For He is the 
blessed God whose holiness is a happy holiness. 
 
 

To know and adore Him is to enter into His 
joy, to find a source of joy in our lives  

that is not dependent on our circumstances, 
to find a source of joy that can be the same,  

yesterday, today and forever. In Him, we 
see what Zephaniah describes, that "the  

Lord (small caps) your God is in your midst, a 
mighty one who will save," Zephaniah says. 
 
 

Can you believe this? "God will rejoice over you 
with singing. He will quiet you by His love. He  

will exult over you with loud singing." Oh, 
and that causes us to exult over Him with  

loud singing in return. Friends. if you would 
grow in life, if you would grow as a Christian,  

if you would grow in heavenliness, Paul says, 
"Be transformed by the renewing of your mind."  

For it is the knowledge of God, the light of His 
glory, that gives life, that transforms. Those  

who know God little, will love him little. 
They will have little joy and much doubt. 
 
 

So, press in like Isaiah, to know Him more, to 
see God more clearly, to know Him as He is. For  

that sight, it will humble you, it will console 
you, it will thrill you, it will enliven you.  

And then, like Isaiah, you will happily say, 
"Here I am, Lord. Send me." As well as that,  

the truth is, we always become like what we 
worship. We carry with us an aroma of what we  

glory in. We always try to hide it, but what you 
love, and cherish, and treasure will show itself.  

People will smell it on you. If you worship this 
holy one, you will start to become holy as He is  

holy: cleaner, kinder, happier, more loving. 
You will become radiant with His Holiness,  

and so your life will grow ever more 
heavenly, "until that day when He appears,  

and we shall be entirely like Him, for we 
shall see Him as He is." Let's pray. 
 
 

"Oh, worship the Lord in the splendor of his 
holiness, tremble before him all the earth.  

Who will not fear, O God, and glorify your 
name? For you alone are holy. All nations  

will come and worship you for your righteous 
acts have been revealed." Amen and amen.
