# Noahic Covenant: The Promise Keeper - God of the Covenants with R.C. Sproul

SPROUL: We've already seen the distinction between
the covenant of works and the covenant of

grace. And we have already said that every
covenant that God makes with people after

the Fall is a covenant that would be incorporated
under the general heading "the covenant of

grace." Because, for God to even provide a
way of salvation after the original covenant

is violated, gives testimony to the marvelous
grace of His very being. So, the first covenant

that we meet after the fall of Adam and Eve
is called the Noahic covenant -- that is,

it's the covenant that God made with Noah.
And there's some, I think, very important

dimensions to the Noahic covenant, and for
us to grasp them we have to try to fill in

the blanks between the time of the fall of
Adam and Eve and the appearance of Noah on

the historical scene.

Immediately after the fall of Adam and Eve
we see first of all what I call the radical

expansion of evil. And this is manifested
initially in the unspeakable horror of fratricide

that is recorded for us in the Bible when
Cain, out of jealousy, murders his brother

Abel. And how the ground was filled with the
blood of Abel, and that blood as it were,

called out for vengeance before God. And so
we read of God's curse upon Cain. But there

is a third son of Adam and Eve, whose name
is Seth. And so we get following the murder

of Abel by Cain and the birth of Seth an extended
genealogy, both of Seth and of Cain.

And then, we read 
in chapter six of Genesis, verse one, these
words, "Now, it came to pass when men began

to multiply on the face of the earth, and
daughters were born to them, that the sons

of God saw the daughters of men that they
were beautiful, and they took wives for themselves

of all whom they chose." And this is another
one of those foreboding introductory statements,

because what immediately follows after this
apparently incidental report of the intermarriage

between the sons of God and the daughters
of men, that we see the full impact of the

radical expansion of evil to such a degree
that God says at this point: "My Spirit shall

not strive with man forever. For he is indeed
flesh; yet his days shall be one-hundred and

twenty years. And there were giants in the
earth in those days and also afterwards when

the sons of men came into the daughters of
men and they bore children to them. These

were the mighty men of old, men of renown.
Then the Lord saw that the wickedness of man

was great in the earth and that every intent
of the thoughts of his heart was only evil

continually. And the Lord was sorry that He
made man in the earth and was grieved in His

heart. And so He said, 'I will destroy man
whom I've created from the face of the earth;

both man and beast, creeping things and birds
of the air, for I am sorry that I made them.

But Noah found grace in the eyes of the Lord."

Now again, we have to ask the question: "What's
going on here?" This incident that is recorded

in Genesis of the expansion of evil is so
bad that God says now, "My spirit will not

strive with men forever." And the concept
there of striving has to do with the Spirit's

exercising restraint. One of the things that
we have to understand, as much sin as there

is in the world, as much sin as there is in
our lives, were it not for the added grace

of the restraining power of God, we would
all be much more wicked than we actually are.

We distinguish in Reformed theology between
total depravity and utter depravity. We say

that fallen man is totally depraved insofar
as depravity penetrates the whole of our humanity

-- our minds, our wills, our hearts, our bodies.
But, that doesn't mean that we're as bad as

we possibly can be. And the reason why we're
not as bad as we possibly can be is because

restraints have been put on us by God.

One of the interesting exercises that I'll
often do with seminary students is ask them

to list the people that they believe were
the most wicked people that ever lived. And

inevitably, the names that will come out are
names like Nero, and Hitler, and Stalin and

people of that ilk. And I'll say that you
notice that these people all have something

in common. That is, that they were dictators
who had unlimited national power. There were

no restraints upon them within their own country.
The only restraints that were put on Hitler

or upon Stalin were the restraints put upon
them from other countries. But in their own

sphere of authority, they were unrestrained.
And that's where we get the idea that power

corrupts and absolute power corrupts absolutely,
except when it applies to God, of course.

But the idea being is that when you take off
the restraints, remove the checks and the

balances, take the leash off the human heart
an unspeakable sin begins to reign.

And that's what happened. God says, "I've
had enough of this. I'm going to remove the

restraints." And at that point, the desires
of the human heart were only wicked continually.

And it's at that stage that God decides to
destroy the creation with the flood.

Now again, we have this incidental report
of the intermarriage between the sons of God

and the daughters of men. What a lot of people
think is that this involved was a kind of

celestial rape of the human women by fallen
angels because there are occasions in the

Bible where angels are described as the "sons
of God." And so the idea is that the angels

come down and intermarry with women and produce
this bizarre race of radically evil people.

I think, frankly that that completely is out
of place in biblical literature. I don't think

that's what the Bible means at all. I think
the clue to understand what's going on here

is the amount of time that's given in the
early chapters of Genesis to give us the genealogies

of Cain and the genealogies of Seth. You see
these two lines coming down from Adam and

Eve -- the line of Cain and the line of Seth.
And if you look at these genealogies carefully,

you'll see that the line of Cain is the begetting
of one criminal after another. The line of

Cain reads like a rogue's gallery, where the
sin of the father, Cain is revisited and recapitulated

in the lives of his descendants. And yet,
when you look at the line next to it, the

line of Seth, you see one godly person after
another, culminating in Enoch "who walked

with God and was not" (presumably because
he was translated directly into heaven). And

so these two lines are clearly distinguished
in the book of Genesis. And those who are

obedient to God, can, as the Scriptures use
the term, be called the "sons of God." I think

what you have here in the text is the intermarriage
between the descendants of Seth who were obedient

and the "sons of God," and they took wives
from the line of Cain, and these two lines

now became merged and blended. And the sin
that was so characteristic of the line of

Cain now engulfed the descendents of Seth
and the whole world now is plunged into this

situation of radical evil.

But we are told, and this is important for
our understanding of this covenant, Noah "found

grace in the eyes of the Lord." And it is
the grace of God, then that defines God's

relationship with Noah. And we read in verse
nine of chapter six, "This is the genealogy

of Noah: Noah was a just man, perfect in his
generation." Now we have to be careful; that

doesn't mean that he was without sin. The
word "perfect" means complete. That as people

went in this world, Noah was in a class by
himself. He stood out from the rest of the

ruined humanity. And "Noah walked with God,"
which is the highest honor that you can give

to a human being at this point in redemptive
history.

Then we're told that "Noah begat three sons:
Shem, Ham and Japheth. And the earth also

was corrupt before God and the earth was filled
with violence. And so God looked upon the

earth and indeed it was corrupt and all flesh
had corrupted their way on the earth." And

so it's at this point that God announces to
His servant Noah that "the end of all things

has come before me, for the earth is filled
with violence, and I'm going to destroy them

with the earth." And so He gives the instructions
for the building of the ark and those whom

God commands him to bring into the ark. So
in chapter seven, verse seven, "Noah with

his sons, his wife, and his sons wives went
into the ark because of the waters of the

earth. And the day that they entered, the
Lord shut them in. And the waters came upon

the earth for forty days." And we all know
the story of the ark and the deliverance of

Noah and his family from the ark.

And then in chapter eight, verse twenty, after
the floodwaters have receded and Noah and

his family have survived the deluge, God makes
his covenant with Noah beginning in chapter

eight, verse twenty. Here's what it says:
"Then Noah built an altar to the Lord and

took every clean animal of every clean bird
and offered burned offerings on the altar.

And the Lord smelled a soothing aroma. Then
the Lord said in His heart, 'I will never

again curse the ground for man's sake, although
the imaginations of man's heart is evil from

his youth. Nor will I again destroy every
living thing as I have done.'" Now do you

see that the terms of this covenant that God
makes with Noah involves promises. For God

says, "I'm making a promise. I'm not going
to destroy the earth with a curse; I'm not

going to destroy every living thing as I have
done. While the earth remains, seedtime and

harvest, cold and heat, winter and summer,
day and night shall not cease." That is, the

pattern, the rhythm, the cycle of nature will
continue until the world is redeemed.

Now, this is important because in the concept
of redemption in Scripture, in the covenant

of grace, according to the New Testament,
what we learn for example from Paul in his

letter to the Romans, that the beneficiaries
of God's covenant of grace are not only human

being, but the plant kingdom, the animal kingdom,
the whole earth because the whole of the earth

is plunged into the curse because of the fall
of the ones whom God has set as His vice-regents

over the earth, He gave dominion over the
earth, over the animals, over the fish, over

the ground and all that to Adam and Eve. When
they fell, the curse that beset man, also

fell upon the whole earth. And so when we
are redeemed, with our redemption, comes the

redemption of the entire earth. That's why
we say the Christ is the cosmic Christ. He

is the one who brings cosmic redemption. Paul
says, "The whole creation groans together

in travail, waiting for the manifestation
of the sons of God." That is, waiting for

our redemption. Now, so we see in the promise
to Noah, the promise of the ongoing cycle

of nature until the final restoration of all
things.

"And so God blessed Noah and his sons and
said to them, 'Be fruitful and multiply and

fill the earth. And the fear of you and the
dread of you shall be on every beast of the

earth, and every bird of the air and all that
move on earth, and all the fish of the sea.

They are given into your hand. Every moving
thing that lives shall be food for you.'"

(You know, let those who say that the Christian
has to be a vegetarian read these texts.)

"I've given you all things even as the green
herbs. But you shall not eat flesh with its

life, (that is with its blood) for surely
for your lifeblood I will require a reckoning.

From the hand of every beast I will require
it and from the hand of man. From the hand

of man's brother I will require the life of
man."

Now, again, we see in the first instance,
that the Noahic covenant, in many regards,

is a reconstitution of the Covenant of Creation.
It's not a covenant that God makes with a

certain ethnic group like with Abram and his
sons or with Moses. But rather, it's again

now in a sense, Noah is functioning in a similar
capacity to what Adam had before the fall.

Because God now has wiped out all of the wicked
descendants of Adam and as it were, is beginning

again. He is reordering and restructuring
the covenant of creation. And that's why we

see with the promises of this covenant also
come obligations that are very similar to

the obligations that God had given to Adam
and Eve, when He told them, "Be fruitful and

multiply and fill the earth." Now that command
is reiterated to Noah.

And now we read in verse six this mandate,
which is one of the most misunderstood and

controversial texts from all of the book of
Genesis: "Whoever sheds man's blood, by man

his blood shall be shed. For in the image
of God, He made man. As for you, be fruitful

and multiply. Bring forth abundantly in the
earth and multiply in it." Now, there are

many people who come to this text in Genesis
and read it merely as a prophecy that means

something like this: "Those who live by the
sword shall die by the sword." That's not

what it is, but rather, it is the covenantal
institution of capital punishment.

Indeed, it's important to understand that
this principle here is not something given

to Moses for the economy of the Mosaic covenant
or to Abraham, but since this is the reconstitution

of the creation covenant, this principle or
this law given to Noah affects all of mankind.

Every person in every generation in every
culture is to be under this principle of capital

punishment. And again, not only does God give
the command, when he says, "Whoever sheds

man's blood, by man shall his blood be shed."
This is in the imperative; this is a command:

this person must be killed. And so God doesn't
just give the magistrate now the right of

capital punishment; He gives the magistrate
the duty of capital punishment, and that's

what causes so much controversy.

Now I remember reading an article from Larry
King in which he was complaining about Christians

constantly protesting against abortion laws
in the United States. And he said he would

not take the Christian community's protest
against abortion seriously until these same

people would mobilize against capital punishment.
And when he said that, he revealed that he

didn't understand the biblical rationale,
which is the same in both cases. What is behind

the church's protest against abortion and
what is behind the historic Christian community's

backing of capital punishment is the concept
of the sanctity of life. And people who oppose

capital punishment, oppose it appealing to
the same principle. The principle is that

every human life is sacred, and if a person
chooses to murder another human being, we're

just added to the desacralization of life
by executing the murderer. Then the point

is that everybody's life is sacred, even the
murderer, and so we have to do something besides

retaliate with revenge or so on. But the point
of the original establishment of capital punishment

is not for deterrence; it's not even for vengeance.
It's to maintain God's directive that every

human life is sacred.

Now notice what He says, "Whoever sheds man's
blood, by man his blood shall be shed. For

in the image of God, He made man." The rationale
that God gives for requiring the death of

the murderer is that in a very real sense,
the person who raises his hand to slay a human

being is making an assault not just on a fellow
human, but is making an assault against God

because every human being is the image bearer
of God. And when you kill a human being, you

kill somebody who is bearing the image of
God. And God is saying that to be in the image

of God is so sacred and so holy, that if somebody
wantonly destroys an image bearer of God,

God says that person forfeits their right
to human life and they are to be executed.

So the punishment for murder, God establishes,
not just in the Mosaic economy, but in the

Noahic economy, is the death penalty.

All right, now, "Then God spoke to Noah and
his sons saying, 'As for Me, behold I establish

my covenant with you and your descendants
after you, and with every living creature

that is with you, the birds, the cattle and
the beasts of the earth, of all of you that

go out of the ark -- every beast of the earth.
Thus I establish my covenant with you. Never

again shall all flesh be cut off by the waters
of the flood. Never again shall there be a

flood to destroy the earth.' And God said,
'This is the sign of the covenant that I make

between Me and you and with every living creature
with you for perpetual generations. I set

my rainbow in the cloud and it shall be for
the sign of the covenant between Me and the

earth. It shall be when I bring a cloud over
the earth, that the rainbow shall be seen

in the cloud. And I will remember my covenant
which is between Me and you and every living

creature. The rainbow shall be in the cloud,
and I will look on it to remember the everlasting

covenant between God and every living creature.'"

Again, the terms of this promise underscore
that this is not a local covenant or simply

a particular ethnic covenant, but it is with
the whole world; that it is again the reestablishing

of the Covenant of Creation and it is to be
perpetual. God says, "Every time it rains

and you see the rainbow in the sky, that is
my sign in the sky. That marks the sacred

promise that I am swearing to you that I will
keep this promise unto all generations." And

it is God's promise to preserve His creation;
which creation is the context for His plan

of redemption.
